# Notes — The macro-financial loop: AI capex + industrial-policy spending → rates, debt, currency, inflation, and the monetary-statecraft endgame

## Theories / lenses (32)

- **Productivity-offset hypothesis: AI supply-side gains absorb fiscal excess** — If AI delivers broad total-factor-productivity gains (as electrification did post-1920), the supply curve shifts out fast enough to neutralize demand-pull inflation from capex and industrial-policy spending — the 'free lunch' scenario where deficits are self-liquidating via growth. This is the bull case for rates staying manageable.  _(#1, r1)_
- **Fiscal dominance endgame: debt service crowds out central bank independence** — When interest expense consumes a large enough share of primary revenue (US approaching ~20% now), political pressure to cap long yields becomes irresistible. Central banks de facto monetize by yield-curve control or large-scale asset purchases, repeating the 1940s financial-repression playbook. Inflation becomes the debt-reduction mechanism.  _(#2, r1)_
- **r* elevation thesis: capex supercycle structurally raises the neutral rate** — Simultaneous AI private capex ($500B+ annual run rate) and government industrial-policy investment (IRA, CHIPS Act, EU Green Deal, China semiconductor fund) compete for global loanable funds. Even if central banks do nothing, equilibrium real rates rise — undoing the 2010s secular-stagnation regime and making existing debt stocks more burdensome.  _(#3, r1)_
- **Dollar milkshake effect: US AI leadership could reinforce dollar dominance** — Brent Johnson's 'dollar milkshake' thesis applies here: US industrial policy + AI technological leadership attracts global capital inflows, raising demand for dollar-denominated assets even as deficits widen. The dollar strengthens because the rest of the world needs to earn dollars to buy US technology and energy — partially offsetting de-dollarization pressure.  _(#6, r1)_
- **Geopolitical fragmentation premium: reshoring is a permanent structural inflation tax** — Industrial policy (CHIPS Act, friend-shoring, EU strategic autonomy) mandates redundant, geographically sub-optimal supply chains. Unlike cyclical inflation, this is a one-way ratchet — political economy prevents re-offshoring once domestic capacity is built. It embeds a structurally higher cost floor in manufacturing, distinct from AI-driven deflation, creating a persistent wedge in core goods inflation.  _(#10, r1)_
- **Capex collision: AI and energy-transition investment compete for the same physical inputs** — AI data centers and grid decarbonization both require transformers, copper, specialized construction labor, and grid interconnection — at the same time. This is not a single-sector bottleneck (already noted) but a structural collision between two government-accelerated supercycles competing over the same capital-goods supply chain, compounding inflationary pressure beyond what either alone would produce.  _(#13, r2)_
- **Private credit intermediation blunts rate transmission to AI capex** — A growing share of AI and industrial-policy capex is intermediated through private credit markets (BDCs, direct lending, infrastructure debt funds) rather than public bond markets. These instruments have floating rates but are negotiated bilaterally with much lower price sensitivity to policy rates and less liquidity risk. Central bank rate hikes may fail to cool AI-related investment as effectively as they cool rate-sensitive sectors, weakening the conventional transmission mechanism.  _(#19, r2)_
- **Sovereign AI funds and state-backed capital act as price-insensitive buyers, extending capex beyond commercial returns** — Gulf SWFs, Singapore's Temasek/GIC, the Japanese government pension fund, and various national AI investment vehicles are making direct equity and infrastructure commitments in AI that are not subject to normal WACC hurdles. These investors are buying strategic optionality, not commercial IRR. This creates a capex cycle that may not self-correct via normal market signals — directly bearing on the 'self-liquidating vs. stranded-asset' crux.  _(#21, r2)_
- **Updated Triffin dilemma: US must run deficits to supply global dollar liquidity, but those deficits erode the reserve-currency confidence they depend on** — The classic Triffin dilemma states that the reserve-currency issuer must run current account deficits to provide the world with liquidity, but persistent deficits undermine confidence in the currency. AI industrial policy amplifies this: fiscal deficits supply dollar liquidity globally (supporting dollar dominance) while simultaneously raising debt/GDP and default-risk perception. The dollar milkshake and reserve-diversification forces are not independent — they are two sides of the same structural contradiction.  _(#22, r2)_
- **Global savings glut reversal: AI capex absorbs excess savings, flipping the secular-stagnation regime** — The low-rate era (2010–2021) was partly explained by a global savings glut — excess savings from aging Asia and petrostates with insufficient investment demand to absorb them, pushing r* down. If AI capex and industrial policy create a sustained investment supercycle that absorbs these savings globally, the secular-stagnation equilibrium inverts: a savings scarcity rather than savings surplus, with structurally higher real rates worldwide. This is a supply-of-capital mechanism distinct from the r* elevation thesis, which focuses on the demand for capital.  _(#23, r2)_
- **AI monopoly rent concentrates in equity without CPI diffusion, mis-calibrating inflation-targeting mandates** — If AI productivity accrues to capital owners as monopoly rents — as platform consolidation did in the 2010s — consumer prices stay contained even as corporate profits and asset values inflate sharply. Central banks targeting CPI headline would see no mandate to tighten, allowing continued fiscal expansion; the inflation is real but measured in the wrong index. A sudden repricing of risk assets could then reveal suppressed monetary excess, creating an abrupt non-linear adjustment.  _(#31, r3)_
- **US–China AI race creates a fiscal prisoner's dilemma — both blocs structurally over-invest relative to standalone equilibrium** — Each bloc's industrial-policy and AI capex spending is partly a strategic response to the other's. Neither can unilaterally stand down without ceding strategic ground, so the coordination failure means global aggregate capex, debt issuance, and r* pressures are permanently higher than any single-bloc model predicts. The r* elevation thesis understates the effect if it ignores the second bloc's symmetric contribution.  _(#35, r4)_
- **RMB internationalization is China's active counter-Triffin play: export the dilemma to the dollar rather than absorb it** — By building bilateral RMB trade-settlement infrastructure (CIPS, mBridge, commodity contracts) especially with resource exporters, China attempts to accumulate current-account surpluses while allowing selective RMB accumulation abroad — freeing itself from the 'supply liquidity or preserve confidence' bind. Even partial success reduces structural demand for USTs while expanding China's own fiscal space, compounding the Triffin pressure on the US side.  _(#38, r4)_
- **The same global cycle produces structurally divergent outcomes: inflationary West, deflationary East-Asian manufacturing core** — Western AI/reshoring/energy-transition capex raises domestic services, energy, and labor costs. China and East Asian exporters face domestic overcapacity deflation while flooding global goods markets. This forces divergent monetary paths (Fed/ECB tighter-for-longer vs. PBoC/BoJ structurally looser), generating persistent exchange-rate pressure, carry-trade instability, and capital-flow volatility — a self-reinforcing divergence rather than a global convergence toward one inflation regime.  _(#41, r4)_
- **US stablecoin legislation conscripts private crypto infrastructure into dollar monetary statecraft** — Legislation such as the GENIUS Act conditions stablecoin issuance on USD denomination and Treasury-reserve backing. This is a deliberate policy to extend dollar settlement rails globally through private crypto networks — without SWIFT, correspondent banking, or reserve-currency diplomacy. It turns stablecoin issuers into de facto agents of dollar hegemony, expanding the dollar's monetary footprint even as physical reserve diversification continues.  _(#48, r5)_
- **Gold remonetization as neutral Schelling-point reserve asset under bloc fragmentation** — Central bank gold purchases ran at 50-year highs in 2022–2024; Basel III's reclassification of gold as a zero-risk-weight Tier 1 asset is an active regulatory remonetization signal. In a fragmented world where USTs are weaponized and Chinese bonds face capital-control risk, gold becomes the only asset neither bloc can sanction — a default neutral reserve. This is a third monetary endgame (neither dollar nor RMB dominance) not represented in any existing note.  _(#51, r5)_
- **CBDC programmability transforms fiscal dominance from political accident into engineered design** — Programmable CBDCs — with negative rates on idle balances, expiry-dated stimulus payments, or sector-restricted spending — give a government direct monetary transmission that bypasses commercial banks entirely. Unlike traditional fiscal dominance (where a distressed sovereign coerces a central bank), CBDC fiscal dominance can be architecturally embedded: the system itself enforces helicopter money, consumption targets, or sectoral subsidies. This makes the 'can central banks maintain political independence' crux potentially obsolete in CBDC regimes.  _(#53, r5)_
- **Demographics — not AI capex — is the primary mechanism driving savings-glut reversal** — The existing savings-glut reversal note attributes the flip to AI capex absorbing excess savings. But the more fundamental driver is aging cohorts globally shifting from net savers to net dissavers: German Boomers drawing down Riester accounts, Japanese households liquidating JGBs, Korean retirees spending pension assets. This demographic dissaving would structurally elevate r* even in a zero-AI-capex counterfactual, and its interaction with AI spending produces a double-barreled r* shock whose magnitude and irreversibility the AI-centric framing systematically understates.  _(#57, r6)_
- **Japan’s full 30-year demographic-macro arc — not just YCC — is the relevant template for China and Europe** — The existing Japan note covers only the YCC monetary-policy episode and its 2024 unwind. The broader arc is more instructive: demographic aging drove disinflation → fiscal expansion to prop demand → debt accumulation → financial repression as the only exit → YCC as the operational expression. China is approximately 10–15 years behind Japan on this arc; Germany and Italy are at intermediate stages. Projecting the full arc implies China enters a debt-deflation trap in the mid-2030s requiring its own YCC analog — with direct implications for global capital flows, CNY dynamics, and the RMB internationalization thesis.  _(#64, r6)_
- **AI equity boom generates pro-cyclical capital-gains/corporate-tax windfalls that mask the true structural deficit** — US federal capital-gains revenue roughly doubles as a share of receipts in sustained equity bull markets; AI-concentration magnifies this. Debt/GDP sustainability arithmetic looks sound only contingent on elevated valuations persisting — the structural deficit (stripping cyclical windfall revenue) is substantially larger than headline figures imply. This is a fiscal-illusion channel wholly distinct from the bond-demand and inflation channels already mapped.  _(#67, r7)_
- **Reflexive equity-fiscal loop: AI multiples are partially justified by benign fiscal/monetary conditions that are themselves sustained by AI equity gains** — High AI equity multiples rest on an assumption of stable rates and low term premia; those conditions are partly sustained by the capital-gains windfalls those same multiples generate for government revenue. This creates a reflexive loop — as in Soros's reflexivity applied to sovereign finance — that amplifies both the boom phase and makes the bust non-linear. The loop is absent from the existing notes, which treat equity and fiscal channels as one-way.  _(#69, r7)_
- **The Fed's Standing Repo Facility is a permanent liquidity put that pre-commits the Fed to backstop Treasury markets, making operational fiscal dominance a fait accompli** — By guaranteeing overnight repo against Treasuries at a fixed rate, the SRF structurally prevents Treasury market dysfunction without formal QE. The implication: fiscal dominance no longer requires the Fed to choose monetization — the commitment is already embedded in the plumbing. The question shifts from 'will the Fed monetize?' to 'the Fed's optionality to refuse is already gone.' This is a pre-political capture mechanism absent from the existing fiscal-dominance note.  _(#75, r7)_
- **GSIB surcharge and bank capital procyclicality create a designed-in Treasury-market withdrawal exactly when stabilization is needed most** — Global systemically important banks face higher GSIB surcharges if they expand their balance sheets in stressed conditions. This means the institutions with the deepest pockets to absorb Treasury supply are subject to regulatory charges that discourage buying during market stress — the opposite of a stabilizing function. Combined with SLR constraints, this creates a regulatory architecture that amplifies Treasury market dislocations rather than dampening them, a procyclical feature baked into post-GFC reform.  _(#77, r7)_
- **Rearmament is a co-equal fiscal supercycle with no productivity-offset hypothesis** — NATO members moving toward 3%+ GDP on defense, Japan doubling its budget, and ASEAN rearmament constitute a second independent structural fiscal impulse running in parallel with AI capex — but unlike AI capex, defense procurement (platforms, munitions stockpiles, force structure) carries no credible supply-side productivity offset to absorb the fiscal excess. The combined twin-supercycle (AI + rearmament) is more unambiguously inflationary than either alone, yet virtually all macro-financial models treat them as separate or subordinate.  _(#79, r8)_
- **Defense-AI overlap creates a price-insensitive, off-balance-sheet government AI demand floor** — Autonomous weapons, JADC2 (joint all-domain command and control), signals intelligence, and logistics optimization require the same GPU/chip infrastructure as commercial AI. Classified defense AI budgets are strategically-mandated, price-insensitive, and largely invisible in commercial capex data. This is distinct from the 'sovereign AI funds act as price-insensitive buyers' note, which covers civilian state-backed capital; defense demand is operationally non-discretionary, funded from defense appropriations, and could sustain the AI capex cycle through a commercial bust in a way that sovereign wealth funds (which face return mandates) cannot.  _(#84, r8)_
- **Energy abundance as hyperinflation escape valve — distinct from productivity-offset** — The productivity-offset operates through AI raising output per worker. Cheap energy operates through a different channel: collapsing the marginal cost of goods and industrial production broadly, breaking the physical supply constraint that historically converts fiscal excess into hyperinflation (as in Weimar, where energy/food bottlenecks were the transmission mechanism). This could enable Japan-style managed fiscal dominance without the hyperinflationary tail — a structurally different resolution.  _(#92, r9)_
- **Differential renewable endowment creates a new geopolitical axis cutting across existing blocs** — Solar irradiance, wind corridors, hydro geography, and adjacent mineral deposits create structural energy-cost advantages for regions like MENA, Australia, Saharan Africa, and parts of Central Asia that do not map cleanly onto the US/China or NATO/non-NATO fault lines. A world of cheap but geographically concentrated energy abundance fractures the existing bloc alignment, creating new monetary and trade dependencies distinct from both the dollar-bloc and RMB-bloc frameworks.  _(#100, r9)_
- **Energy-abundance growth-out-of-debt via real cost-of-capital reduction — distinct from AI labour productivity** — The productivity-offset hypothesis works through AI raising output per worker. Energy abundance works through reducing the real cost of physical production inputs, lowering the hurdle rate for capital investment broadly, and enabling sustained real GDP growth across all sectors simultaneously — potentially outrunning nominal debt growth without requiring inflation. This is the post-WWII US growth mechanism (cheap energy + demographics + pent-up demand) but supplied through technology rather than demographic tailwinds, and it is structurally different from AI labour productivity gains.  _(#102, r9)_
- **US mortgage lock-in creates a structural gap in monetary transmission through the household sector** — ~90% of US mortgages are 30-year fixed; the 2020-22 refinancing wave left ~$12T of household debt at 3-4%, far below policy rates. Unlike corporate debt, UK variable-rate mortgages, or credit cards, existing homeowners do not feel rate hikes through their primary balance-sheet liability. This means the Fed must inflict proportionally more damage via job losses and non-mortgage credit to achieve equivalent demand destruction — fundamentally changing the distributional incidence of tightening in ways absent from sovereign/corporate/bank-plumbing analysis.  _(#103, r10)_
- **Democratic polities exhibit a revealed historical preference for inflate-away over explicit austerity — this is the political SELECTION mechanism for the endgame, not merely a risk** — Cross-country evidence from interwar Europe, post-WWII repression, and EM fiscal crises consistently shows that democratic governments choose inflation because it is a diffuse, hard-to-attribute tax, while austerity produces visible, directly traceable cuts with identifiable losers who organize politically. The existing notes describe the endgame paths as financially determined; this lens treats path selection as a political equilibrium problem with a systematic directional bias toward monetary solutions.  _(#106, r10)_
- **Wealth taxation / AI-rent redistribution is a distinct fourth resolution path that changes the macro forecast radically if achieved** — Alongside financial repression, inflate-away, and austerity, a targeted tax on AI monopoly rents (mark-to-market capital gains, excess-profit levies on hyperscalers, corporate minimum taxes) could in principle close fiscal gaps without monetary dilution. The economic question is whether the tax base can be captured before capital flight; the political question is whether the wealth-tax coalition can overcome capital mobility and lobbying concentration. This path produces a fundamentally different macro outcome (non-inflationary consolidation) and is almost entirely absent from the existing analysis.  _(#108, r10)_
- **Sequential legitimacy exhaustion: each attempted resolution path destroys political capital for the next — the ORDER in which paths are tried determines which becomes unavoidable** — A government that attempts austerity first burns the center-left coalition; attempting inflation next burns the savings constituency; financial repression burns pensioners. Once two paths have been exhausted politically, the third is forced regardless of economic optimality. This sequential-legitimacy lens is missing from the note set and has high predictive value for endgame timing: a polity that tried austerity in 2010-15 (Eurozone) arrives at the inflation/repression junction with far less political runway than one that did not.  _(#110, r10)_

## Hypotheses (23)

- **Sectorally concentrated inflation: power, land, and specialized labor as bottlenecks** — AI buildout creates acute supply constraints in specific inputs — electrical grid capacity, data-center-suitable land near fiber/power, and a tiny pool of high-voltage electrical engineers. These bottlenecks raise costs in AI-adjacent industries without showing clearly in headline CPI, distorting capital allocation and potentially triggering energy-infrastructure inflation that spills into manufacturing.  _(#8, r1)_
- **Sovereign debt duration cliff: non-linear fiscal pressure as low-rate issuance rolls over** — Most G7 governments issued heavily at near-zero rates in 2020–21 with 5–10 year maturities. As this stock rolls into 2025–2030, interest costs jump discontinuously rather than gradually — a cliff, not a ramp. The fiscal-dominance endgame may therefore arrive with sudden political force rather than being absorbed incrementally, leaving little time for orderly central-bank independence negotiation.  _(#16, r2)_
- **LDI/NBFI duration-mismatch creates a non-linear trap constraining central bank tightening** — The 2022 UK gilt crisis showed that pension funds running liability-driven investment (LDI) strategies can force emergency central bank bond purchases mid-tightening cycle. Globally, insurers and pension funds hold trillions in long-duration bonds bought at low rates; rate rises sufficient to suppress AI-capex inflation could trigger cascading margin calls and forced selling, compelling CB backstops and effectively aborting tightening — a distinct mechanism from Japan YCC and more systemic than private credit intermediation.  _(#27, r3)_
- **AI capex bust triggers Fisher debt-deflation, inverting the dominant inflation scenario** — If AI capex generates a credit-driven asset bubble in data-center assets, GPU valuations, and AI-adjacent real estate, and that bubble subsequently bursts, Irving Fisher's debt-deflation mechanism produces self-reinforcing contraction: asset price falls → collateral shrinks → credit contracts → further asset selling. Central banks constrained by fiscal dominance and inflation credibility concerns may be unable to provide sufficient stimulus, producing a deflationary depression as the tail scenario directly inverting the fiscal-dominance narrative.  _(#34, r3)_
- **Simultaneous fiscal dominance in both anchor blocs eliminates the global 'credible hawk' and breaks the risk-free benchmark** — The existing fiscal-dominance note assumes a single dominant issuer losing credibility while others remain anchors. If China's state banks are also implicitly monetizing AI/infrastructure debt while the Fed faces fiscal-dominance pressure, the global flight-to-quality dynamic breaks down: there is no credible-hawk central bank remaining to anchor inflation expectations worldwide, and the 'risk-free rate' loses its anchor function for pricing all other assets globally.  _(#43, r4)_
- **China faces an internal devaluation dilemma: CNY weakness exports deflation but directly undermines RMB internationalization and triggers capital flight** — As AI/industrial capex raises domestic costs and export demand softens, China faces pressure to devalue the CNY. But devaluation conflicts with the RMB internationalization strategy (which requires currency credibility for foreign holders) and risks accelerating capital outflows that even capital controls cannot fully contain at scale. Resolving this internal contradiction determines whether China exports deflation or inflation to global markets — and the direction is not predetermined.  _(#44, r4)_
- **A stablecoin run is a faster, less-supervised non-linear destabilizer than the LDI/NBFI trap** — A large stablecoin redemption event — triggered by a regulatory shock, a peg break, or a credit crisis — would force near-instantaneous liquidation of hundreds of billions in short-duration Treasuries into an already-stressed market, with no lender-of-last-resort and no central-bank intervention protocol. Unlike the 2022 UK LDI crisis (which had a recognizable counterparty and a BOE backstop), stablecoin issuers operate outside the regulatory perimeter. The resulting T-bill fire sale would spike short-end yields and tighten broader financial conditions in hours, not days.  _(#52, r5)_
- **Offshore stablecoin dollar creation muddies Fed exchange-rate and M-supply signaling** — The $200B+ in stablecoins represents synthetic dollar creation outside the Federal Reserve's balance sheet — structurally analogous to Eurodollar expansion but more transparent and faster-growing. This expands the effective global dollar supply beyond what M2 or reserve statistics capture, creating a systematic downward bias in dollar-scarcity estimates. In crises, stablecoin flight-to-quality can mimic Eurodollar squeeze dynamics, generating sharp synthetic demand for underlying Treasury collateral that amplifies rather than smooths rate volatility.  _(#55, r5)_
- **China’s deflation export is a structural demographic outcome, not a reversible strategic policy choice** — Existing notes frame China’s overcapacity-driven deflation export as a strategic ‘weapon’ or deliberate policy posture. But with a fertility rate of ~1.0–1.1 and the 4-2-1 dependency inversion from the one-child policy baked in for 30+ years, China’s domestic demand is structurally contractionary relative to its installed productive capacity. The deflationary surplus is not a dial Beijing can turn down; it is a demographic arithmetic problem. This reframes the deflation-export thesis from ‘reversible if Beijing chooses’ to ‘durable for a generation regardless of policy’.  _(#58, r6)_
- **Bifurcated inflation — services sticky, goods deflationary — breaks headline CPI targeting and delegitimizes central banks** — Aging demographics structurally shift demand toward labor-intensive, AI-resistant services (elder care, home health, assisted living) whose prices are governed by local wage rates and regulatory staffing ratios, not global AI productivity. Simultaneously, AI-driven goods deflation continues. The composite CPI can remain ‘on target’ while services inflation is severe and goods are in secular deflation — a split that systematically mis-signals monetary policy and produces a lived-experience divergence that delegitimizes the inflation-targeting regime politically. This is distinct from the existing mis-calibration note (which targets AI monopoly rents, not demographic demand composition).  _(#62, r6)_
- **AI capex/equity bust produces a 'double whammy' fiscal non-linearity: revenue collapse coincides with counter-cyclical bailout spending** — A bust simultaneously destroys capital-gains/corporate-tax receipts AND triggers automatic-stabilizer outlays plus potential financial-sector bailouts (FDIC, GSE guarantees, sovereign-backed AI lenders). The fiscal deterioration is asymmetric and non-linear — unlike an inflationary fiscal-dominance scenario, the bust path is deflationary but the fiscal position may deteriorate faster, echoing 2008's revenue-plus-TARP shock.  _(#68, r7)_
- **Repo-financed cash-futures basis trade is the specific Treasury-market fault line for a non-linear stress event** — Hedge funds hold an estimated $600B–$1T+ in repo-financed long-cash/short-futures Treasury basis positions. An unwind (as in March 2020 and the April 2025 tariff shock) creates sudden forced selling of cash Treasuries into an illiquid market, requiring emergency Fed backstop. This is operationally distinct from the existing LDI/NBFI duration-mismatch note — it is about repo-leverage and short-dated liquidity, not duration — and represents the specific mechanism for a Treasury 'flash crash.'  _(#74, r7)_
- **Capital controls are the war-finance resolution mechanism that collapses open-market discipline** — Historical hot conflicts uniformly triggered capital controls to prevent capital flight and ensure domestic savings are conscripted into war bonds at below-market rates. The existing 'capital-account asymmetry bifurcates the endgame' note addresses peacetime divergence between China (closed) and US (open); it does not address the scenario where a hot conflict forces the US to impose capital controls, eliminating the open-market discipline that is the primary constraint on US fiscal dominance. Capital controls transform the 'will markets enforce fiscal discipline?' crux into a non-question.  _(#81, r8)_
- **War economy resource commandeering disrupts AI capex via administrative direction, not market crowding-out** — Hot conflict historically triggers government direction of scarce industrial resources — semiconductors, specialized metals, energy, freight capacity — away from civilian/commercial use via Defense Production Act-style mechanisms, export controls, and rationing. This is categorically distinct from the 'capex collision' note (competitive market crowding-out for the same inputs) and the TSMC crux (supply-chain disruption): administrative commandeering can halt commercial AI buildout regardless of price signals or financing conditions, and it is not reversible through monetary policy. It also creates a peacetime precedent for state direction of the tech sector that outlasts the conflict.  _(#88, r8)_
- **Geopolitical tension insurance premium is a structural, continuous inflation floor even absent hot conflict** — Elevated but sub-conflict geopolitical tension forces firms to permanently increase inventory buffers (against supply disruption risk), build redundant supplier relationships (higher procurement costs), pay war-risk premiums on shipping and insurance, and hold excess working capital. Unlike the 'geopolitical fragmentation premium' note (which covers the one-time capital cost of reshoring), this mechanism operates as an ongoing operational cost that compounds continuously and is impervious to monetary tightening. It functions as a tax on global trade that raises the effective neutral rate of inflation even in peacetime — a floor below which goods disinflation cannot reach.  _(#89, r8)_
- **Rapid clean-energy deployment triggers fossil-fuel stranded-asset credit event** — If AI-accelerated energy abundance materialises over 10–15 years, it could force a $5–10T write-down of upstream fossil fuel reserves, midstream infrastructure, and petro-state sovereign balance sheets. Unlike the AI capex bust scenario (which runs through tech equity and fiscal revenue), this credit event runs through energy finance and commodity-currency sovereign debt — a distinct and potentially concurrent non-linearity.  _(#95, r9)_
- **Nuclear J-curve: SMR/nuclear buildout is inflationary before it is deflationary** — SMRs require 10–15 years of capital-intensive construction, specialised labour, and regulatory approvals before delivering cheap electrons. The path to nuclear-driven energy abundance therefore adds to near-term capex collision and skilled-labour inflation exactly when the fiscal pressure is worst, only delivering the deflationary terminal state outside the relevant policy window — a J-curve in the energy inflation dynamic.  _(#97, r9)_
- **Carbon pricing political-economy collapse removes a fiscal pillar just as it is needed most** — If AI-accelerated clean energy makes decarbonisation achievable cheaply, the political economy supporting carbon taxes and green industrial subsidies collapses — because the market already delivers the outcome. This removes carbon pricing revenue (currently €50–150/tonne across EU ETS, UK ETS, Canada) and eliminates the industrial-policy spending rationale simultaneously, shrinking the fiscal toolkit at the exact moment debt-service pressures are highest. It is a second-order fiscal headwind from energy abundance.  _(#101, r9)_
- **Mortgage lock-in suppresses labor mobility to AI and industrial-policy growth centers, amplifying bottleneck wage inflation exactly where policy targets investment** — A worker locked into a 3% mortgage faces a 200–300bp penalty to refinance if relocating to an Arizona semiconductor fab or Texas data-center hub. This reduces the labor supply elasticity for precisely the sectors receiving the largest capex subsidies, generating persistent wage-cost inflation in growth zones while creating frozen demand in departure regions. It is a distinct supply-side mechanism for 'sectorally concentrated inflation' (specialized labor bottleneck) beyond land and power.  _(#105, r10)_
- **AI-accelerated tax avoidance is a feedback loop that erodes the wealth-tax resolution before it can be implemented** — Just as governments move to tax AI-generated wealth, AI tools dramatically improve corporate and personal tax planning, profit shifting, and structuring — potentially expanding the fiscal gap at the same pace as rents concentrate. This creates a race between public fiscal capacity and private avoidance capability that systematically favors the private sector, making 'tax the AI rents' a moving target and pushing the resolution menu back toward monetary options. It is a novel AI-specific amplifier of the existing tax-base-erosion problem.  _(#109, r10)_
- **AI displacement velocity creates a positive political-fiscal feedback loop: faster displacement → larger displaced constituency → larger redistribution deficits → faster fiscal dominance** — The existing disconfirming note treats AI labor displacement as a demand-destruction effect. The missing step is the political intermediation: displaced workers vote for fiscal transfers (UBI, expanded unemployment, retraining programs), turning displacement velocity into a fiscal forcing function. If displacement runs ahead of new job creation, the politically mandatory fiscal response adds to structural deficits before the productivity offset arrives — inverting the sequence and accelerating the fiscal dominance trajectory. The variable is displacement SPEED relative to political tolerance, not the eventual equilibrium.  _(#111, r10)_
- **Industrial policy generates permanent organized constituencies for continued subsidy, making the fiscal supercycle self-reinforcing through political economy rather than economic returns** — Unlike private capex cycles that end when returns disappoint, industrial policy programs create unions, regional development agencies, and elected representatives in recipient districts who actively lobby to maintain and expand programs independent of economic outcomes. The inflationary fiscal spending thus acquires a political perpetuation mechanism — once a fab is built in a congressional district, withdrawing its subsidy is electorally toxic. This 'constituency lock-in' is a missing political-economy amplifier of the fiscal supercycle's duration.  _(#112, r10)_
- **Rising renter share is a dynamic tipping point: as housing costs convert homeowners to renters — especially among younger cohorts — the political-economy incentive to protect housing equity gradually inverts** — In jurisdictions where renters form a political majority (major coastal metros), rent control, supply reform, and deliberate housing repricing are already electorally viable. If the affordability crisis extends nationally over a 10-20 year horizon, the implicit 'third mandate' protecting homeowner balance sheets weakens and may flip — freeing monetary policy to normalize more aggressively or enabling forced housing repricing via taxation. This is a time-varying crux that changes the monetary constraint over the forecast horizon, absent from the static political-economy notes.  _(#113, r10)_

## Supporting evidence (12)

- **WWII financial repression as template: precedent for managed rates under high debt** — From 1942–1951, the US Fed pegged Treasury yields (2.5% cap on long bonds) while inflation ran above 5%, effectively transferring wealth from creditors to the sovereign. Real debt/GDP fell ~40pp in a decade. This precedent suggests fiscal dominance is achievable and historically survivable — but required explicit central bank subordination and negative real rates for savers.  _(#9, r1)_
- **Critical-mineral supply lags impose a structural commodity inflation floor lasting a decade** — New copper, lithium, cobalt, and rare-earth mine development takes 10–15 years from discovery to production. AI data centers and energy-transition hardware require unprecedented quantities of these inputs. Even if AI productivity eventually materializes, the physical supply chain for the buildout imposes a cost floor that neither monetary tightening nor software efficiency can resolve — and unlike semiconductor fabs, mines cannot be permitted or built on political timelines.  _(#30, r3)_
- **Geopolitical tech-stack duplication forces redundant global capex, raising world r* beyond any single-bloc projection** — Export controls compel China to build parallel semiconductor fabs, AI training infrastructure, and software ecosystems that replicate at higher cost what the US/West has already built. Global investment-to-output ratios rise without proportional productivity gains, since each stack must be separately funded by global savings. This amplifies the r* elevation beyond what the capex supercycle thesis calculates from one bloc alone.  _(#39, r4)_
- **State-directed strategic commodity stockpiling creates a politically-driven demand floor impervious to monetary tightening** — Both the US/West (critical minerals, rare earths, lithium) and China (grain, energy, metals) are accumulating strategic reserves well beyond economic demand, as geopolitical insurance against supply cutoffs. Unlike supply-lag-driven commodity inflation, this political demand floor persists through recessions and rate hikes — it is price-inelastic by design. Monetary policy cannot extinguish it without causing a recession deep enough to destroy the political mandate for the stockpiling itself.  _(#40, r4)_
- **Globally synchronized industrial policy creates simultaneous demand shocks for the same capital goods, with no off-peak capacity to buffer** — US IRA/CHIPS Act, EU Green Deal/Net-Zero Industrial Act, and China's AI/semiconductor Five-Year Plans are all bidding for the same capital goods (advanced fabs, grid infrastructure, battery manufacturing) at the same time. Prior industrial policy waves were sequential and regional, so excess capacity from one wave absorbed the next. The current synchronization eliminates that buffer, turning sectoral bottlenecks from transient to structural and amplifying the sectorally concentrated inflation hypothesis.  _(#45, r4)_
- **Stablecoins are a fast-growing, structurally price-insensitive marginal UST bid** — Dollar-denominated stablecoins (USDT, USDC, et al.) must hold short-duration US Treasuries as reserves to maintain their pegs. At ~$220B and growing toward a plausible $1–2T, they represent a new class of mandatory T-bill buyer that is indifferent to rate levels — buying more UST precisely when the stablecoin supply expands. This partially offsets the shrinking sovereign UST bid from weaponization and petrodollar recycling decline, and is not captured in any of the existing 46 notes.  _(#47, r5)_
- **Spontaneous EM stablecoin dollarization is a demand floor independent of reserve policy** — In high-inflation economies (Argentina, Turkey, sub-Saharan Africa), dollar-denominated stablecoins are replacing local currency for savings and everyday transactions at the retail level — a spontaneous, bottom-up dollarization that operates entirely outside central-bank reserve decisions. This reinforces structural dollar demand even as sovereign reserve managers diversify, creating two diverging demand trends that aggregate reserve statistics cannot distinguish.  _(#49, r5)_
- **Entitlement spending is quasi-automatic and demographically locked-in, making fiscal dominance near-inevitable in G7 aging states** — Unlike industrial-policy capex or discretionary stimulus, age-triggered healthcare and defined-benefit pension entitlements are actuarially determined: IMF projections show G7 age-related spending rising 4–6 ppts of GDP by 2035 versus 2020 baselines under unchanged policy. This structural fiscal pressure is additive to AI capex and debt-rollover stresses already modeled, operates without any legislative action, and is largely irreversible on political timescales — creating a near-deterministic path toward fiscal dominance independent of discretionary choices.  _(#59, r6)_
- **Wealth-effect consumption sustains payroll and income-tax receipts well beyond direct capital-gains — broadening the fiscal revenue at risk** — The AI equity boom generates a second-order fiscal windfall: elevated household wealth drives consumption, supporting employment-level payroll taxes and income taxes on wages — revenues that accrue even to households that never realize capital gains. In a bust, this second-order channel reverses, meaning the total fiscal revenue erosion is substantially larger than a capital-gains-only analysis implies and cuts across federal, state, and local government simultaneously.  _(#72, r7)_
- **Recurring debt-ceiling brinkmanship exhausts extraordinary measures and progressively normalizes temporary US default risk, each episode requiring a larger implicit Fed backstop** — Each debt-ceiling episode (2011, 2013, 2021, 2023, 2025) has consumed more extraordinary-measure headroom, lasted longer, and come closer to technical default. Markets have treated each as an event that 'always resolves' — but this baseline assumption is the confidence that, if broken even once, turns a technical event into a full fiscal-credibility crisis. The cumulative erosion of institutional guardrails is itself a form of slow-motion fiscal dominance that no existing note captures.  _(#78, r7)_
- **War finance + simultaneous semiconductor supply shock = structurally stagflationary combination** — A hot Taiwan conflict produces two shocks concurrently: a massive fiscal expansion (war spending, mobilization) that is unambiguously demand-inflationary, and a 3-to-5-year semiconductor supply disruption (Taiwan fabs produce ~90% of leading-edge chips) that is simultaneously cost-inflationary for capital goods and deflationary for AI productivity potential. The stagflationary combination — fiscal expansion into a constrained-supply economy — is far worse for the inflation/rate tradeoff than either shock in isolation, and it is precisely the scenario where the 'Treasuries rally as safe-haven vs. fall on fiscal-credibility crisis' crux is resolved in the worst direction.  _(#83, r8)_
- **Cheap energy → AI compute cost collapse: a nonlinear compounding feedback** — Energy costs constitute roughly 50–70% of AI inference and training expenses at hyperscale. A 5–10x reduction in industrial electricity prices produces a near-commensurate reduction in AI compute marginal cost, which in turn accelerates AI-driven productivity gains and further optimises energy systems (grid dispatch, demand forecasting, exploration). This positive feedback makes the energy-abundance/productivity-offset compounding materially more powerful than additive projections, potentially enabling growth rates high enough to outrun nominal debt accumulation.  _(#99, r9)_

## Disconfirming evidence (21)

- **Historical tech waves have been net deflationary despite massive capex** — The internet buildout (late 1990s) and semiconductor scaling compressed unit costs across the economy despite enormous capital investment. Unit labor costs in AI-exposed sectors are already declining. If AI follows this path, the inflation-from-capex argument collapses — the bottleneck is political spending, not private investment.  _(#4, r1)_
- **Dollar weaponization accelerates reserve diversification, shrinking the marginal UST bid** — Russia sanctions (2022) demonstrated that dollar reserves can be frozen. BRICS+ payment corridors, commodity invoicing in non-dollar currencies, and central bank gold accumulation are measurable responses. If foreign official holders reduce UST duration or share, the US must offer higher yields or the Fed must absorb more supply — the 'who buys the bonds' problem.  _(#7, r1)_
- **Japan's YCC experiment and its 2024 unwind is a closer, messier template than WWII repression** — Japan ran yield-curve control for nearly a decade under a high-debt, low-growth regime — far more structurally analogous to a post-AI-capex world than wartime America. The exit was disorderly: yen depreciation, imported inflation, and an abrupt unwind in global carry trades (August 2024). The WWII template implies a clean, controlled suppression; Japan demonstrates that the exit from financial repression is the dangerous phase, not the repression itself.  _(#15, r2)_
- **Eurodollar credit contraction is the opposite-direction tail: deflationary rather than inflationary** — The offshore dollar credit system creates multiples of the Fed's balance sheet in global dollar liquidity through interbank lending, cross-border repo, and dollar-denominated trade finance. A stress event — geopolitical shock, hyperscaler credit event, or UST liquidity crisis — that causes eurodollar contraction would be sharply deflationary (as in 2008 and March 2020), overwhelming domestic inflationary forces. This tail is underweighted in all the inflation-dominant scenarios.  _(#17, r2)_
- **AI capex could crowd out non-AI R&D and physical investment, making the net productivity offset ambiguous** — Government industrial policy and hyperscaler capex attract capital, engineers, and skilled labor away from other sectors — pharmaceuticals, manufacturing process innovation, basic science. If marginal researchers, chips, and power capacity are bid into AI at the expense of other productivity-enhancing investment, the economy-wide productivity gain from AI may be partially offset by productivity stagnation elsewhere. The productivity-offset hypothesis implicitly assumes additionality; crowding-out makes the net gain uncertain.  _(#24, r2)_
- **EM sovereign doom loop exports deflation back to developed markets** — When US rates rise and the dollar strengthens, EM sovereigns and corporates with dollar-denominated debt face solvency stress; the resulting capital flight, import compression, and commodity demand collapse transmit a deflationary impulse back to DM via trade volumes and financial contagion. This counter-current could dominate the inflationary narrative from AI capex — the existing notes have no mechanism for how the rest of the world responds to a high-rate, strong-dollar US.  _(#25, r3)_
- **Fiscal multipliers compress (possibly go negative) at high debt-to-GDP ratios** — IMF research and academic work (Blanchard, Barro, Corsetti) shows fiscal multipliers fall sharply as debt/GDP rises, turning near-zero or negative above ~90% — because rising risk premia and Ricardian offset crowd out private activity. If so, industrial-policy spending does not generate the inflationary impulse assumed by the fiscal dominance narrative; the spending may be self-defeating even before productivity pays off.  _(#26, r3)_
- **Government industrial policy can crowd IN private capital, undermining the r* elevation thesis** — Standard r* elevation assumes government spending competes with private capital for fixed savings. But developmental-state evidence (South Korea, Taiwan semiconductor buildout, early US DARPA investments) shows government as first-mover de-risks frontier investments and catalyzes private co-investment, expanding the total investment frontier. If the CHIPS Act and IRA replicate this pattern, private capex rises rather than falls, making the r* elevation self-defeating as a forecast.  _(#28, r3)_
- **Petrodollar recycling structural decline removes a pillar of UST demand independent of weaponization** — Gulf sovereign wealth funds have historically recycled oil surpluses into US Treasuries. As energy transition matures and oil demand peaks, this flow shrinks regardless of any deliberate diversification or geopolitical choice. Unlike the dollar-weaponization note (which focuses on sanctions-driven diversification), this is a mechanical revenue-decline channel — a supply-side shock to UST demand compounding the Triffin dilemma without requiring any hostile act.  _(#29, r3)_
- **Rapid AI labor displacement destroys aggregate demand faster than fiscal transfers compensate** — If AI substitutes labor at a pace exceeding job reabsorption, aggregate wage income and consumer spending fall faster than redistribution can offset — generating a demand-side deflationary impulse that could dominate the supply-side capex inflation. This is categorically distinct from the income-inequality open question (which concerns distribution of gains); this concerns aggregate wage-bill collapse and the Keynesian demand floor.  _(#32, r3)_
- **Mega-cap FCF self-funding severs the rate-transmission channel for the largest AI capex drivers** — Microsoft, Alphabet, Meta, and Amazon collectively generate several hundred billion dollars of annual free cash flow and hold enormous net-cash positions, making their AI capex decisions essentially rate-immune — no debt financing needed. This extends the private-credit intermediation note to its logical endpoint: the dominant capex actors require no external capital at all, meaning rate hikes cannot cool the AI investment cycle without causing collateral damage to rate-sensitive sectors far from the AI build.  _(#33, r3)_
- **China's AI-adjacent manufacturing overcapacity is an active deflation-export weapon, not passive EM distress** — Subsidized Chinese production of EVs, solar panels, industrial robots, and AI-hardware components floods Western import markets, suppressing goods prices even as services, energy, and labor inflation rises domestically in the West. Unlike the passive EM doom-loop note, this is a deliberate great-power trade strategy that partially offsets AI-capex inflation in import-exposed economies and complicates central-bank inflation readings.  _(#36, r4)_
- **Secondary-sanction risk creates forced (risk-driven) dedollarization with a more convex demand curve than preference-driven diversification** — Countries that credibly fear future designation as sanction targets (not just those with ideological reservations about dollar hegemony) pre-emptively reduce dollar holdings regardless of yield differentials. Unlike preference-driven diversification — which is yield-sensitive and partially reversible — forced dedollarization is structural and accelerates discontinuously with each new sanction episode. This implies UST demand is more convex and fragile under stress than standard reserve-diversification models assume.  _(#42, r4)_
- **Tokenized sovereign bonds democratize UST demand but also accelerate capital-flight velocity** — Tokenized Treasury products (BlackRock BUIDL, Franklin Templeton BENJI, and their successors) allow any globally connected wallet to hold fractional US government bonds — potentially expanding the sovereign debt demand base to billions of retail savers. However, 24/7 instant liquidity and programmable collateral release also mean that a confidence shock propagates into mass redemptions at machine speed, eliminating the natural friction that historically gave fiscal authorities time to respond. The same technology that deepens the buyer base also shortens the fuse.  _(#56, r5)_
- **East Asian demographic convergence collapses the inflationary-West / deflationary-East divergence thesis within 15 years** — The existing divergence note treats the East Asian manufacturing core as a durable deflationary anchor. But Japan (median age ~49) is already as old as Western Europe; Korea is aging at the fastest rate in OECD history; China’s median age will match Germany’s by ~2040. As these populations shift to net dissavers, the structural excess-savings capacity that underpins East Asian deflationary export capacity erodes. If the divergence collapses, both blocs become simultaneously inflationary, eliminating the global deflationary offset — the worst-case for global inflation.  _(#61, r6)_
- **Mature pension fund decumulation creates a secular institutional bid for duration that partially offsets fiscal bond-supply pressure** — As defined-benefit pension plans in the Netherlands, Japan, Canada, and UK shift from accumulation to payout phase, they rebalance from equities into long-duration fixed income to match liabilities, creating a persistent structural bid for long bonds even as government issuance surges. This is the opposite of the LDI/NBFI non-linear stress note: secular decumulation-driven rebalancing could partially suppress term premiums despite fiscal expansion, delaying the duration cliff. The offset breaks down only if fiscal supply overwhelms the decumulation flow — but the transition period could be 5–10 years of suppressed term premium.  _(#65, r6)_
- **Fed's ON-RRP drawdown from $2.5T to near-zero has already consumed the 'hidden Treasury bid'; the structural demand gap is now visible** — Money market funds parking reserves in the Fed's overnight reverse repo facility indirectly supported Treasury demand by keeping money-market rates from collapsing and drawing in substitutable short-term debt. With ON-RRP now drained, this multi-year buffer is exhausted. The Treasury market now faces its underlying supply/demand balance without this structural absorber — making the absence of an explicit Federal Reserve backstop more acute, precisely as fiscal deficits are expanding.  _(#76, r7)_
- **Defense-to-civilian tech spillover historically amplifies the productivity offset — a disconfirming note on rearmament-is-inflationary** — DARPA-lineage innovations (internet, GPS, jet engines, nuclear energy) produced civilian productivity gains that far exceeded their initial defense budgets. In the current cycle, the defense-AI overlap runs in both directions: defense funding accelerates AI development (JADC2, autonomy, ISR), which then diffuses into civilian applications. If military AI spending functions as publicly-funded basic research that commercial AI then scales, it *strengthens* the productivity-offset hypothesis for the combined AI+defense supercycle, partially negating the 'defense spending has no productivity offset' premise. This is a genuine counter-argument to the rearmament-as-pure-inflation thesis.  _(#87, r8)_
- **Jevons paradox: cheap energy expands consumption, offsetting deflationary unit-cost gains** — Every historical episode of dramatic energy cost reduction — coal in the Industrial Revolution, cheap natural gas post-shale — increased total energy consumption rather than shrinking energy-sector expenditure. If AI-cheap energy follows the same pattern, the deflationary impulse from per-unit cost declines is largely absorbed by volume expansion, and the net macroeconomic effect on inflation and nominal GDP is ambiguous rather than cleanly deflationary.  _(#93, r9)_
- **Fiscal revenue destruction: energy deflation hollows the government revenue base** — G7 governments collectively collect hundreds of billions annually in fuel excise duties, carbon prices, and corporate taxes from fossil fuel companies. Rapid energy deflation collapses this revenue stream at the exact moment entitlement and debt-service costs peak. Rather than growing out of debt, energy deflation could worsen the structural deficit — making cheap energy a headwind for fiscal sustainability, not a tailwind.  _(#96, r9)_
- **Grid transmission bottleneck decouples generation costs from economic delivery by a decade** — US and EU transmission networks require an estimated $2–4T in upgrades and face 15–20 year permitting/construction timelines regardless of how cheap generation becomes. Even zero-cost solar or fusion power cannot reach data centres, factories, or consumers without the grid. The energy-abundance deflationary thesis assumes generation cost = delivered cost; the transmission bottleneck severs this linkage on the exact fiscal timeline that matters.  _(#98, r9)_

## Cruxes (22)

- **Crux: Is the AI capex cycle self-liquidating, or does it leave a stranded-asset overhang?** — If AI ROI materializes (revenue grows, productivity gains show in GDP), the debt incurred is self-financing and sovereign balance sheets improve. If AI capex creates stranded assets (as telecom fiber did in 2000–2002), corporate balance sheet repair amplifies fiscal pressure, weakens tax revenues, and forces central banks to ease — stagflationary if supply hasn't come through.  _(#5, r1)_
- **Crux: Can central banks maintain political independence when debt service becomes intolerable?** — Fiscal dominance only materializes if governments can override or co-opt central banks. In the 1970s, Fed independence eventually won (Volcker). Post-COVID, no central bank has been formally subordinated — but none faced debt/GDP ratios above 120% with deficits above 6% simultaneously. Whether institutional independence survives that pressure is the key governance crux; resolution depends on constitutional, electoral, and personnel factors, not economics.  _(#11, r1)_
- **Temporal mismatch: AI capex inflates today; productivity payoffs arrive years later** — The productivity-offset hypothesis treats supply-side gains as contemporaneous with capex, but the actual lag between infrastructure buildout and economy-wide productivity diffusion is historically 5–15 years (electrification, internet). Central banks face a holding problem: they must respond to demand-pull inflation now while being told the deflationary offset is coming. If they tighten, they risk aborting the cycle; if they wait, expectations de-anchor. The timing asymmetry — not the magnitude — is the operative crux.  _(#14, r2)_
- **TSMC/Taiwan concentration is a binary crux on whether the AI capex cycle can complete** — Leading-edge AI accelerator production is almost entirely dependent on TSMC's fabs in Taiwan. A Taiwan Strait contingency — or even credible threat — would simultaneously terminate AI capex plans for hyperscalers, trigger massive defense spending, and force emergency reshoring investment. This is not a gradual fragmentation-premium scenario but a binary discontinuity that could make every other macro-loop model irrelevant. Whether this risk is priced into long-duration AI investments is an open question.  _(#18, r2)_
- **Inflation expectations can de-anchor before fiscal dominance actually arrives, making it self-fulfilling** — If bond markets come to believe that fiscal dominance is the likely endgame, long-term inflation expectations will de-anchor and term premiums will rise before central banks lose formal independence. Higher term premiums increase debt service costs, accelerating the fiscal math toward dominance — a self-fulfilling spiral. The crux is not just whether fiscal dominance arrives but whether market expectations of it trigger the very conditions that make it inevitable.  _(#20, r2)_
- **Capital-account asymmetry bifurcates the fiscal-dominance endgame: China resolves it quietly; the US faces open-market discipline** — China can administratively repress domestic interest rates without triggering capital flight because its capital account is closed; the US cannot do so without imposing capital controls that would shatter dollar reserve status. This means the two blocs face structurally different fiscal-dominance resolution paths — China slides into administered rates and directed credit invisibly, while the US faces a visible, potentially acute bond-market discipline event. The endgame is not one scenario but two divergent ones.  _(#37, r4)_
- **Multi-CBDC platforms (mBridge) are the first credible non-dollar commodity-settlement rail — the operational crux for dedollarization** — BIS Project mBridge (China, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Thailand, Hong Kong) provides technical infrastructure to settle commodity trades in non-dollar CBDCs without correspondent banking or SWIFT. If Saudi Arabia clears even a marginal share of oil exports in e-CNY via mBridge, dedollarization ceases to be aspirational and gains an actual clearing mechanism. The petrodollar notes address demand for USTs; this addresses the payment-rail chokepoint that has, until now, made dedollarization structurally incoherent.  _(#50, r5)_
- **Crux: Does digital-money adoption extend or fragment the dollar system?** — If major EMs and commodity exporters adopt dollar-backed CBDC or stablecoin rails, digital money extends dollar dominance even as physical reserve diversification continues — 'dollarization 2.0.' If instead they adopt incompatible non-dollar CBDC systems (e.g., mBridge-native e-CNY rails), capital flows fragment beyond currency blocs into incommensurable monetary jurisdictions. This bifurcation is the master crux for the entire digital-money channel: the same technology either reinforces the dollar system or accelerates its replacement, depending on adoption path.  _(#54, r5)_
- **Aging median voter makes entitlement reform democratically impossible — fiscal dominance is a political equilibrium, not just a financial outcome** — In G7 countries, the median voter will cross age 50 before 2035. A median voter whose income is largely transfer-based (pensions, Medicare/NHS) has a direct economic interest in opposing fiscal consolidation. This creates a structural political lock-in: the fiscal dominance endgame is not merely probable given financial dynamics, it is the rational electoral equilibrium in aging democracies. The crux is whether technocratic institutions can override democratic preferences — and that crux is not primarily financial.  _(#60, r6)_
- **Crux: Does demographic labor-force contraction absorb AI displacement, or does displacement outrun the demographic decline?** — The existing disconfirming note (AI displacement destroys aggregate demand) assumes an elastic labor supply generating a wage-deflationary surplus. But working-age populations are contracting in Japan, Germany, Korea, China, and parts of Eastern Europe. If AI displacement velocity is slower than the demographic headcount decline, displaced workers exit a labor force already shrinking — no wage-deflationary glut materializes. The direction of the macro outcome hinges entirely on which force dominates in each decade — and the answer likely differs by decade (demographic contraction faster in 2020s; AI faster in 2030s).  _(#63, r6)_
- **Crux: In an AI-driven bust, do Treasuries rally (safe-haven) or fall with equities (fiscal-credibility crisis)?** — The bust's impact on government finances hinges entirely on which correlation regime holds. In a standard risk-off recession, Treasuries rally, automatic stabilizers work, and the fiscal shock is manageable. If the bust is simultaneously a loss of fiscal credibility (2022 UK gilt crisis template, Japan JGB sell-off), equities and bonds fall together, the automatic stabilizers are funded at rising cost, and a doom loop opens. This regime-switch is the critical crux that none of the existing notes name.  _(#70, r7)_
- **AI equity concentration in a handful of stocks creates non-diversifiable, correlated fiscal-revenue risk** — If the top 5–10 AI stocks account for a disproportionate share of capital-gains realizations (as Magnificent-7 concentration implies), a sector-specific AI bust delivers a concentrated fiscal revenue shock — unlike a diversified market correction where gains in other sectors partially offset losses. There is no fiscal hedge; IRS revenue is as concentrated as the index.  _(#71, r7)_
- **SLR/dealer-balance-sheet constraints are the binding plumbing constraint that converts fiscal dominance into forced Fed intervention** — Primary dealers are constrained by the Supplementary Leverage Ratio, capping their capacity to warehouse expanding Treasury supply. When Treasury issuance exceeds dealer intermediation capacity — as it structurally must if deficits grow — the Fed is operationally compelled to intervene to prevent market dysfunction, regardless of its stated policy stance. Fiscal dominance thus arrives not through political capture but through market-plumbing necessity — a critically different mechanism from the existing fiscal-dominance note.  _(#73, r7)_
- **Hot kinetic conflict is the classic trigger — not just a template or risk — for coerced financial repression** — The WWII note treats financial repression as a historical template to emulate voluntarily; what it misses is the *causal mechanism*: hot war forces rate suppression below inflation within weeks (not years) because the borrowing requirement is immediate, non-deferrable, and existentially framed, eliminating the political agency that makes 'fiscal dominance' a gradual endgame. A Taiwan or Korea-adjacent conflict would not 'gate' the AI capex cycle — it would decisively resolve the fiscal-dominance crux by triggering war-speed monetary financing, rendering central bank independence moot by emergency fiat rather than by incremental debt-service pressure.  _(#80, r8)_
- **China's ~$750B–$1T in UST is a binary non-linear event, not a gradual diversification** — Existing notes cover dollar weaponization (gradual reserve diversification) and petrodollar decline (structural erosion). A Taiwan conflict creates a categorically different event: either the US freezes Chinese UST holdings (extending the Russia-reserve-freeze precedent roughly 10x in scale, ~$300B Russia vs ~$750B+ China), or China dumps them offensively as a financial weapon before a freeze is imposed. Either is a discrete, non-linear Treasury demand destruction event — not a trend — that immediately reprices the risk-free rate and forces Fed intervention. This resolves the 'dollar milkshake vs. weaponization' tension with a discontinuous shock rather than a gradual tipping point.  _(#82, r8)_
- **Enemy-asset sequestration establishes monetary-warfare as a new fiscal tool at Taiwan-conflict scale** — The Russia sanctions introduced a template: adversary central bank reserves (~$300B) can be frozen and potentially deployed to fund the opposing war effort. Congress actively debated seizing and spending Russian assets for Ukraine. In a Taiwan conflict, the potentially sequestrable Chinese-linked claims in US jurisdiction are an order of magnitude larger. If seized assets are monetized to fund war expenditure, this creates a novel category: war finance that is neither bond issuance nor money printing, but confiscation — with profound implications for international monetary law, the credibility of any dollar-denominated asset held by a non-allied sovereign, and the velocity of reserve diversification globally.  _(#85, r8)_
- **Belligerent-asset freeze converts dollar-reserve safety from a continuous gradient to a binary political variable** — The Russia freeze changed the nature of reserve diversification: holding dollar assets is no longer about yield, liquidity, and FX stability, but about whether the holder is a current or potential US adversary — a political classification that can change overnight. Every non-allied sovereign now implicitly holds an option on confiscation that is priced discontinuously (alliance status changes in discrete steps, not continuously). This converts the 'secondary-sanction risk creates forced dedollarization' note from a linear demand curve into a convex, threshold-triggered one: a Taiwan conflict could cause simultaneous reserve diversification across all 'non-allied' or 'ambiguously-aligned' sovereigns, not just the combatants.  _(#86, r8)_
- **Japan's treaty-ally status makes a Taiwan conflict a simultaneous JGB + UST dual-anchor stress event** — Japan is the US's primary treaty ally for any Taiwan scenario, holds ~$1.1T in UST (the largest single foreign holder), and has a domestic fiscal position already stressed by YCC unwind and aging demographics. Military mobilization alongside the US would require immediate Japanese defense expenditure that competes directly with JGB market stability and BOJ normalization — stressing both anchor-currency bond markets simultaneously. The existing Japan note covers the YCC unwind as a standalone template; the dual-anchor stress scenario (JGB and UST under simultaneous war-finance pressure) is a distinct, more severe systemic configuration not addressed anywhere in the note graph.  _(#90, r8)_
- **Energy-abundance arrival window vs. fiscal-dominance lock-in window** — Solar+storage is already on an exponential cost curve (utility-scale LCOS now sub-$50/MWh in prime locations); SMRs are 10–15 years out; fusion is 20+. The crux is whether meaningful energy-price deflation reaches industrial users within the 5–10 year window before fiscal dominance becomes structurally entrenched — a timing question entirely distinct from whether energy abundance is eventually possible.  _(#91, r9)_
- **Wright's Law compounding simultaneously across solar, batteries, and AI chips** — Unlike all prior tech waves, three mutually reinforcing learning curves are running in parallel: solar PV (halving every ~3 years), lithium-ion/solid-state storage, and AI accelerator chips. The crux is whether this simultaneous multi-sector compounding produces a deflationary impulse that is qualitatively larger than any single historical tech wave — strong enough to overwhelm fiscal expansion rather than just dampening it at the margin.  _(#94, r9)_
- **Housing equity as 60–70% of median household net worth creates an implicit Fed 'third mandate' that constrains rate normalization politically** — Unlike the Volcker era (lower homeownership financialization), today's homeowner-voter coalition would suffer severe balance-sheet losses from aggressive normalization. This is a political-economy binding constraint on the central bank that is independent of bond-market vigilantes or institutional fiscal dominance — it operates through electoral punishment of any government or appointed official who presides over housing deflation. The crux is whether this implicit mandate strengthens or weakens as renter share rises.  _(#104, r10)_
- **The inflation-tax incidence crux: financial repression taxes nominal creditors, inflate-away taxes wage earners, wealth taxation taxes equity holders, austerity taxes public-service recipients — the governing political coalition determines which path is chosen** — No resolution path is politically costless; each destroys a different constituency. The crux is not 'which path is financially optimal' but 'which injured constituency is least able to punish the governing coalition.' In the US: equity-holding top quintile blocks wealth taxes; homeowners block rate hikes; wage earners resist inflation; retirees resist nominal repression. The macro endgame is therefore a political-coalition equilibrium, and the forecast requires a model of coalition stability — which is entirely absent from the existing note set.  _(#107, r10)_

## Open questions (3)

- **Open question: Does AI compress or expand income inequality, and how does that change the inflation transmission?** — If AI concentrates gains at the top of the income distribution (low marginal propensity to consume), fiscal spending may not translate to broad demand inflation — it leaks into asset prices instead. If AI democratizes income (broadly raises wages by eliminating drudge work), MPC is higher and spending translates to goods/services inflation. The distributional channel is underspecified in most macro-financial analyses of this loop.  _(#12, r1)_
- **Do middle powers (India, Gulf, ASEAN, Brazil) face a new monetary trilemma: align with the dollar bloc, the RMB bloc, or pay the cost of non-alignment?** — In a bifurcated monetary world, middle-power central banks cannot simultaneously maintain exchange-rate stability with both blocs, preserve two-way capital mobility, and retain independent monetary policy. The alignment choices of the ~30 largest non-bloc economies collectively determine the pace of dollar erosion, the viability of RMB internationalization, and the distribution of inflation vs. deflation across the global system — making their aggregate choice a macro variable as significant as any G2 policy decision.  _(#46, r4)_
- **Open question: Does immigration-as-demographic-offset remain politically viable, and what happens to US fiscal and labor trajectories if it closes?** — The US and Canada have used above-trend immigration to partially offset demographic labor-force contraction throughout the 2010s–2020s, producing labor-market and inflation dynamics that diverge from Japan, Germany, and Korea. The open question is whether the current political backlash closes this valve — and if it does, whether the US demographic trajectory converges toward the Japan/Germany model (slower growth, mounting entitlement pressure, labor scarcity amplified). The immigration valve is a binary fork in the US-specific version of every hypothesis in this note graph: fiscal dominance probability, r* path, and labor scarcity vs. displacement all pivot on it.  _(#66, r6)_

## Historical analogies (36)

- **US WWII Mobilization + Fed Yield Curve Control, 1941–1951** — The Fed formally capped Treasury bill yields at 0.375% and long-bond yields at 2.5% to finance the war effort — the first explicit yield-curve control regime. Fiscal deficits ran 25–30% of GDP. Post-war, the peg was maintained even as inflation hit 15–20% (1946–47), silently eroding the real value of debt from ~120% to ~80% of GDP without default. The monetary-statecraft endgame was the Fed-Treasury Accord of March 1951, which restored nominal Fed independence only after the debt load was manageable.

- **Italy: Lira Weakness → Maastricht Convergence → Borrowed ECB Credibility as Monetary Endgame, 1992–1999** — Italy had chronic inflation (12–20% in the late 1970s, still 5–6% into the late 1980s) driven by fiscal deficits, indexation (scala mobile), and political inability to sustain monetary discipline. Italy was forced out of the ERM alongside the UK in September 1992. Rather than treating this as a licence to inflate, successive Italian governments (including Prodi's) implemented severe austerity (one-off 'Eurotax', pension reforms, primary surplus generation) to meet the Maastricht criteria. By 1999, Italy joined the euro — in effect outsourcing its monetary policy to the ECB (which inherited Bundesbank credibility). Italian 10-year bond yields collapsed from 12% to 4%, saving roughly 4–5% of GDP in annual interest costs. The strategy was 'import credibility via external supranational anchor' rather than generate it domestically.
  - _Similar:_ Italy used the EMU entry commitment as a device to do domestically what it lacked political will to do independently — the external constraint as domestic reform enabler is a recurring monetary-statecraft pattern; Bond market spread compression was the immediate reward: the credibility import was priced in real-time through yield convergence, creating a positive feedback loop that made the political pain politically sustainable; Illustrates the structural asymmetry that is central to the focal topic: countries WITH a credible external anchor available can borrow monetary credibility; countries that ARE the anchor must generate it internally
  - _Different:_ The strategy is structurally unavailable to the US: there is no supranational monetary authority above the Fed to which the US can outsource credibility — the US is, by definition, the terminal anchor of the global system; Italy's entry at what proved to be an overvalued rate stored up competitiveness problems that erupted in the 2010–2012 euro crisis — credibility import delayed but did not eliminate the structural adjustment; The Maastricht convergence criteria were externally imposed legal constraints; there is no equivalent external discipline mechanism for US fiscal-monetary policy; EMU stripped Italy of the exchange rate as an adjustment tool — the resulting 'internal devaluation' (wage deflation) was politically explosive in a way that dollar depreciation is not for the US
  - _Implies:_ The Italy/EMU case illuminates the focal topic's monetary-statecraft endgame by showing the structural impossibility of the 'borrow credibility' strategy for the reserve-currency issuer. Every other country in history facing a credibility deficit has had access to some form of external anchor — IMF programme, currency board, EMU entry, dollar peg. The US has none. If AI-capex fiscal dominance erodes dollar credibility sufficiently, the endgame must be entirely self-generated: a Volcker-type personality shock, or an NZ-type institutional reform, with no external backstop. This makes the US credibility problem uniquely difficult to resolve once lost.  _(#A35, r6)_
- **Turkey: Erdoganomics CB Independence Destruction → Lira Collapse → Simsek/Karahan Rate Shock, 2021–2024** — President Erdogan fired three CBRT governors between 2019 and 2021 who raised rates against his wishes. His publicly stated theory — that high interest rates cause inflation — led to the CBRT cutting its policy rate from 19% to 8.5% between August and November 2021 even as inflation surged. CPI reached 85% (October 2022). The lira lost roughly 80% of its value against the dollar between 2021 and 2023. After winning re-election in May 2023 with his mandate secured, Erdogan reversed course entirely, appointing orthodox economist Mehmet Simsek as Finance Minister and installing a series of market-credible CBRT governors. The policy rate was raised from 8.5% to 50% in 18 months — one of the most aggressive tightening cycles in emerging-market history. By mid-2024, real rates turned positive for the first time in years and CPI began declining from its peak.
  - _Similar:_ The most vivid recent case of central bank independence being openly and publicly contested/destroyed by political actors, followed by monetary/currency crisis, followed by a forced reassertion of orthodox policy — the complete arc in a compressed timeframe; The rate shock magnitude (8.5% to 50%) and the political timing (post-election, after mandate secured) mirrors the Volcker dynamic: the rate shock only became politically viable once the political cycle gave cover; Demonstrates that the forcing mechanism for CB independence reassertion is typically exogenous pressure (currency collapse, reserve depletion, market access loss) rather than proactive political will — directly relevant to whether the US can impose a Volcker-type shock before, not after, a dollar credibility event; The sequence — heterodox experiment → crisis → forced orthodox reversal — is the prototype for what may follow if AI-capex fiscal dominance erodes Fed independence progressively
  - _Different:_ Turkey is an EM country with a non-reserve, non-anchor currency: the forcing mechanism (lira collapse → import inflation → political crisis) is structurally unavailable for the USD, which can depreciate substantially without triggering equivalent domestic price-level shock; The rate shock was reactive and crisis-induced — Volcker acted proactively before the dollar collapsed; the Turkish case is the cautionary 'what happens if you wait' scenario, not the deliberate endgame management scenario; Turkey's inflation had structural components (energy import dependency, dollarised contracts, FX pass-through >50%) that differ from AI-capex demand-side inflation in the US; Simsek's credibility came partly from his Goldman Sachs / IMF pedigree signalling to foreign investors — the US cannot similarly signal credibility to an external audience because there is no higher-credibility authority to signal alignment with
  - _Implies:_ Turkey is the sharpest contemporary illustration of the monetary-statecraft endgame's political economy: CB independence, once openly contested and destroyed, can be reasserted — but only after paying a large real cost (80% currency depreciation, 85% inflation), and only because the political cycle gave the executive cover to reverse. For the AI-capex era, the Turkish case is the cautionary scenario for the US: if fiscal dominance progressively erodes Fed independence through appointment pressure and mandate creep rather than formal destruction, the US may find itself in a slow-motion Turkish pattern — needing a crisis-triggered Simsek moment but lacking the forcing mechanism (dollar cannot collapse in the Turkish way) that would make the political reversal viable. The endgame is harder to reach, but also harder to escape.  _(#A36, r6)_
