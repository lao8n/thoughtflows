# Notes — Tail discontinuities and regime breaks: Taiwan/TSMC kinetic shock, decisive capability advantage, safety-catastrophe clampdown, and great-power war

## Theories / lenses (31)

- **TSMC as irreplaceable single-point-of-failure: the 'silicon shield' cuts both ways** — TSMC fabricates ~90% of leading-edge logic (<5nm). Kinetic shock destroys this capacity irreversibly for 5–10 years (fabs cannot be relocated or rapidly rebuilt). The 'silicon shield' hypothesis says this deters attack; the inverse is that it makes Taiwan maximally valuable as a hostage/prize, and that a state willing to absorb the economic cost can hold the world's AI compute supply hostage.  _(#1, r1)_
- **Decisive capability advantage follows a 'nuclear moment' structure, not a gradual lead** — The Manhattan Project analogy: capability advantages only become 'decisive' if they cross a qualitative threshold that cannot be offset by quantity or time. Below the threshold, diffusion and competitor catch-up dominate. The crux for AI is whether recursive self-improvement or autonomous R&D acceleration creates a discontinuous jump before diffusion closes the gap.  _(#3, r1)_
- **Great-power war as 'scenario amplifier': it does not merely add risk but transforms the probability space of all other discontinuities** — A US-China kinetic conflict short of nuclear exchange would simultaneously: (a) destroy TSMC compute supply, (b) fracture the global AI talent pool and research collaboration, (c) likely trigger emergency national security AI development programs, and (d) make safety norms politically untenable. Great-power war is not one tail among four—it is a superposition state that triggers or transforms the others.  _(#9, r1)_
- **Hegemonic stability lens: decisive AI advantage by one power may stabilize rather than destabilize** — Classical hegemonic stability theory holds that a single dominant power is a prerequisite for stable international order. A multipolar world where three or four states each believe they may soon achieve decisive AI advantage is more likely to produce preventive war than one where dominance is already settled. Decisive advantage could end racing pressure, not ignite it.  _(#16, r2)_
- **War duration is the governing variable: short vs. protracted conflict produce opposite AI-development effects** — A short, decisive conflict (weeks to months, like 1967 Six Day War model) may compress rather than disrupt AI development timelines by forcing emergency capability investment. A protracted conflict (years, like Ukraine) would likely delay decisive AI advantage by destroying the economic and supply-chain foundations of frontier compute. The 'great-power war as amplifier' framing is ambiguous across these radically different durations.  _(#23, r2)_
- **Decisive AI advantage creates first-strike instability, opposite of nuclear MAD** — Nuclear weapons produce crisis stability via mutually assured destruction — neither side benefits from striking first. A decisive AI capability advantage creates the inverse: use-it-or-lose-it incentives, since the trailing power is racing to close the gap and the leading power's window is temporary. This predicts *destabilized* crises, not hegemonic stability, during any period when advantage is large but contested.  _(#26, r3)_
- **Normal-accidents lens: safety catastrophe from tight coupling, not intentional misuse** — Perrow's normal accidents theory predicts that tightly coupled, complexly interactive systems produce catastrophic failures as a near-mathematical inevitability, independent of bad actors or deliberate delegation. AI systems embedded in military C2, financial infrastructure, or grid management exhibit exactly these properties. This lens predicts catastrophe from emergent complexity rather than misuse or delegation failure — a distinct mechanism with different prevention implications.  _(#30, r3)_
- **Inventory-cliff dynamic makes the first 12–18 months catastrophically worse than equilibrium models** — Leading-edge chip consumers (hyperscalers, defense primes, auto OEMs) run lean inventories of weeks to a few months under just-in-time supply logic. A sudden TSMC shutdown creates an acute collapse phase — production lines halting across unrelated sectors before any alternative supply comes online — that steady-state disruption models systematically underestimate. This phase is the period of maximum economic and political leverage.  _(#36, r4)_
- **Semiconductor supply collapse is a financial-contagion catalyst, not just a production disruption** — Tech-sector equity comprises ~30% of major indices; a credible or actual TSMC disruption would trigger simultaneous repricing of AI-infrastructure equities, hyperscaler bonds, and sovereign debt for tech-export-dependent economies (Taiwan, South Korea, Netherlands). The resulting credit-market feedback could amplify the real-economy impact by an order of magnitude — analogous structurally to how 2008's mortgage shock became a broader financial crisis.  _(#41, r4)_
- **Nuclear-conventional C2 entanglement creates inadvertent escalation paths independent of intent** — US conventional precision-strike systems (hypersonics, sub-launched Tomahawks) share command-and-control and targeting infrastructure with nuclear forces; a Chinese strike on conventional C2 nodes can appear—or functionally be—a decapitation attempt on nuclear command, triggering nuclear response logic. China has a symmetric entanglement: its DF-26 is dual-capable, so US targeting of conventional ballistic missile launchers is indistinguishable from an attempt to destroy China's nuclear deterrent. This mechanism produces escalation without either side intending it.  _(#47, r5)_
- **Space domain follows a use-before-lose ASAT logic that can structurally precede and trigger kinetic conflict** — Both the US and China have strong first-mover incentives to blind adversary ISR, GPS, and communications satellites at conflict onset. Unlike nuclear or conventional exchanges, ASAT use is irreversible (debris cascades, Kessler syndrome risk) and there is no arms-control framework governing thresholds or retaliation norms. This means space domain attacks could initiate — not merely accompany — a kinetic conflict, on a timeline driven by satellite orbital mechanics rather than human political decision-making.  _(#49, r5)_
- **AI-assisted ISR may for the first time make adversary nuclear forces trackable, creating genuine use-it-or-lose-it pressure on nuclear posture** — The existing first-strike instability note covers AI capability advantage generally, but the specific nuclear mechanism is: AI-fused satellite, acoustic, and signals intelligence could make Chinese (or US) mobile ICBM/SSBN locations sufficiently legible that a precision conventional or nuclear disarming strike becomes plausible. This tips nuclear deterrence from assured-retaliation to 'strike before you're tracked' — a qualitatively different regime than the existing note describes.  _(#50, r5)_
- **Autonomous weapons operating inside human decision cycles can trigger wartime exchanges no human authorized — distinct from peacetime delegation failure** — The existing 'military AI delegation failure' hypothesis concerns peacetime safety catastrophe from AI misuse. The wartime escalation version is different: AI-enabled drone swarms, autonomous naval systems, and cyber-attack bots act at machine tempo (milliseconds to seconds), compressing response windows below the ~3-8 minutes that human nuclear authorization protocols require. An autonomous exchange that begins as a conventional skirmish can reach irreversible escalation before any human decision is possible.  _(#55, r5)_
- **Hyperscaler compute-ownership is a de facto nationalization lever that makes formal lab expropriation unnecessary** — Microsoft (Azure/OpenAI), Google (GCP/DeepMind), and Amazon (AWS) hold the compute contracts that underpin frontier capability. Governments can redirect or deny this compute access through contract direction or emergency requisition of cloud infrastructure without ever touching the lab's corporate structure — the hyperscaler is the hidden swing actor in any state-capture scenario, not the lab itself.  _(#58, r6)_
- **Model weights as information assets make AI nationalization categorically harder than all historical physical-asset precedents** — Unlike oil fields, enrichment plants, or TSMC fabs, decisive-advantage model weights are copyable and concealable — exfiltrable on a thumb drive by key employees before any commandeering order takes effect. The state therefore confronts a novel principal-agent problem: it can seize the corporate entity but may capture only a shell, since the actual capability lives in weights and in the heads of researchers who can resign or emigrate.  _(#59, r6)_
- **FFRDC/quasi-governmental hybrid is the historically dominant US equilibrium — outright nationalization is the tail, not the mode** — The US government has consistently preferred hybrid structures (RAND, Mitre, Lincoln Lab, NatLab) that preserve talent incentives while securing classified access, over formal expropriation that triggers talent flight and destroys the knowledge base being acquired. A FFRDC-style arrangement for a decisive-advantage AI lab — not nationalization — is the modal US state-capture scenario, with distinct implications for rent-distribution and governance.  _(#60, r6)_
- **Talent is the non-commandeerable core asset — nationalization systematically cannot retain the capability it is designed to acquire** — Historical analogies where talent-commandeering succeeded (Soviet atomic program, wartime Manhattan Project) required coercive retention that is unavailable in liberal democracies. AI capability is unusually researcher-concentrated: a small number of architects understand why a system works. A commandeered lab from which ten key researchers resign or emigrate likely captures less than 50% of the decisive advantage. This talent-flight discount is structural and applies even when weights are secured.  _(#65, r6)_
- **Compute concentration is a verification *enabler*, making AI governance more tractable than nuclear** — Frontier training runs require a small number of identifiable, power-hungry, commercially visible data centres. Unlike fissile material, which is dispersible and concealable, frontier compute leaves observable signatures — satellite-detectable power draw, grid load, chip-import manifests, hyperscaler billing records. This is the affirmative structural case for why an IAEA-style regime is more physically feasible for AI than for nuclear, inverting the usual framing that treats concentration only as a nationalization lever.  _(#70, r7)_
- **Mutual catastrophic-risk exposure creates a pre-shock shared-interest basis for governance, distinct from post-shock coordination failure** — If both superpowers genuinely believe a misaligned or uncontrolled AI could damage their own strategic position — destroying AI investments, destabilising domestic governance, or triggering accidental escalation — this produces a pre-catastrophe Reykjavik dynamic: neither side needs to wait for the shock to see the value of an accord. The existing notes model coordination failure exclusively as a post-shock problem; this is the prior-to-shock analogue that actually enables arms-control formation.  _(#71, r7)_
- **Third-actor defection problem: bilateral US–China accord is structurally undermined by out-of-accord actors** — Even a functioning US–China compute-governance agreement can be defeated by a frontier EU lab, a UAE/Saudi sovereign AI fund, a well-resourced open-source release, or a smaller state operating outside the accord. Unlike nuclear, where breakout requires controlled rare materials, AI capability could be achieved by a non-signatory using commercially available hardware at a fraction of frontier cost. This structural leakage problem is categorically harder than the analogous NPT challenge.  _(#74, r7)_
- **China-first decisive advantage inverts — not merely extends — the hegemonic stability thesis** — The existing hegemonic stability note implicitly frames decisive advantage as a potential US/Western outcome. If China achieves the threshold first, every inference reverses: the stabilising hegemon is now the strategic adversary of the current order, revisionist goals become achievable, and the 'stability' produced is hostile to liberal international institutions. This is a categorically distinct scenario, not a symmetric one, with different geopolitical, alliance, and governance implications.  _(#75, r7)_
- **Systematic discontinuity over-prediction is a structural bias in strategic analysis — the null hypothesis of gradual adaptation is the empirical base case** — Strategic and intelligence communities have institutional incentive structures that reward threat-inflation and novel discontinuity forecasting (the 'missile gap,' bioweapons revolution predictions, Y2K infrastructure catastrophe). The empirical base rate of predicted technological discontinuities that actually produced abrupt geopolitical breaks is low; the dominant historical pattern is that states adapt, capabilities diffuse, and the international system absorbs shocks without regime breaks. The no-discontinuity scenario deserves explicit probability mass as the baseline, not as a residual.  _(#78, r7)_
- **Window-to-exploit closes before decisive advantage can be converted to strategic outcome — the decisive-advantage concept may be irrelevant in practice** — Even if a lab achieves a sharp capability threshold, converting it into geopolitical leverage requires integrating that capability into military systems, training operators, updating doctrine, and deploying at scale — all of which take years. If diffusion to competitors occurs faster than the exploitation pipeline completes, the 'decisive' window never opens in practice. This is distinct from the 5–10 year diffusion base-rate note: it concerns the exploitation timeline relative to diffusion, not diffusion alone.  _(#79, r7)_
- **Exogenous natural triggers form a zero-deterrence class: all existing analysis is inapplicable** — The entire deterrence, credibility, and escalation architecture in the existing notes presupposes an adversary whose cost-benefit calculation can be shaped. Earthquakes, typhoons, pandemic-cluster events, and solar storms have no decision-maker to deter. Every chokepoint vulnerability identified (TSMC, cables, data centers) is equally exploitable by natural events, but none of the deterrence levers — coercive signaling, force posture, alliance credibility — applies. This is a structurally distinct tail class, not a variant of the existing scenario tree.  _(#82, r8)_
- **Carrington-class solar event is a geographically non-discriminating global compute/grid discontinuity that cannot be deterred, gamed, or asymmetrically exploited** — A geomagnetic storm comparable to 1859 (estimated ~1-in-100–150-year probability, so ~0.7–1% per decade) would simultaneously damage unshielded transformers, data-center power infrastructure, and satellite-dependent communication globally. Unlike every other scenario in this analysis, no actor gains relative advantage: all frontier AI development pauses together. The existing notes implicitly model all discontinuities as zero-sum between US and China; a Carrington event is the only scenario where both lose simultaneously at roughly equal rates, potentially resetting the competitive landscape to a joint-recovery dynamic rather than a contest.  _(#85, r8)_
- **Diversionary war theory: CCP action from legitimacy weakness/economic collapse is structurally opposite to the 'strategic window' mechanism in existing notes** — All existing notes treat CCP Taiwan action as a calculated exploitation of strategic opportunity (US distraction, capability window, political transition). Classic diversionary war theory posits the opposite: leaders facing domestic legitimacy collapse seek external conflict to suppress internal dissent and channel nationalism. China's property-sector implosion, youth unemployment (21%+ officially in 2023), and demographic cliff create a distinct trigger mechanism. Crucially, this trigger is most likely when China is weakest — exactly the condition under which strategic-window logic would say China should not act — making the two triggers nearly observationally distinct only in hindsight.  _(#86, r8)_
- **'Window of weakness' is dual-valenced: CCP domestic collapse simultaneously elevates diversionary-aggression probability AND elevates deterrence effectiveness** — Existing notes treat 'windows' exclusively from a US/Western capability perspective (when does China gain decisive advantage). The dual-valence insight is that CCP domestic economic collapse creates two opposing forces simultaneously: elevated incentive to act (diversionary logic) and elevated susceptibility to cost-imposition (China less able to absorb a protracted conflict or economic sanctions). A collapsing China is therefore both more dangerous and more deterrable than a stable China — making the policy response to rising Chinese economic stress analytically ambiguous in ways the existing framework does not surface.  _(#92, r8)_
- **Algorithmic efficiency improvement perpetually re-calibrates export-control thresholds downward, making hardware-based chokepoints structurally self-obsoleting** — Each DeepSeek-class efficiency breakthrough (MLA, MoE, distillation improvements) lowers the compute required to reach a given capability level, meaning the H100/A100 restriction boundary is perpetually chasing a moving target. The governance architecture assumes a stable capability-per-FLOP curve; efficiency gains systematically violate that assumption without requiring any hardware above the control threshold.  _(#94, r9)_
- **Open-weight release is a one-way irreversibility event that categorically differs from every other proliferation pathway in these scenarios** — Once frontier-class weights are publicly released (e.g., Llama, Mistral, future open-weight releases approaching GPT-4/5 class), no subsequent governance intervention — nationalization, export control, FFRDC conversion, liability regime — can un-proliferate them. This creates a permanent, jurisdiction-independent capability floor that bypasses every existing discontinuity-management mechanism simultaneously.  _(#96, r9)_
- **Distillation cascade creates a closed-to-open capability bleed that makes API access a de facto export of near-frontier capability** — Frontier closed models accessible via API can be systematically distilled into open-weight successors — models trained on outputs of GPT-4/5-class systems inherit substantial capability without requiring the training compute. This means any export-control regime that restricts hardware but permits API access creates a diffusion pathway that converts closed-model advantage into the open-weight ecosystem, independent of direct chip access.  _(#98, r9)_
- **Algorithmic-efficiency discoveries diffuse globally in weeks; this is categorically faster than hardware-dependent capability jumps, making efficiency shocks ungovernable by any policy operating on regulatory timescales** — A transformer-architecture-class efficiency breakthrough can be published, reproduced, and deployed globally within weeks of release. Hardware-dependent capability advances require months to years to diffuse (fab capacity, chip delivery, integration). This asymmetry means that efficiency-based capability equalization outruns every governance intervention designed for the slower hardware-diffusion timescale — the policy window simply does not exist.  _(#102, r9)_

## Hypotheses (23)

- **Safety catastrophe clampdown is most likely triggered by a single high-salience misuse event, not gradual risk accumulation** — Regulatory regime breaks historically occur after acute, visible failures (Thalidomide → drug trials, Chernobyl → nuclear moratorium, 9/11 → aviation security). A mass-casualty bioweapon synthesis, critical infrastructure attack, or large-scale AI-enabled fraud attributed to a specific model could trigger emergency global coordination that incremental risk accumulation never would.  _(#6, r1)_
- **The safety-catastrophe and decisive-advantage scenarios are anti-correlated: a convincing capability demonstration accelerates both racing and safety concern simultaneously** — A GPT-4-level public jump (or larger) both raises the probability of a safety-driven clampdown push AND raises the competitive pressure that makes clampdown politically infeasible. This creates a structural tension where the very events that make safety regulation most warranted are the ones that most undermine its political viability—a 'capability trap.'  _(#12, r1)_
- **Asymmetric safety regulation could paradoxically narrow the Western AI lead** — A safety-catastrophe clampdown applied under democratic rule-of-law primarily constrains US/EU frontier labs while leaving Chinese state-directed AI development unaffected. The more stringent and unilateral the democratic clampdown, the more it functions as competitive displacement — accelerating relative Chinese capability even as it slows absolute risk.  _(#17, r2)_
- **Military AI delegation failure is a more plausible safety-catastrophe trigger than consumer misuse** — The existing trigger hypothesis focuses on a 'high-salience misuse event.' A distinct and arguably more likely trigger is an AI system acting against operator intent in a live military or intelligence context — a missile strike on wrong targets, an autonomous escalation — producing mass casualties and a crisis of legitimacy rather than a consumer harm event. The political salience is immediate and the attribution is unambiguous.  _(#18, r2)_
- **Information asymmetry under racing conditions creates miscalculation risk about who has decisive advantage** — During an intense AI capability race — especially under conflict conditions that degrade intelligence — actors may hold false beliefs about whether a rival has already achieved decisive advantage. This could trigger preemptive military or economic action against a rival that does not yet have the advantage (or against one that already does). The risk is not from the advantage itself but from ambiguity about it.  _(#21, r2)_
- **Taiwan naval blockade is a distinct discontinuity pathway distinct from kinetic invasion** — China could impose a sustained naval and air blockade of Taiwan — disrupting TSMC's supply chains (chemicals, gases, equipment parts) and export routes without a full land/amphibious invasion. This would degrade fab output over months, fall below the threshold of triggering formal alliance responses, and not require China to risk destroying assets it intends to keep. It is a structurally different scenario than either seizure or kinetic destruction.  _(#27, r3)_
- **Decisive capability advantage increases, not decreases, near-term safety catastrophe probability** — The power achieving decisive advantage is under pressure to deploy rapidly at scale — military, economic, and political — before the gap closes (see first-strike instability). Rapid deployment at scale outpaces safety validation, creating new failure modes (hallucinated military intelligence, autonomous weapons acting on stale models) that are higher-probability when the leader is racing to extract value. Decisive advantage and safety catastrophe may be positively, not negatively, correlated.  _(#29, r3)_
- **Private ordering via liability and insurance could impose effective safety floors independent of government** — Product liability exposure, D&O insurance underwriting requirements, and institutional investor ESG mandates can create binding safety constraints on AI developers without legislative action. If a high-salience AI harm produces successful tort litigation, insurers repricing coverage and investors repricing risk could impose safety standards faster and more durably than a regulatory clampdown that requires cross-jurisdictional coordination.  _(#34, r3)_
- **Allied chokepoint holders (Japan, South Korea, Netherlands) gain durable structural leverage that rebalances US alliance architecture** — Post-disruption, TSMC's JASM fabs in Kumamoto, Samsung in Pyeongtaek, and ASML in Veldhoven become existential dependencies for US AI and defense programs. This transforms these countries from security consumers (sheltered by US deterrence) into irreplaceable suppliers with genuine veto power over US industrial policy — a structural shift that would persist across administrations and outlast any particular crisis.  _(#38, r4)_
- **Credible Taiwan disruption threat is the most effective known force for supply-chain diversification — creating a self-limiting dynamic** — Voluntary coordination to reshore semiconductors was economically irrational under peacetime conditions; only the credible threat of a Taiwan crisis generated the political will for CHIPS Act and EU Chips Act spending at scale. If this reshoring succeeds, it gradually reduces the discontinuity magnitude of an actual disruption. The threat is thus self-limiting: the more credible and sustained it is, the lower the eventual impact — provided the conflict window is later than the fab build timeline.  _(#43, r4)_
- **High-altitude EMP is a plausible sub-nuclear coercive tool that degrades AI/grid infrastructure below perceived escalation threshold** — A high-altitude nuclear detonation (~400 km altitude over the Pacific) creates catastrophic electromagnetic pulse effects across continental-scale geography without direct territorial attack or immediate casualties — potentially below the threshold China calculates would trigger nuclear retaliation. It would simultaneously destroy AI compute clusters, semiconductor fabrication control systems, and power grids. China may miscalculate Western red lines on this option precisely because no precedent exists.  _(#51, r5)_
- **Undersea cable severance is a pre-kinetic escalation rung that degrades AI and financial infrastructure without triggering Article 5** — China and Russia have mapped and pre-positioned assets adjacent to the ~400 cables carrying ~95% of intercontinental internet traffic. Selective cable cutting can accompany a Taiwan blockade as gray-zone coercion — disrupting AI inference, financial settlement, and military logistics without a clearly attributable kinetic act. The legal and treaty ambiguity (cables are not territory) means it may not trigger collective defense responses, creating a distinct coercion window.  _(#52, r5)_
- **China may open horizontal secondary fronts not to win them but to fracture US alliance coherence and overextend force deployment** — Horizontal escalation strategy — simultaneously activating Senkaku pressure, South China Sea incidents, and North Korean provocation — imposes on the US a simultaneous multi-front posture that existing force-structure models show as unmanageable. The aim is not to win secondary theaters but to force US allies to prioritize their own perimeters over Taiwan commitment. Existing notes cover allied leverage on semiconductors but not on the military basing and logistics dependencies that horizontal escalation attacks.  _(#53, r5)_
- **Wartime cyberattacks targeting AI model weights and training infrastructure could degrade leading AI advantage faster than physical semiconductor disruption** — A great-power conflict would include offensive cyber operations against the adversary's AI stack — poisoning training datasets, corrupting deployed model weights, destroying RLHF pipelines, and disabling inference clusters. Unlike physical fab destruction (which takes years to rebuild), logical destruction of AI systems could be executed in days and is harder to attribute and deter. This creates an interaction between the decisive-capability-advantage and great-power-war pillars not captured by the compute-hardware framing of existing notes.  _(#56, r5)_
- **Winner's curse: achieving decisive advantage maximally concentrates hostile regulatory, political, and adversary responses — creating a rational incentive to stay below the visible threshold** — A lab that demonstrably achieves decisive advantage simultaneously becomes the target of antitrust action, allied-country pressure, nationalization threat, adversary targeting, and safety-clampdown advocates. This convergence of hostile forces at the exact moment of 'winning' may create a rational pre-commitment to remain just below the threshold of visible decisiveness — a self-limiting dynamic inside the race that the existing notes do not model.  _(#61, r6)_
- **Hyperscaler layer likely captures the majority of economic rents from decisive advantage, not the frontier lab that produced it** — Hyperscalers hold prior equity stakes (Microsoft ~49% of OpenAI economics), provide the compute that is the binding cost input, and own the distribution channel (Azure AI, Google Cloud) through which capability is monetized. Even if a lab achieves a decisive capability lead, the structural bargaining position of the compute-and-distribution layer above it means rent extraction flows predominantly to the hyperscaler — analogous to how record labels historically captured artist revenue, or how app stores captured developer surplus.  _(#62, r6)_
- **Antitrust mandatory-access orders are a slow-motion expropriation mechanism that achieves functional rent-transfer without the political cost of formal nationalization** — Forcing a decisive-advantage lab to provide API access at regulated prices, or to divest compute from model development, achieves functional socialization of the capability without formally nationalizing it — similar to how telecom unbundling or railroad common-carrier rules transferred rents from incumbents to users and competitors. This is a plausible US or EU equilibrium that sits between 'private lab' and 'nationalized asset' and has distinct implications for who captures rents.  _(#66, r6)_
- **Energy infrastructure commandeering is a more legally tractable and less disruptive nationalization lever than direct lab expropriation** — Under the Federal Power Act and DPA Title I, the US government can allocate grid capacity, data-center power permits, or cooling-water rights under existing emergency authorities — effectively controlling which AI workloads run without touching the lab's corporate structure or triggering talent flight. This 'soft nationalization via energy bottleneck' is legally cleaner, politically more defensible, and achieves directional control of decisive-advantage compute while avoiding the information-asset commandeering problem entirely.  _(#69, r6)_
- **Xi succession or incapacitation creates maximum-variance outcome precisely because the CCP concentrates Taiwan decisions in a single principal** — The existing notes treat CCP political economy as a decisive advantage (faster exploitation, no principal-agent delay). This same concentration creates catastrophic single-point-of-failure risk if that principal is removed unexpectedly. A successor proving toughness (hawkish acceleration) and a collective-leadership reversion to committee paralysis (risk-averse delay) are structurally opposite outcomes — both plausible from the same triggering event. Unlike US presidential succession (clear constitutional transfer, continuity doctrine for nuclear posture), CCP succession lacks publicly known protocols, creating a maximum-uncertainty window.  _(#88, r8)_
- **US domestic constitutional or political crisis during a Taiwan flash-point is a pure credibility failure that military posture cannot hedge** — A disputed US election outcome, prolonged governmental paralysis, or debt-ceiling-driven defense-appropriations crisis coinciding with a Taiwan contingency would degrade alliance credibility without any degradation of hardware capability. The deterrence architecture assumes the US government can make and keep commitments; a scenario where the US president cannot obtain congressional authorization or where executive authority is disputed removes the political will to act while leaving military capability intact. This is a qualitatively different failure mode from all capability-based gaps in the existing analysis.  _(#89, r8)_
- **Multi-theater simultaneous aggression is an explicit adversary coordination strategy, not merely a US-distraction side-effect** — Russia-China-DPRK trilateral military alignment, cemented post-2022 (joint exercises, munitions transfers, diplomatic cover), creates for the first time a scenario in which simultaneous aggression across three theaters is militarily available and plausibly coordinated. The existing note treats great-power war as a 'scenario amplifier'; this hypothesis is stronger: adversaries may deliberately time Taiwan action to coincide with a Russian NATO-border push and a North Korean provocation, calculated to exhaust US logistics, ISR, and political bandwidth simultaneously. This is a deliberate strategic design, not an opportunistic coincidence.  _(#90, r8)_
- **A natural-disaster TSMC disruption — unlike war — creates a governance coordination window where US, China, and Japan have aligned interests in restoration** — All intentional-action scenarios (invasion, blockade, EMP) place US and China as adversaries, making coordination on semiconductor recovery politically impossible. A major Taiwan earthquake or drought-induced production collapse would give all major consumers — including China, which depends on TSMC-produced chips for its own AI and consumer electronics industries — shared interest in rapid restoration. This is the only identified scenario in which a joint US-China-Japan semiconductor-governance regime becomes politically feasible, and could serve as the coordination experiment that builds the institutional infrastructure for future arms-control analogues.  _(#93, r8)_
- **An open-weight capability floor caps the strategic convertibility of decisive advantage: if adversary and non-state actors can reach 80–90% capability for free, the marginal strategic value of the last 10–20% shrinks toward zero** — Decisive advantage is strategically meaningful only if it confers capabilities the adversary cannot replicate or approximate. An open-weight floor means the advantage must be maintained in the narrow band between the open frontier and the true frontier — a band that compression dynamics (efficiency gains, distillation) continuously narrow. This puts decisive advantage on a permanent treadmill rather than a stable strategic plateau, undermining the nuclear-moment analogy.  _(#103, r9)_

## Supporting evidence (12)

- **Compute concentration in a handful of data-center clusters makes AI advantage structurally harder to diffuse than prior tech** — Unlike nuclear blueprints or software code, frontier AI training requires ~$100M–$1B compute runs on hardware that must be physically manufactured and shipped. TSMC fab capacity, NVIDIA H100/H200 allocations, and export controls create a physical chokepoint with no historical analogue. Algorithmic diffusion is fast; hardware diffusion is slow and governable.  _(#5, r1)_
- **Taiwan invasion would not destroy TSMC fabs — seizure is the coherent Chinese strategy, creating a different discontinuity** — A PLA strategy targeting TSMC seizure intact (rather than destruction) would give Beijing control of the world's leading-edge fab capacity. This is a different regime break than 'supply destroyed': it means a decisive hardware advantage accrues to China rather than a bilateral destruction of semiconductor capacity. The West's response (sanctions, fab evacuation, TSMC scorched-earth protocol) determines which outcome materializes.  _(#10, r1)_
- **Taiwan disruption is a macro-financial discontinuity, not just a supply-chain disruption** — TSMC produces ~90% of logic chips below 7nm; credible estimates place the GDP impact of a sustained disruption at $1–3T annually. At that scale the shock is not a sectoral supply event but a systemic macro-financial rupture comparable to a global credit seizure — affecting equity valuations, sovereign debt servicing (for tech-exposed economies), and central-bank frameworks simultaneously, independent of the military outcome.  _(#35, r4)_
- **South Korea (Samsung/SK Hynix) is the primary near-term value-capture winner from TSMC disruption** — Samsung's foundry and SK Hynix's HBM memory are the only non-TSMC sources with anything near leading-edge capacity. A Taiwan shock would flood both with orders they cannot absorb but would price at monopoly rents. This gives Seoul a paradoxical financial interest in the crisis it also fears militarily, creating complex and potentially exploitable fissures in the US-South Korea alliance posture.  _(#37, r4)_
- **Japan's JASM investment reveals state-level pricing of Taiwan disruption risk at a very high probability** — Japan has committed billions in subsidies to attract TSMC's Kumamoto fab (JASM Phase 1 and planned Phase 2), despite the 2–3× cost premium over Taiwanese production. This is rational only if Japan's government assigns a high expected-value loss to Taiwan dependency. It is thus revealed-preference evidence — already observable, not conjectural — that at least one major ally treats Taiwan disruption as sufficiently probable to justify enormous economic insurance premiums.  _(#42, r4)_
- **US/NATO military readiness directly depends on commercial semiconductor supply, creating a strategic feedback loop** — Modern US defense systems — F-35 avionics, Excalibur munitions, JASSM guidance, C4ISR infrastructure — rely on commercial leading-edge chips sourced from TSMC and East Asian supply chains. A Taiwan disruption would degrade the very military capacity needed to deter or respond to further Chinese aggression, simultaneous with the crisis that caused the degradation. This feedback loop transforms the military scenarios: the adversary that triggers the disruption gains a derivative military advantage through the supply shock itself.  _(#45, r4)_
- **Hyperscalers are already partially quasi-nationalized via existing classified government cloud contracts** — Azure Government (IL5/IL6), AWS GovCloud (Secret/TS-SCI), and Google's classified cloud infrastructure operate under security agreements and access obligations that give the US government indirect capability leverage today — before any emergency action. This revealed partial quasi-nationalization means the de facto expropriation surface already exists and the question is only one of scope expansion, not creation.  _(#63, r6)_
- **CCP legal architecture enables operational direction of AI labs without formal nationalization — structurally faster than any democratic expropriation process** — China's National Security Law (Art. 7 duty to support state intelligence), mandatory Party committee presence inside private tech firms, and the demonstrated forced reversals of Didi, Ant Group, and Tencent show that the CCP can direct AI capability within weeks via internal Party apparatus. This is operationally equivalent to nationalization without the legal friction, and is already operative for Baidu, Huawei, and ByteDance — creating an asymmetric expropriation speed advantage that is absent from the existing notes.  _(#64, r6)_
- **China's structural AI deployment advantages — mandated data access, no consent friction, unified direction — may offset compute deficits for certain capability metrics** — China can compel data sharing across sectors, deploy AI systems in government and military applications without democratic consent processes, and direct academic and corporate AI work centrally. For capability metrics that depend on deployment scale, behavioural data breadth, or rapid integration into state systems, these structural advantages may partially offset the compute and algorithmic gaps that Western chokepoints are designed to maintain.  _(#76, r7)_
- **CCP political economy eliminates the reveal/conceal principal-agent problem, enabling faster exploitation of decisive advantage** — The existing reveal/conceal crux is a Western/private-lab problem: labs fear that disclosing decisive capability triggers nationalisation. Chinese frontier labs face no analogous dilemma — the CCP can direct disclosure and operationalisation without the lab's consent, and there is no private equity interest to protect. This asymmetry means a Chinese lab achieving threshold capability can be exploited faster and more completely by the state than any Western analog, compressing the exploitation window for whoever moves second.  _(#77, r7)_
- **Taiwan seismic risk is quantified, historically demonstrated, and systematically underpriced in supply-chain models** — The 1999 M7.6 Chi-Chi earthquake damaged fabs, disrupted DRAM supply, and caused a global semiconductor price spike. TSMC's Hsinchu Science Park sits within 15 km of active fault systems; paleoseismic data suggest recurrence intervals for M7+ events of 30–60 years, implying a non-trivial within-decade probability. Unlike invasion scenarios, seismic disruption is not deterrable, not preceded by intelligence-observable mobilisation, and not hedgeable through coercive diplomacy — it arrives without warning and damages fabs whether or not any military force is present.  _(#83, r8)_
- **Taiwan ultrapure-water dependency is a recurrent, climate-amplifying partial-disruption risk requiring no adversary** — TSMC's leading-edge nodes consume roughly 150,000 tonnes of ultrapure water per day. The 2020–21 drought forced Taiwan to drain reservoirs, idle agricultural users, and implement emergency trucking — fabs operated but at elevated cost and political stress. Climate projections increase drought frequency and severity in the western Taiwan plains. This creates a spectrum of partial-disruption events (10–40% capacity reduction for 3–12 months) that sit below the threshold of a full discontinuity but above the threshold of normal supply-chain buffering, and that repeat with increasing frequency independent of any geopolitical decision.  _(#84, r8)_

## Disconfirming evidence (20)

- **Historical base-rate: major military-relevant technologies diffuse within 5–10 years** — The USSR replicated the atomic bomb in 4 years; ballistic missiles, stealth, and GPS-guided munitions diffused rapidly despite classification efforts. Open-source model weights (Llama, Mistral) suggest frontier AI follows a similar pattern. If diffusion is the historical norm, a durable decisive advantage is an outlier requiring extraordinary barriers.  _(#4, r1)_
- **Coordination failure makes a durable safety clampdown extremely unlikely post-shock** — Even after Chernobyl, the USSR and later Russia continued civilian nuclear expansion. After COVID, no durable biosecurity regime emerged. Competitive dynamics ensure that any actor restrained by a safety clampdown is undercut by a defector. Unless the triggering event is so severe it destroys the defecting state's capacity (not just willingness), the clampdown decays rapidly.  _(#7, r1)_
- **Seized TSMC fabs may not function without continuous foreign inputs** — Leading-edge fabrication requires uninterrupted supply of ASML EUV machines (Dutch, undeliverable under sanctions), ultra-pure specialty gases, and institutional process knowledge held by engineers who could flee or be evacuated. Seizure of physical structures may not deliver the capability; China would inherit shells, not a functioning foundry.  _(#13, r2)_
- **The nuclear-moment analogy breaks on the demonstration problem** — Nuclear decisive advantage was made legible by a physical, undeniable event (Trinity, Hiroshima). AI decisive advantage is opaque: demonstrating it reveals the capability to rivals and invites countermeasures, while withholding the demonstration means geopolitical effects may never materialize. The 'nuclear moment' structure may not apply if the advantage cannot be credibly communicated.  _(#14, r2)_
- **Coordination failure on safety clampdown does not apply to unilateral autocratic actors** — The coordination failure note implicitly assumes a multi-actor game requiring joint action (democratic governments, industry, international bodies). China or another autocratic state can implement a full domestic clampdown on AI deployment unilaterally with no coordination problem. A safety catastrophe that triggers decisive state action in an autocracy bypasses the diffuse-veto problem entirely.  _(#19, r2)_
- **Non-kinetic talent extraction is an under-priced alternative to Taiwan invasion** — China has sustained long-running programs to recruit semiconductor engineers from Taiwan and South Korea through third-country job offers and academic partnerships (e.g. documented SMIC hiring of TSMC talent). Successful talent extraction could transfer leading-edge process knowledge without triggering the kinetic shock scenario at all — a slower but lower-risk route to fab capability that makes the Taiwan military option less necessary.  _(#22, r2)_
- **AI harm's causal diffuseness makes legally actionable safety triggers structurally harder than nuclear/bio precedents** — Unlike a nuclear detonation or a bioweapon release, AI-caused harms are typically probabilistic, multi-causal, and contested in attribution (did the model cause this, or the operator, or the user?). This structural ambiguity means even a high-salience event may not produce the clean political trigger needed for decisive regulatory action — legal and political actors will dispute causation, delaying or diluting the response.  _(#24, r2)_
- **ASML EUV monopoly is a tighter chokepoint than TSMC itself** — Even a successfully seized TSMC cannot replicate leading-edge process nodes without ASML's extreme-ultraviolet lithography machines, which are already export-controlled and cannot be serviced or updated without Dutch/US cooperation. The fab buildings are less valuable than the tool supply chain behind them, which is a harder target to seize or replicate.  _(#25, r3)_
- **Leader's revealed architectural choices accelerate follower catch-up beyond base-rate diffusion** — When the frontier power publicly demonstrates what works (transformer scaling, RLHF, specific hardware configurations), it eliminates years of dead-end exploration for followers. The diffusion time is therefore shorter than historical 5–10 year base rates for opaque technologies — followers need to replicate a known target, not discover it. This is a structural argument that decisive advantage windows are narrower than the base-rate note implies.  _(#28, r3)_
- **Post-catastrophe emergency mobilization may accelerate capability rather than clamp it** — Historical precedent (Manhattan Project, Apollo, wartime radar) shows that high-salience catastrophes can trigger emergency government investment and coordination that surpasses what organic private development would have achieved. A major AI-linked catastrophe might produce the same effect: massive state resources, suspended normal regulatory review, cleared procurement pathways — net result is faster capability development, not slower.  _(#31, r3)_
- **EDA tool and chip-design IP concentration is a second, independent US chokepoint** — Cadence and Synopsys jointly supply the vast majority of electronic design automation tools required to design advanced semiconductors; both are US companies subject to export controls. Even if China bypasses TSMC via its own fabs, and even if ASML controls are circumvented over time, China still cannot design competitive chips without access to leading EDA software. This creates a layered chokepoint structure that is more resilient than any single point.  _(#33, r3)_
- **CHIPS Act / EU Chips Act reshoring timelines peak after the conflict risk window, not before** — New greenfield fabs require 4–7 years from groundbreaking to volume production at leading-edge nodes. The Taiwan conflict window most analysts place at 2027–2030. TSMC Arizona N4 volume production began only in late 2024; Intel's Ohio fabs and subsequent nodes slip further. The insurance policy matures after the insured risk is highest — a structural timing mismatch that undermines reshoring as a near-term deterrent.  _(#39, r4)_
- **Persistent 2–3× cost differential caps reshored capacity regardless of subsidy levels** — Structural labor, land, permitting, and regulatory costs in the US and EU mean leading-edge fab construction and operation costs 2–3× the East Asian equivalent even after CHIPS Act grants. Subsidies cover upfront capital but not ongoing operating cost gaps. This structural disadvantage limits the scale of economically viable reshored capacity, meaning the 'resilience dividend' is capped well below full supply substitution even in the most optimistic CHIPS Act outcome.  _(#44, r4)_
- **Alliance fragmentation under nuclear shadow may deny US the basing and logistics needed to contest a Taiwan blockade** — Japan (Article 9 constraints, public pacifism), South Korea (focused on North Korea, economic exposure to China), and Philippines (domestic political opposition) may refuse active participation or basing access under Chinese nuclear signaling even if they maintain political solidarity with the US. The existing allied-leverage note addresses semiconductor chokepoints but not the operational military consequence: loss of Okinawa/Kadena basing would reduce US air sortie generation by an estimated 70% in some wargame scenarios.  _(#54, r5)_
- **Verification is structurally asymmetric: democratic openness vs. authoritarian opacity undermines 'mutual' monitoring** — Any monitoring regime is far more enforceable against open societies with free press, commercial cloud providers, and legislative oversight than against authoritarian systems with state-controlled data and closed infrastructure. A nominally symmetric accord would in practice impose real constraints on Western labs while leaving Chinese compliance largely unverifiable. This asymmetry is a specific structural mechanism — distinct from generic coordination failure — that any cooperative-regime forecast must price.  _(#73, r7)_
- **Compute scaling may plateau before any strategically decisive capability threshold is reached, invalidating the discontinuity framework** — If current AI architectures hit diminishing returns on scaling before crossing any 'nuclear moment' threshold — due to algorithmic bottlenecks, data scarcity, inference-cost physics, or emergent capability ceilings — then no decisive-advantage event occurs, no safety-catastrophe trigger fires, and the entire discontinuity frame fails. This is the 'no threshold exists' version of skepticism, distinct from 'threshold exists but diffuses quickly.'  _(#80, r7)_
- **North Korea's autonomous nuclear capability creates a second, independent escalation ladder that cannot be subordinated to US-China deterrence logic** — Every existing nuclear-escalation note treats the US-China dyad as the governing structure. North Korea possesses demonstrated nuclear delivery capability and decision-making entirely outside Chinese direction — Kim Jong-un has historically acted contrary to Chinese preferences. A Taiwan-conflict-triggered Korean Peninsula exchange could produce first nuclear use by a third party, creating facts on the ground (radiation, refugee crisis, Japanese territory potentially targeted) that force US escalation responses independent of Taiwan calculus. This is a genuine second nuclear escalation ladder that all existing deterrence architecture implicitly ignores.  _(#91, r8)_
- **Export controls on leading-edge chips endogenously incentivize exactly the research strategy most capable of circumventing them** — Restricting access to H100-class chips raises the marginal return on algorithmic efficiency research relative to brute-force scaling — creating precisely the economic incentive structure that drives adversary labs toward DeepSeek-style efficiency-first approaches. The control regime partially selects for its own defeater, a perverse dynamic absent from all standard export-control effectiveness analyses.  _(#95, r9)_
- **Open-weight proliferation establishes a multi-actor capability floor that breaks the bilateral US–China decisive-advantage framing entirely** — If open-weight models reach near-frontier capability, decisive advantage becomes a question not of US vs. China but of who is marginally ahead of a globally accessible baseline that includes non-state actors, mid-tier states, and criminal organizations. The entire strategic framework of bilateral decisive advantage — arms control, stability analysis, hegemonic stabilization — is calibrated to a two-actor competition that open proliferation structurally dissolves.  _(#97, r9)_
- **Open-weight proliferation creates a safety-catastrophe pathway that is structurally inaccessible to every governance architecture calibrated to closed-model labs** — FFRDC conversion, nationalization, liability regimes, hyperscaler commandeering, and lab-level export controls all operate on the assumption that capability resides in a governable entity. Open-weight releases move capability to a distributed, jurisdictionally fragmented ecosystem where no single actor can be held accountable, no weights can be recalled, and no compute restriction applies post-release. The safety-clampdown scenario presupposes a governable lab; it is inapplicable to the open-weight proliferation vector.  _(#101, r9)_

## Cruxes (17)

- **Crux: Does economic interdependence constrain Xi, or does strategic imperative override it?** — If CCP leadership weights national-unification as a terminal goal that dominates economic costs, the silicon shield deters nothing. If leadership is primarily motivated by regime stability and economic performance, the cost of triggering a global semiconductor collapse is prohibitive. Everything about Taiwan timelines pivots on which utility function governs Beijing's decision.  _(#2, r1)_
- **Crux: Is the decisive-advantage threshold reachable before the Taiwan/great-power conflict window closes?** — Xi has stated a 2027 PLA readiness target; many analysts place Taiwan risk elevated 2027–2032. If transformative AI arrives before that window, the geopolitical calculus changes entirely (deterrence by AI-enabled defense, or acceleration of conflict). If the conflict window precedes the AI threshold, the two scenarios interact: a Taiwan shock could reset compute access and shift which actor crosses the threshold first.  _(#8, r1)_
- **Crux: Can TSMC's leading-edge process knowledge be credibly threatened with destruction (scorched-earth) as a deterrent?** — If Taiwan and/or the US can credibly commit to destroying TSMC fab capacity (equipment, process IP, key engineers) before Chinese seizure, the strategic calculus changes—seizure is no longer profitable. This deters PLA seizure strategies but potentially invites a 'destroy rather than capture' logic. Whether a credible scorched-earth commitment can be established and communicated without triggering preemptive action is the key question.  _(#11, r1)_
- **Crux: Can export controls on advanced chips be enforced durably, or does leakage via third-country routing erode them?** — The compute-concentration advantage rests on the assumption that BIS controls hold. Post-2022 Russia sanctions demonstrated significant leakage via UAE, Turkey, and other intermediaries. If similar leakage scales to AI chips, the US advantage in compute concentration is a temporary artifact of enforcement, not a structural feature.  _(#15, r2)_
- **Crux: Does order of discontinuities matter — does Taiwan precede or follow decisive-advantage threshold?** — If kinetic conflict over Taiwan occurs before decisive AI advantage is achieved, the resulting disruption to chip supply and global economy may prevent any actor from reaching that threshold during the critical window. If it occurs after, the possessor of decisive advantage enters the conflict with a potentially war-determining asymmetry. These two orderings produce qualitatively different outcomes and the sequencing is not addressed by existing notes.  _(#20, r2)_
- **Crux: Has Taiwan credibly pre-committed to fab demolition via verifiable technical means?** — The existing crux asks whether scorched-earth can be credibly threatened, but the operative question is whether Taiwan has already installed verifiable pre-commitment technology (physical demolition charges, remote-kill switches observable to adversaries) that shifts the credibility problem from intention to capability. If such systems exist and are known to China, the deterrent functions. If they are merely declared policy, they are likely discounted.  _(#32, r3)_
- **Crux: Does China sufficiently price its own advanced-semiconductor dependency on TSMC-produced chips?** — China's own AI hardware, 5G infrastructure, and consumer electronics sectors run largely on TSMC-manufactured chips that cannot yet be replaced by SMIC. A kinetic Taiwan action would be an act of economic self-immolation for China's own tech ambitions. Whether Xi's leadership treats this self-harm as tolerable, or whether it functions as a genuine deterrent, is a crux that distinguishes scenarios of coercion vs. invasion.  _(#40, r4)_
- **Crux: Is the disruption concentrated in leading-edge AI chips or does it cascade broadly into commodity logic?** — If a Taiwan disruption primarily affects <5nm logic (AI training, advanced defense systems), the direct AI-development impact is acute but the general economy impact is bounded. If it cascades into mature-node production (automotive, industrial, consumer) — which TSMC also dominates — the macro-economic magnitude is an order of magnitude higher but the AI-specific effect may be diluted by non-AI demand destruction. Which half of this crux is true determines whether a Taiwan shock primarily pauses AI development or primarily collapses industrial economies.  _(#46, r4)_
- **China's 'no first use' pledge is the load-bearing assumption that breaks under conventional defeat** — Every current Taiwan deterrence model treats Chinese NFU as a durable constraint. But if PLA conventional forces face imminent catastrophic defeat (fleet destroyed, landing force annihilated), the political cost of conventional humiliation may exceed the cost of nuclear first use against military targets — especially if Xi's domestic legitimacy depends on Taiwan 'success'. Whether PLA Second Artillery / Rocket Force has internal authority to override civilian NFU in extremis is unknown and unverifiable from open sources.  _(#48, r5)_
- **Crux: Can nuclear de-escalation signals be reliably communicated in an AI-mediated disinformation environment?** — Traditional nuclear crisis management depends on legible state-to-state communication (hotlines, official statements, signaling through military posture). If the information environment in a 2027-2030 conflict is saturated with AI-generated deepfakes, adversarial jamming, and synthetic media, the receiving state cannot verify whether a de-escalation signal is genuine or adversarial. This makes the established playbook for managing nuclear crises structurally unreliable in the AI era — a crux distinct from all existing cruxes.  _(#57, r5)_
- **Crux: Is decisive advantage bottlenecked on compute (commandeerable) or on model weights/architecture (hard to commandeer)?** — If the binding constraint on decisive advantage is raw compute (FLOPs, clusters), the state has a tractable expropriation lever via data-center infrastructure. If the bottleneck is architectural insight embedded in weights and researcher knowledge, commandeering compute gets the state a shell without the capability. This crux determines whether state capture is effective or illusory — and is entirely unaddressed in the existing note graph.  _(#67, r6)_
- **Crux: Reveal/conceal game — does the lab that achieves decisive advantage disclose it to government, triggering nationalization risk, or conceal it to preserve private control?** — A lab that privately achieves decisive advantage faces a non-trivial strategic choice: reveal to government (triggering commandeering risk, regulation, and forced sharing) vs. conceal (maintaining private control but losing government protection and potentially violating classification obligations on dual-use systems). The equilibrium of this game determines whether government expropriation happens at all, and on whose timeline — a structurally novel reveal-game that has no parallel in semiconductor or nuclear analogies.  _(#68, r6)_
- **Bilateral US–China accord vs. multilateral IAEA-style body: architecture is the load-bearing design variable for regime durability** — An N-party multilateral regime requires solving the full coordination problem and is easily blocked by one defector. A bilateral US–China accord — modelled on INF rather than NPT — requires only two consenting parties and can cover the bulk of frontier capability. The crux is whether effective AI governance demands universal participation (which likely fails) or whether bilateral coverage of the two dominant actors achieves most of the risk-reduction benefit.  _(#72, r7)_
- **Crux: Is China's actual frontier capability level verifiable by external inspectors — the load-bearing condition for any arms-control regime?** — An arms-control regime requires both parties to have sufficient confidence about where the other stands to make compliance credible. If China's frontier capability is opaque even to treaty inspectors — due to closed infrastructure, state secrecy, and the difficulty of distinguishing research from deployment runs — the verification foundation collapses regardless of treaty text. This crux is distinct from the export-controls enforcement crux: it concerns ongoing capability monitoring, not hardware flow.  _(#81, r7)_
- **CCP aggression from strength vs. from weakness is observationally similar ex ante but has opposite deterrence implications** — Deterrence theory assumes the aggressor is calculating costs and benefits rationally from a position of choice. Diversionary aggression under legitimacy collapse is semi-irrational from a national-interest standpoint — the expected military costs may exceed the strategic benefit, but the domestic-political calculus overrides it. External analysts using capability indicators, economic readiness, and alliance posture as predictors of Chinese intent will systematically misread a weakness-driven trigger as a non-threatening period (economic stress = less capable adversary) while missing the elevated political pressure to act. No existing crux distinguishes these two trigger classes.  _(#87, r8)_
- **Crux: Does decisive capability advantage require leading-edge training compute specifically, or can algorithmic efficiency achieve equivalent capability at export-controlled hardware levels?** — The entire export-control architecture rests on the assumption that frontier capability is inseparable from frontier hardware. If efficiency gains mean a 10× smaller compute cluster achieves 90% of the capability, the compute chokepoint fails as both a decisive-advantage moat and a governance verification mechanism. This is distinct from the existing crux about compute vs. weights — it asks whether the compute bottleneck itself can be circumvented algorithmically.  _(#99, r9)_
- **Crux: Does algorithmic efficiency itself plateau before reaching a regime where restricted hardware achieves decisive-class capability?** — If efficiency gains are subject to their own diminishing returns (analogous to the compute-scaling plateau noted in disconfirming evidence), compute concentration re-emerges as the binding constraint and the hardware chokepoint partially recovers its validity. The entire open-weight/efficiency-shock analysis hinges on whether the efficiency frontier is still far from its ceiling at the decisive-capability threshold.  _(#100, r9)_

## Historical analogies (60)

- **US oil embargo on Japan → Pearl Harbor, 1940–41** — The US cut off roughly 80% of Japan's oil supply (plus scrap metal and aviation fuel) in mid-1941, confronting Japan with a choice between strategic retreat or fighting before its reserves ran out. Japan chose the preemptive kinetic strike at Pearl Harbor, calculated to buy time to seize SE Asian oil fields and achieve autarky before US industrial mobilization could dominate. The US carriers were at sea; the war ended in Japan's total defeat and occupation four years later.
  - _Similar:_ TSMC represents ~90% of leading-edge logic chips — a concentration of critical industrial input comparable to Japan's oil dependency on US/Dutch sources; 'Use it or lose it' logic: the party facing the chokepoint has strong incentive to act preemptively rather than accept slow strangulation; The chokepoint was geographically concentrated and could not be quickly substituted (Japan had no domestic oil; most chip consumers have no alternative fab); The chokepoint holder believed the threshold for kinetic response was higher than it turned out to be
  - _Different:_ Japan was a declining relative power vs. a rising one; China is the opposite — rising, with time arguably on its side if willing to wait; Oil is consumable and irreplaceable in real-time; advanced chips can be stockpiled (Taiwan and allies are building reserves) and fabs can theoretically be rebuilt over 5–10 years; Nuclear deterrence did not exist in 1941; the modern Taiwan scenario operates under existential mutual risk that Japan-US did not face; Japan's decision was made by a small military council; China's is made in a different institutional context with more internal checks on pure military logic
  - _Implies:_ The chokepoint structure creates a 'use it or lose it' pressure that can override conventional deterrence. If China concluded that US chip controls would permanently cap its capability, the analog suggests kinetic action becomes more likely, not less — especially if the window of relative military advantage is perceived as closing.  _(#A1, r1)_
- **British naval blockade of Germany, WWI, 1914–18** — Britain's maritime dominance allowed it to cut Germany off from global trade in food and raw materials. Germany's industrial output degraded over four years; roughly 750,000 civilians died from malnutrition-related causes. Germany's asymmetric counter — unrestricted submarine warfare — sank neutral US shipping and triggered US entry into the war, decisively shifting the balance. Germany's internal collapse (November 1918) was as much economic as military.
  - _Similar:_ A dominant power uses logistics/infrastructure chokepoint control to degrade an adversary's industrial-military capacity over time; The targeted power cannot quickly substitute the choked input and escalates asymmetrically (U-boats then; China's potential asymmetric denial of TSMC outputs or undersea cable attacks now); Third-party nations are drawn in by collateral damage to their interests — as US entry in 1917 parallels potential ally mobilization in a Taiwan scenario; Chokepoint superiority is durable if the holder can defend it, but produces retaliatory escalation spirals
  - _Different:_ The blockade was attrition over four years; a Taiwan TSMC shock would be a near-instantaneous supply cliff, not a slow squeeze — inventory cycles matter enormously; Germany's blockade affected all goods; a TSMC shock is narrowly targeted at leading-edge compute, hitting defence/AI sectors first and consumer electronics with a lag; WWI blockade targeted a land power with no sea-denial capability; China has a credible anti-access/area-denial posture that complicates the maritime chokepoint analogy; Germany had prewar stockpiles and some domestic substitution; most chip consumers have minimal buffer inventory relative to design cycles
  - _Implies:_ Chokepoint wars are long and produce escalation dynamics the initiator does not control. The historical lesson is that the side holding the chokepoint has structural advantage but must survive the escalatory counter, and third-party entry is probable — which means a Taiwan kinetic shock is unlikely to remain a bilateral event.  _(#A2, r1)_
- **Manhattan Project → US atomic monopoly → Soviet Joe-1 test, 1945–49** — The US achieved a decisive capability advantage in nuclear weapons (Trinity, July 1945; Hiroshima/Nagasaki, August 1945). US planners expected the monopoly to last 7–10 years. Soviet Joe-1 was tested in August 1949 — just four years later — aided by espionage (Klaus Fuchs, Theodore Hall) and massive state prioritization. The regime broke from US monopoly to mutual assured destruction, restructuring all post-WWII geopolitics. The monopoly phase proved strategically useful but far shorter than assumed.
  - _Similar:_ A decisive, classified technical lead achieved through extraordinary state investment and secrecy — directly analogous to frontier AI capability advantage; Adversarial catch-up via espionage and state mobilization was faster than the leading power anticipated; The resulting equilibrium (MAD) was fundamentally different from and less favorable than the monopoly — capability advantage did not freeze into permanent dominance; A small number of defectors/spies (weights theft / insider exfiltration in AI terms) could compress the adversary's timeline by years
  - _Different:_ Nuclear weapons required scarce fissile material (physical scarcity); AI model weights can be copied, stolen, or independently derived without physical bottleneck; Nuclear manufacturing required massive, detectable industrial infrastructure; AI training can be distributed and hidden more easily; Nuclear use produced immediate, irreversible kinetic effects; AI capability advantage operates over a slower deployment curve with less clear-cut 'use'; The Soviet program required nearly replicating US physics knowledge from scratch; Chinese AI researchers are already at the frontier, making the gap narrower to close
  - _Implies:_ History strongly suggests decisive AI capability leads are shorter-lived than partisans claim. Espionage, state mobilization, and independent parallel development have always compressed the timeline. The more important question is what the post-parity equilibrium looks like — MAD was stable but created a 70-year crisis structure. An AI 'MAD' could be less stable.  _(#A3, r1)_
- **Three Mile Island + Chernobyl → Western nuclear moratorium, 1979–2000s** — Three Mile Island (March 1979) caused no direct deaths but triggered a wave of US regulatory tightening and cost escalation that effectively ended new nuclear plant orders. Chernobyl (April 1986) — 31 direct deaths, ~4,000 estimated additional cancers, 150,000 km² contaminated — caused Germany, Austria, Sweden and others to begin nuclear phase-outs. IAEA standards were overhauled. No new nuclear plants were ordered in the US for over 30 years. The nuclear industry's growth trajectory was permanently broken, even though actuarial risk per TWh remained lower than many alternatives.
  - _Similar:_ A single high-visibility catastrophe in a powerful technology triggered regulatory and political response far exceeding actuarial risk, because the imagery was visceral and public trust in operator assurances had been violated; The industry's prior safety claims ('too cheap to meter'; 'inherently safe') made the reversal more severe — overclaiming creates fragility; International regulatory fragmentation followed: different countries made radically different policy choices based more on political economy than physics; The technology did not die but entered a multi-decade suppression phase from which it is only now partially recovering
  - _Different:_ Nuclear accidents are physically visible (radiation, bodies, contaminated land that lasts decades); an AI safety catastrophe might be more diffuse, contested in attribution, and epistemically murky; Nuclear was already state-regulated utility infrastructure with clear regulatory bodies; AI development is primarily private/commercial with fragmented oversight; Nuclear's risk was concentrated (one plant, one site); AI failure modes are distributed and systemic, making 'shutting down the plant' less obviously available as a policy action; International coordination on nuclear was easier — fewer state actors with physical programs; AI capability is more widely distributed across the private sector globally
  - _Implies:_ A sufficiently visceral AI safety event (mass-casualty autonomous system failure, critical infrastructure attack credibly attributed to AI) could trigger a decade-long regulatory freeze regardless of its actuarial probability. The nuclear analog also shows that the freeze hits incumbents and newcomers unevenly — large incumbents survive moratoria; startup ecosystems collapse.  _(#A4, r1)_
- **Suez Crisis — canal nationalization, military intervention, superpower veto, 1956** — Nasser nationalized the Suez Canal (a critical global trade chokepoint) in July 1956. Britain, France, and Israel launched Operation Musketeer to retake it militarily. The US under Eisenhower refused to back the operation and threatened to sell sterling reserves, triggering a UK currency crisis. The UK was forced to stand down. Nasser retained the canal. The crisis marked the end of British imperial reach and demonstrated that a rising hegemon (US) could impose its preferred outcome on a critical chokepoint dispute through financial rather than kinetic coercion.
  - _Similar:_ A critical infrastructure chokepoint (Suez Canal → TSMC/Taiwan Strait) becomes the contested object in a great-power-adjacent conflict; A regional military operation against the chokepoint is vetoed by the dominant global power using financial/political rather than kinetic leverage; The chokepoint's strategic value is asymmetric: the side that controls it gains leverage over all parties dependent on the flow; Third-party nations (France, Israel in 1956; Japan, South Korea, Netherlands in a Taiwan scenario) have vital interests at stake but limited ability to determine the outcome
  - _Different:_ The Suez Canal could be operated by whoever physically controlled it; TSMC's value is in tacit human capital and proprietary process knowledge — conquest does not automatically transfer capability, and denial (scorching the fabs) is a credible threat; Nasser was the uncontested sovereign of Egypt; Taiwan's sovereignty status is explicitly contested, changing the legal and political calculus for third-party responses; In 1956, the US was veto-ing its own allies; in a Taiwan scenario, the US would be defending the chokepoint, not overruling the defender — the geometry is different; The US financial leverage over the UK (sterling dependence) was overwhelming; China's financial leverage over the US is real but bilateral, not as asymmetric
  - _Implies:_ Even military operations against critical chokepoints can be stopped by superpower financial/economic leverage, without direct military confrontation. The Suez analog suggests that the US retains potent non-kinetic tools — the key question is whether China's reduced dollar dependence has eroded those tools enough to change the calculus.  _(#A5, r1)_
- **COCOM — multilateral technology denial toward the Soviet Union, 1949–94** — The Coordinating Committee for Multilateral Export Controls (COCOM), established in 1949 by NATO members plus Japan and Australia, systematically denied advanced semiconductors, computers, and manufacturing equipment to the Soviet bloc. The USSR produced inferior clones of Western chips (the ES EVM series mimicked IBM 360/370 architecture) but never closed the gap in commercial electronics or sub-micron fabrication. The technology deficit became a significant factor in Soviet economic stagnation in the 1980s. However, the Soviets achieved near-parity in nuclear warheads and specific military applications through independent development and industrial espionage.
  - _Similar:_ Near-identical architecture to current US semiconductor export controls: multilateral controls targeting leading-edge compute/fabrication equipment to deny capability to a great-power adversary; The goal is to maintain a capability gap in frontier technology — then mainframes and minicomputers, now advanced logic chips and AI accelerators; China is explicitly attempting to build domestic alternatives (SMIC, Huawei HiSilicon) as the USSR built its domestic clone programs; Technology denial works asymmetrically: it slows commercial and AI capability more than it slows military-specific applications, which the adversary prioritizes

- **Human Genome Project vs. Celera Genomics — deliberate open-publication as a governance weapon against DSA monopoly, 1998–2001** — In 1998 Craig Venter's Celera Genomics announced it would sequence the human genome faster than the public Human Genome Project using whole-genome shotgun sequencing, intending to patent key sequences and sell database access. The Wellcome Trust and NIH, recognising an impending decisive information monopoly, made a strategic commitment: the 'Bermuda Principles' required daily release of all sequence data to public databases before any IP could be asserted. They also dramatically accelerated the public project timeline. Celera raced ahead on speed but found that any sequence it finished was immediately pre-empted by the public release. The joint publication in 2001 de facto commoditised the reference genome; Celera's monopoly strategy collapsed. The key governance intervention was not regulation but a credible commitment by a well-resourced public actor to open-publish faster than the private actor could establish IP.
  - _Similar:_ A decisive capability advantage (unique genomic database with commercial and scientific monopoly) was deliberately dissolved by a governance decision to pre-empt private lock-in; The public actor raced the private actor not to monopolise but to commoditise — 'open-publish to prevent lock-in' as an explicit strategy; Worked: the human genome reference sequence became a true public commons; no single actor controls access to it; The Bermuda Principles functioned as a credible commitment device — once daily release was institutionalised, Celera could not claw back IP on previously released data
  - _Different:_ The human genome is a static natural artifact; publishing it does not give competitors the capability to use it (you still need sequencing equipment and bioinformatics infrastructure). AI models are the capability itself, not a precursor to it — open-sourcing weights transfers the capability directly; The adversary was a commercial firm, not a geopolitical peer with military applications; no national security second-order effects from open publication; The public actor (NIH + Wellcome) had comparable technical capability to the private actor — a necessary condition. If the public actor is technically inferior, open-publication accelerates the leading private actor rather than commoditising; OpenAI's original mission was precisely the HGP analogy (open-publish to prevent private monopoly) and it failed: the organisation pivoted to closure once frontier costs became clear, showing the model does not transfer straightforwardly to AI
  - _Implies:_ The HGP case shows that the 'race to commoditise' governance strategy can work, but requires: (1) a credible public actor with near-frontier capability, (2) the technology being an information artifact that is fully transferred by publication, and (3) no catastrophic downside from public release. AI fails on conditions 2 and 3: open-sourcing frontier weights transfers full military/dual-use capability to all adversaries simultaneously, and the 'public actor' capable of racing to open-publish does not exist. The HGP analogy thus clarifies why the AI governance fork looks more like the Baruch Plan than the Bermuda Principles.  _(#A58, r10)_
- **Nassau Agreement — US shares Polaris/Trident SLBM technology exclusively with UK; France refuses and builds independent deterrent (1962–present)** — When the US cancelled the Skybolt air-launched missile in 1962, the UK's planned nuclear deterrent collapsed. At Nassau, Kennedy offered Macmillan Polaris submarine-launched ballistic missiles under the condition that UK nuclear forces would be 'assigned' to NATO (under US operational architecture) except in cases of supreme national interest. The UK accepted; the arrangement has endured 65 years, extended to Trident in 1980. UK nuclear submarines use US-manufactured missiles from a shared US pool; rely on US nuclear test infrastructure for warhead design confidence; and depend on US for targeting data and satellite navigation. De Gaulle rejected an identical offer for France, read it correctly as strategic dependence, and built the fully independent Force de Frappe. Two NATO allies, same technology offer, divergent choices: one accepted deep strategic dependence, one paid the cost of full independence.
  - _Similar:_ US held decisive capability (SLBM technology) and used selective sharing to create durable strategic dependence in a close ally — a durable non-commercial DSA lock-in that has not commoditised to third parties; The technology has never proliferated: no non-nuclear state, and no adversary, gained SLBM capability from the Nassau arrangement; Created a two-tier arrangement explicitly: allies inside the arrangement (UK) and allies outside (France) with distinct political and capability consequences; The dependence is deep and hard to exit: UK cannot independently test, certify, or replace its deterrent without US cooperation — structural lock-in lasting decades
  - _Different:_ Military alliance technology sharing has political and legal architecture (NATO Article 5, bilateral defense treaties) that has no equivalent in AI compute governance; France's exit was possible because nuclear weapons technology, once mastered domestically, is permanent — an AI capability ecosystem requires continuous investment and supply chain access that may make 'going independent' perpetually expensive, not a one-time cost; The UK-US relationship had WWII-era intelligence and operational trust that made dependence politically acceptable domestically; no equivalent trust exists between US and most potential AI partner states; Nuclear deterrent is a purely military capability; AI is economically productive — dependence on US AI infrastructure has civilian economic costs that make the political sustainability of deep strategic dependence harder to sustain
  - _Implies:_ The Nassau model — selective DSA sharing that creates deliberate structural dependence in allies, while denying it to adversaries — is a plausible template for US AI strategy with Japan, South Korea, the Netherlands, and the UK. The UK/France divergence shows that this bifurcates the world into dependent allies and independent competitors: the EU's AI Act and Mistral's existence suggest Europe is choosing the Gaullist path, not the UK path. The implication for Taiwan/TSMC: a Taiwan under US 'protection' whose semiconductor capability is deeply integrated into US supply chains is the Nassau model applied to chips — durable, hard to exit, and stable until a crisis forces the question of whether the protector will actually act.  _(#A59, r10)_
- **Outer Space Treaty (1967) — narrow, verifiable DSA prohibition agreed before the capability was deployed, with built-in carve-outs for non-WMD military use** — Negotiated and signed at the height of the space race, the OST banned nuclear weapons and WMD in orbit and on celestial bodies, prohibited military bases on the Moon, and declared space a 'province of all mankind.' Crucially, it explicitly permitted military reconnaissance satellites — which both superpowers needed and neither wanted to ban. The treaty has held for 58 years on its core WMD prohibition: no state has deployed nuclear weapons in orbit. It has, however, completely failed to prevent the militarisation of space in every other dimension: reconnaissance satellites, GPS/GLONASS, ASAT weapons, and commercial dual-use infrastructure now crowd orbit. The treaty succeeded by being deliberately narrow — it banned the one thing both sides agreed they did not want the other to have in orbit (surprise nuclear strike capability) while carving out everything both sides wanted for themselves.
  - _Similar:_ Governance agreed before the capability was deployed — neither superpower had nuclear weapons in orbit; the ban was prospective, not retrospective; Both parties had symmetric incentives to ban the specific prohibited category (neither wanted the other's nukes overhead) — this made verification less adversarial; Succeeded because it was narrow and verifiable: orbital objects are detectable by radar; a nuke-armed satellite is detectable differently from a reconnaissance satellite; The carve-out for military reconnaissance (intelligence satellites) was essential: states retained the capability they needed for strategic stability while banning the destabilising extreme
  - _Different:_ Nuclear weapons in orbit are uniquely distinguishable from civilian objects; AI capability is inherently dual-use and does not separate cleanly into 'banned military AI' and 'permitted civilian AI'; The OST was negotiated between two dominant powers with rough capability parity; an AI governance fork would be negotiated between a leader (US) and a determined challenger (China) with asymmetric capabilities and incentives; The OST left ASAT development completely unaddressed — the very category of capability that now threatens strategic stability in space. Any AI governance agreement that carves out too much (e.g., 'only autonomous lethal weapons are banned') may similarly entrench the destabilising capabilities while banning only the headline case; No 'space commons' governance institution was created — the OST is a prohibition treaty, not an IAEA-type inspectorate. It works because the prohibited object is physically detectable, not because of institutional monitoring
  - _Implies:_ The OST is the template for a narrow, prospective, verifiable AI prohibition: agree before deployment on the specific application neither side wants the adversary to have (e.g., AI in nuclear command-and-control, AI-generated deepfake strategic deception, autonomous lethal strike without human authorisation) while explicitly permitting ISR, logistics, and civilian AI. The OST lesson is that narrow agreements with built-in carve-outs for what both sides actually want are more durable than comprehensive frameworks. The failure mode is the ASAT analogy: whatever is not explicitly banned will be raced, and will eventually threaten the prohibition's underlying purpose.  _(#A60, r10)_
