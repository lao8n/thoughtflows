# Notes — Is the post-1945 free-trade order permanently broken? Managed trade, bloc formation, and the fate of multilateral rules

## Theories / lenses (27)

- **Hegemonic Stability Theory: the order requires a willing liberal hegemon** — Kindleberger/Gilpin: open trading orders are public goods that only a hegemon will supply unilaterally. Post-1945 worked because the US bore asymmetric costs (open markets, dollar liquidity, security umbrella). US relative decline and domestic political reversal remove the engine — no plausible replacement exists (EU lacks strategic coherence, China is a revisionist non-liberal power).  _(#1, r1)_
- **Domestic political economy (Stolper-Samuelson): distributional losers finally organized** — Factor-price equalization predicts that labor in capital-abundant rich countries loses relative income from trade liberalization. Decades of wage stagnation and manufacturing hollowing-out in US/EU rust belts created a mass constituency for protection that cross-class coalitions (Trump, Sanders, Le Pen, UK Leave) finally mobilized. The breakdown is endogenous to liberalization's own distributional logic.  _(#2, r1)_
- **Power transition theory: trade conflict is epiphenomenal to US-China parity approach** — Organski/Copeland: when a rising power approaches incumbent parity, conflict over the rules is structurally overdetermined regardless of the incumbent's ideology. On this view, the trade war is not about tariffs per se but about who writes the next order's rules. Resolution comes only when the transition completes (Chinese dominance, US retrenchment, or power equilibrium) — the interim is necessarily disruptive.  _(#3, r1)_
- **Regime complexity theory: WTO-centric order fragments into overlapping partial regimes** — Alter & Raustiala show that when a focal multilateral institution stalls, rules don't disappear — they migrate into a dense web of bilateral FTAs, plurilateral clubs (CPTPP, RCEP), and sector-specific agreements. The post-1945 order may 'break' at the WTO level while surviving in disaggregated form. This is degradation, not destruction, and retains substantial rule-following.  _(#4, r1)_
- **Varieties of Capitalism: structural incompatibility, not just bad rules** — Hall & Soskice's VoC framework predicts that liberal market economies and state-directed coordinated economies generate systematically different externalities (subsidies, IP regimes, SOE competition) that the GATT/WTO rulebook—designed for LME-to-LME trade—cannot arbitrate. The China shock is thus not a failure of enforcement but a category error in institutional design.  _(#13, r2)_
- **Rodrik's globalization trilemma constrains the solution space** — Deep economic integration, national sovereignty, and democratic legitimacy cannot coexist simultaneously. The post-1945 order resolved this via the 'thin' GATT bargain (sovereignty preserved, legitimacy bracketed). WTO-era deep integration violated the bargain, making the current crisis an equilibrating return rather than anomalous breakdown.  _(#14, r2)_
- **Embedded liberalism (Ruggie): the compensation bargain collapsed, not the trade doctrine** — Ruggie argued the post-war order was premised on an implicit deal—trade openness in exchange for domestic insulation via welfare states. The order erodes when the compensation side fails (deindustrialised workers receive no adjustment assistance), not when trade doctrine is challenged. This is distinct from Stolper-Samuelson: it is an institutional-political failure, not a factor-endowment prediction.  _(#15, r2)_
- **Strategic trade theory legitimises managed trade on welfare grounds** — Brander-Spencer and Krugman show that under oligopoly and increasing returns, strategic industrial policy (subsidies, infant-industry protection) can capture rents and raise national welfare. This gives the current US/EU managed-trade turn an intellectual foundation beyond populism—the comparative-advantage consensus was always contested in economics, and its hold has weakened since the 1980s.  _(#16, r2)_
- **Defense-industrial-base logic, not just coercive interdependence, drives fragmentation** — The weaponization-of-interdependence note covers economic coercion; a distinct mechanism is the security externality argument: sustaining military-grade production capacity (semiconductors, shipbuilding, rare earths, pharmaceuticals) requires domestic supply that comparative-advantage specialization systematically erodes. This is a welfare-improving rationale for managed trade entirely independent of coercion, and it has mainstream endorsement from economists who still oppose tariffs for other reasons.  _(#25, r3)_
- **Tech-standards divergence creates de facto fragmentation without explicit trade restrictions** — Beyond explicit export controls (already noted), incompatible regulatory architectures — the EU AI Act vs. US light-touch approach vs. China's data-sovereignty rules — generate de facto market-access barriers in digital, AI, and platform services. Unlike tariffs or quotas, these are not governed by any existing WTO chapter and cannot be adjudicated under GATS. This opens a second front of fragmentation that post-WTO minilateral deals have not solved.  _(#27, r3)_
- **Currency-manipulation asymmetry was always the WTO's structural blind spot** — The GATT/WTO architecture governs goods barriers and some subsidies but has never had enforceable disciplines over exchange-rate manipulation. Persistent undervaluation (China 2003–2014, Japan's Abenomics era) generated trade effects larger than the tariff concessions being negotiated away, delegitimising the order from within long before the 2025 tariff regime. This is a distinct mechanism from dollar weaponisation, which concerns the US using the dollar offensively; this is about others gaming the system through the monetary back-door.  _(#28, r3)_
- **Connector-economy studied neutrality is a deliberate national industrial strategy, not a passive windfall** — Vietnam, Mexico, UAE, and Turkey pursue calculated ambiguity — maintaining preferential market access to both the US/EU bloc and China — as sovereign rent-extraction strategies. This is distinct from 'non-alignment': governments actively court both sides and extract FDI premiums, export-processing-zone incentives, and logistics fees precisely by refusing to choose. The connector equilibrium is therefore endogenous to bloc rivalry, not merely a side effect.  _(#36, r4)_
- **Public-choice / Stigler capture: managed trade is self-reinforcing because incumbents write the rent rules** — Industrial policy under managed trade (IRA domestic-content thresholds, CHIPS Act grantee concentration, EU CBAM design) systematically favours large, politically connected incumbents who helped draft the rules. New entrants face the full barrier regime; incumbents receive subsidies that offset compliance costs. This creates a political coalition that lobbies to sustain and extend managed-trade restrictions beyond any efficiency or security rationale — a self-reinforcing dynamic distinct from the strategic-trade-theory justification already noted.  _(#39, r4)_
- **Brussels Effect as post-hegemonic ordering mechanism challenges HST's necessity condition** — The EU's regulatory reach (GDPR, CBAM, AI Act, deforestation regulation) demonstrates that large single markets can export binding rules globally without military or currency hegemony — firms comply to access the market. In a reconsolidated order, a US–EU regulatory coalition, rather than a returning US hegemon, could provide the rule-writing function, making HST's requirement for a single liberal hegemon empirically contingent rather than structurally necessary.  _(#46, r5)_
- **Quantified welfare-loss materialization is the political-economy pressure valve for a new bargain** — IMF and World Bank models estimate full fragmentation imposes 2–7% of global GDP in permanent welfare losses. When these costs become concrete and politically attributable — through a stagflationary shock, capital-flight episode, or investment collapse — they generate the constituency for a new settlement. The 1930s depression was the welfare loss event that made Bretton Woods politically feasible; a comparable shock in 2026–2030 could play the same role.  _(#50, r5)_
- **Stable managed bipolarity may be welfare-superior to current fragmentation-uncertainty, making it a viable new order rather than a degraded interim** — Existing notes treat managed trade as welfare-reducing relative to free trade. But the relevant counterfactual is not the pre-2018 order (which cannot be restored) but the current high-uncertainty fragmentation. A stable, explicitly agreed bipolar system with codified inter-bloc rules reduces the ambiguity premium currently suppressing investment and cross-border activity — potentially delivering better outcomes than uncertain fragmentation even if worse than first-best liberalization.  _(#52, r5)_
- **Functionalist spillover from AI governance coordination could rebuild multilateral trust infrastructure** — AI safety coordination among major powers requires shared technical standards, verification mechanisms, and diplomatic trust-building that structurally parallels nuclear non-proliferation architecture. The nuclear NPT created negotiating channels and precedents that later enabled trade détente (Nixon's China opening, SALT → broader engagement). If US–China AI safety talks reach a durable framework, the institutional trust infrastructure could spill over into broader trade rule negotiation — neofunctional integration through a non-trade domain.  _(#56, r5)_
- **Defense industrial prime oligopoly is a distinct firm-level lobby that entrenches managed trade beyond any geopolitical justification** — A small number of US and allied defense primes (Lockheed Martin, RTX, Northrop Grumman, BAE Systems) capture a concentrated share of defense-industrial-base subsidy and procurement reorientation. Unlike diffuse manufacturing workers, these firms have specific, well-organized lobbying power, revolving-door access, and financial incentives to perpetuate the national-security framing that legitimizes managed trade. This is the Stigler-capture mechanism applied at the firm level to a specific sector, but the mechanism is more potent than general Stigler capture because national security is harder to refute than industrial policy rationales.  _(#58, r6)_
- **Data localization operates through regulatory compliance mechanisms structurally outside WTO dispute settlement — a qualitatively different trade-restriction architecture** — Unlike tariffs or even technical standards, data-localization mandates (China's PIPL, India's DPDP Act, EU's GDPR data-transfer rules, Russian sovereign internet law) operate as domestic administrative compliance requirements with no tariff-line equivalent. WTO panels can adjudicate scheduled commitments; they have no effective mechanism to review general domestic data-privacy or national-security-justified localization rules. This means the primary tool of digital-trade restriction is structurally WTO-immune, not just temporarily ungoverned — a distinct failure mode from the Appellate Body crisis.  _(#60, r6)_
- **Network-effect lock-in makes digital/platform bifurcation structurally more irreversible than goods supply chain fragmentation** — Goods supply chains can be rewired in five to ten years given sufficient capital; platform and protocol ecosystems exhibit strong network effects that make bifurcation self-reinforcing over decades. WeChat vs. WhatsApp, Baidu vs. Google, TikTok vs. Instagram represent not just separate products but separate data graphs, recommendation systems, and developer ecosystems. The splinternet, once consolidated, is not susceptible to a 'new Bretton Woods' type negotiated reunification because there is no tariff rate to cut — the network effects ARE the barrier. This means digital fragmentation may be the durable end-state even if goods-trade reconsolidation occurs.  _(#63, r6)_
- **Private equity and infrastructure funds constitute a new managed-trade rent-capture class with lobbying incentives to entrench geographic fragmentation** — Nearshoring and friendshoring require enormous infrastructure investment — ports, logistics hubs, data centers, energy infrastructure — in Mexico, India, Vietnam, Eastern Europe. This creates a multi-hundred-billion-dollar opportunity set for private equity and infrastructure funds (Blackstone, Apollo, Macquarie, KKR). Unlike manufacturing firms, these funds benefit from the managed-trade geography itself (not any specific protection), so they have lobbying incentives to entrench whatever geographic configuration exists, regardless of its efficiency. They represent a new and largely unanalyzed political economy actor in the managed-trade ecosystem.  _(#66, r6)_
- **Processing concentration creates a structural dependency floor beneath which bloc separation cannot reach** — China controls roughly 80% of rare-earth processing, ~70% of cobalt refining, and ~60%+ of battery-precursor (lithium hydroxide/cathode active material) manufacturing. Even complete mining diversification — sourcing ore from Australia, Chile, DRC, Canada — leaves Western supply chains dependent on Chinese processing for the foreseeable decade. This 'processing moat' is a structural floor that is categorically different from supply-chain stickiness in manufactured goods, because it cannot be bypassed by rerouting through a connector economy.  _(#70, r7)_
- **Geology as hard constraint fundamentally limits the solution space unavailable to goods trade** — The top lithium reserves are in Chile, Argentina, Australia; ~70% of cobalt in the DRC; rare-earth deposits concentrated in China, Australia, and a handful of others. Unlike semiconductor fabs or auto plants, ore bodies cannot be reshored, subsidized into existence, or duplicated by policy. This imposes a hard geological ceiling on friend-shoring applicability that has no analogue in manufactured-goods trade, making managed trade in this domain structurally different from and potentially more durable than industrial managed trade.  _(#73, r7)_
- **State-owned enterprise dominance in critical minerals makes WTO subsidy disciplines structurally inapplicable** — WTO countervailing duty law targets public financial contributions distorting private competition. In critical minerals, the dominant actors are state-owned resource enterprises — Codelco (Chile), COMIBOL (Bolivia), Gécamines (DRC), plus China's state-directed mining and processing conglomerates — where the state ownership of the resource base itself constitutes the 'subsidy.' WTO subsidy disciplines have no effective remedy for a state that simply owns its mineral deposits, making the entire framework structurally inapplicable to this sector in a way distinct from the existing AB-crisis analysis.  _(#78, r7)_
- **Investment screening regimes constitute a new managed-trade architecture structurally outside WTO jurisdiction** — CFIUS and its G7 analogues (UK NSI Act, German AWG §55, EU FDI Screening Regulation 2019/452) restrict capital flows rather than goods flows, placing them entirely outside GATT/WTO remedies. Unlike tariffs — which distort trade but are at least WTO-challengeable — investment screening blocks produce managed-trade outcomes that are legally unreachable by any multilateral dispute-settlement mechanism, making them a qualitatively distinct and durable fragmentation layer.  _(#81, r8)_
- **Security-scope expansion in investment screening has no limiting principle — a ratchet toward investment mercantilism** — CFIUS jurisdiction has expanded from defense/technology in its 1988 Exon-Florio origins to cover personal data aggregation, biotech, food supply chains, and residential real estate near military bases under FIRRMA (2018) and subsequent rules. Each scope expansion is politically frictionless (opposition = 'soft on national security') and creates precedent for the next. Unlike WTO tariff concessions — which are negotiated, bound, and subject to retaliatory discipline — there is no multilateral mechanism to bound the expansion of domestic investment-security definitions, making this a one-way ratchet.  _(#87, r8)_
- **Anticipatory investment chilling creates managed-trade effects invisible to WTO monitoring** — CFIUS and allied screening processes are not merely ex-post blocking mechanisms — the uncertainty of ex-ante review deters investment before any formal restriction is applied. Firms self-censor cross-border M&A, JV structures, and greenfield FDI in sensitive sectors without any formal prohibition triggering. This produces a shadow managed-trade effect that appears in no WTO notification, no tariff schedule, and no trade statistics, making it structurally harder to quantify, negotiate away, or legally challenge than formal trade barriers.  _(#89, r8)_

## Hypotheses (10)

- **The order is cycling, not collapsing — historical precedent of recovery exists** — Smoot-Hawley (1930) collapsed trade 66% yet GATT emerged 17 years later. The 1971 Nixon shock ended Bretton Woods convertibility but trade liberalization accelerated in the Tokyo/Uruguay rounds. 1980s VERs and managed trade in steel/autos coexisted with overall liberalization momentum. The current shock may be severe but the 1930s precedent argues recovery is possible if great-power politics allow.  _(#5, r1)_
- **Minilateralism as new equilibrium: club goods among like-minded economies** — Rather than fragmenting into anarchy, the order may consolidate into a small number of exclusive trade clubs (G7+, Indo-Pacific Economic Framework, EU single market extensions) that set high-standard rules among members and erect common external barriers. This is a specific, structured endpoint beyond regime complexity—one in which rules survive but universality does not.  _(#17, r2)_
- **Climate governance may constitute a rival multilateral ordering principle, not merely a fracture line** — The CBAM note treats the EU carbon border measure as a north-south trade fracture. A stronger, more material hypothesis is that climate finance, carbon pricing, and clean-energy standards are coalescing into a parallel multilateral regime with its own disciplines, compliance machinery, and negotiating currency. If so, the 'post-1945 free-trade order' could be superseded rather than merely broken — replaced by a climate-trade hybrid architecture where market access is conditioned on emissions standards rather than tariff bindings.  _(#29, r3)_
- **Connector-economy rents are transitional, not structural: a smile-curve squeeze threatens them as blocs build domestic capability** — Connector economies capture assembly-stage rents during the transition period when blocs are rebuilding domestic capacity. As IRA-backed US battery factories, EU solar manufacturing, and CHIPS-Act fabs come online, demand for connector assembly shrinks. Simultaneously, Chinese producers move up the value chain. Connector economies face a temporal squeeze — their rents depend on a window of bloc-building incompleteness that will close. This makes the connector equilibrium a transitional phase rather than a stable new order.  _(#43, r4)_
- **Personalistic bilateral US–China framework deal could produce de facto managed bipolarity with explicit rules** — The multilateral institutional path to reconsolidation (WTO reform, new Bretton Woods) faces severe collective-action obstacles. A personalistic US–China bilateral — analogous to Nixon–Mao 1972 or Reagan–Gorbachev Reykjavik — could establish a de facto managed bipolarity with agreed inter-bloc rules on tariff floors, technology transfer, and investment screening, bypassing institutional sclerosis entirely. The risk is that it is non-binding and personality-dependent.  _(#49, r5)_
- **G20 mandate expansion is the most institutionally plausible 'new Bretton Woods' pathway** — The G20 already coordinates macroeconomic policy across blocs and includes the US, China, EU, India, and key connector economies. A formal mandate expansion to trade subsidy transparency, investment screening standards, and technology transfer rules would constitute a new plurilateral settlement without requiring new institutions or US WTO leadership. The G20 Finance Ministers process for debt restructuring (Zambia, Ghana) is a precedent for mandate creep under crisis pressure.  _(#51, r5)_
- **SDR reform or multi-CBDC settlement layer is the specific monetary foundation a new Bretton Woods requires** — The original Bretton Woods paired a trade institution (GATT) with a monetary institution (IMF/dollar). Dollar weaponization has eroded the monetary foundation without a replacement. IMF SDR expansion, the BIS mBridge multi-CBDC platform, or a neutral trade-invoicing currency could provide a monetary layer that is neither dollar-dominated nor yuan-dominated, removing the reserve-currency constraint on reconsolidation. This is the concrete institutional design question that the 'does dollar status survive?' crux raises but does not answer.  _(#55, r5)_
- **Global South downstream processing mandates are a distinct value-capture strategy, not connector-economy rents** — Indonesia, DRC, Zimbabwe, Namibia, and Chile are implementing ore-export bans to force domestic smelting and precursor manufacturing — a 'resource curse inversion' that targets capture of the full value chain from extraction through battery-grade chemical. Unlike connector-economy rents (which derive from trade routing with minimal value addition), these mandates require massive domestic industrial investment and represent a structural development strategy. Their durability depends on whether processing capacity can actually be built domestically, not on arbitrage margins.  _(#76, r7)_
- **A critical-minerals producer club (COPEC analog) is structurally unviable without China, unlike an oil cartel** — OPEC+ is the only durable resource-exporter cartel. A critical-minerals analog would require participation from Chile, DRC, Indonesia, Australia, and China — but China is simultaneously the dominant miner, dominant processor, and dominant buyer for several minerals. Any cartel excluding China lacks processing leverage; any cartel including China becomes a Chinese geopolitical instrument. The dual role of dominant producer-processor-buyer destroys the symmetric producer-interest logic that sustains OPEC+, making a minerals security club along those lines structurally non-viable as a multilateral ordering mechanism.  _(#77, r7)_
- **UNCITRAL Working Group III's Multilateral Investment Court is the only plausible institutional rebuild path for investment protection** — UNCITRAL WGIII has been negotiating a standing Multilateral Investment Court (MIC) since 2017 to replace ad-hoc ISDS arbitration. It is at advanced text stages but the US has not engaged, and China's participation is uncertain. The MIC's fate is to investment governance what the MPIA is to goods dispute settlement — a plurilateral workaround that either scales into a new norm or confirms institutional fragmentation. Whether the MIC reaches critical mass (EU + major investment exporters) is the leading indicator for whether investment protection rebuilds or continues to dissolve.  _(#90, r8)_

## Supporting evidence (18)

- **Weaponization of interdependence reveals economic ties as coercive instruments, incentivizing decoupling beyond efficiency calculus** — Farrell & Newman (2019) codified what Russia/SWIFT sanctions (2022), Huawei/TSMC chip bans, and rare-earth export controls made viscerally real: dollar-clearing, semiconductor supply chains, and critical minerals have become 'panopticon' and 'chokepoint' tools. Governments now rationally invest in redundancy and autarky as insurance even at large efficiency cost. This security logic was absent in the original liberal order's design and is not easily reversed.  _(#6, r1)_
- **US industrial policy (IRA, CHIPS Act, 2025 tariff regime) constitutes the order's architect abandoning comparative advantage doctrine** — Unlike 1980s protectionism (which was defensive/bilateral), the IRA and CHIPS Act represent the US government affirmatively deploying $500B+ in subsidies to onshore strategic sectors, explicitly rejecting WTO-consistent behavior (domestic content rules). When the hegemon that designed the non-discrimination rules openly violates them without apology, the normative foundation — not just the compliance — collapses.  _(#7, r1)_
- **Technology export controls introduce a qualitatively new trade restriction the GATT architecture cannot govern** — US semiconductor export controls (Entity List, FDPR, Oct 2022/Oct 2023 rules), allied chip equipment restrictions, and emerging AI governance regimes create dual-use trade barriers with national-security justifications. GATT Article XXI national-security exceptions were designed for arms embargoes, not pervasive technology-stack bifurcation—leaving no neutral adjudicator and no agreed rules.  _(#18, r2)_
- **Dollar weaponisation post-2022 erodes the financial plumbing underpinning trade order** — The freezing of ~$300bn in Russian sovereign reserves in 2022 demonstrated that dollar-denominated assets held by adversarial states are confiscable. This incentivises reserve diversification and bilateral currency arrangements (CNY invoicing, BRICS payment systems), potentially fragmenting the settlement layer that makes multilateral trade settlement tractable—independent of tariff policy.  _(#19, r2)_
- **EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism opens a new, legitimate north-south trade fracture** — The CBAM imposes carbon-cost equalisation on imports from countries without equivalent carbon pricing, creating a WTO-incompatible trade barrier backed by climate-policy legitimacy. Unlike US-China rivalry, this fracture runs along development lines (EU vs. emerging exporters) and enjoys domestic democratic support in the EU—making it resistant to WTO challenge and replicable by other jurisdictions.  _(#22, r2)_
- **Mexico's 2023 emergence as the US's top goods trade partner is concrete evidence of rerouting over reshoring** — Mexico overtook China and Canada in 2023 US goods import rankings. Analysis of Mexican export composition shows a sharp rise in intermediate-goods re-processing of Chinese-origin inputs under USMCA rules-of-origin thresholds. This is direct empirical evidence that 'nearshoring' has primarily meant inserting a Mexican value-adding step into Chinese production chains, not replicating those chains domestically.  _(#38, r4)_
- **WTO e-commerce moratorium near-lapse (2024) is the clearest institutional fracture point for digital trade order** — The moratorium on customs duties on electronic transmissions, continuously renewed since 1998, nearly expired at MC13 (Abu Dhabi, February 2024) due to opposition from India, South Africa, and Indonesia, who want to tax digital flows for development-financing and infant-industry reasons. It was extended only narrowly and for a limited period. Its permanent expiration would constitute the digital equivalent of a GATT tariff ratchet reversal — allowing WTO members to erect tariff barriers on software, streaming, digital services — and would signal the end of a 26-year open-by-default digital trade regime.  _(#61, r6)_
- **Semiconductor export controls are embedded in hardware classification and EAR licensing, making them technically durable and WTO-unchallengeable** — Unlike tariffs, US export controls on advanced AI chips (BIS Entity List, October 2022 and subsequent tightening) are implemented via Export Administration Regulations licensing requirements tied to chip specifications (FLOPS thresholds, interconnect bandwidth). They cannot be adjudicated at the WTO because national-security exceptions (GATT Article XXI) are effectively self-judging post the 2019 Russia-Ukraine panel. They are technically durable because NVIDIA and other firms must design hardware product lines around them. This creates a hardware-embedded two-tier AI development regime that is structurally more irreversible than any tariff-based managed trade.  _(#62, r6)_
- **mBridge CBDC settlement network is an operational (not hypothetical) parallel financial infrastructure for non-SWIFT trade settlement** — The BIS Innovation Hub's mBridge project — involving China's PBOC, UAE, Thailand, Saudi Arabia, and Hong Kong — completed a pilot of real-value transactions in 2024 and is in minimum viable product phase. Unlike SDR reform (a multilateral reform hypothesis), mBridge is a functioning, China-anchored multilateral CBDC bridge that could allow commodity exporters to settle in digital yuan or local CBDCs outside SWIFT. Its geographic alignment (Middle East, Southeast Asia, China) tracks precisely with the connector-economy and Global South non-alignment evidence. If it scales, it provides the actual financial plumbing for a parallel non-dollar trade order — making dollar weaponization durably less coercive.  _(#65, r6)_
- **Geopolitical risk premium in trade finance and credit insurance is a WTO-invisible structural barrier to cross-bloc trade** — Letters of credit, export credit insurance, and trade credit pricing now embed explicit geopolitical-risk premia for cross-bloc transactions. Euler Hermes, Atradius, and Coface have repriced or withdrawn cover for certain China-linked supply chains; US Exim Bank explicitly ties concessional financing to friend-shoring geography. Unlike tariffs, these risk premia arise from private market pricing of political risk, are not subject to WTO disciplines, and are self-reinforcing: higher perceived risk → higher cost → less trade → thinner market → higher perceived risk. This is a non-tariff fragmentation mechanism that persists even if formal trade barriers are reduced.  _(#67, r6)_
- **China's mineral export controls mirror US semiconductor controls but from supplier side** — China's sequential export controls on gallium and germanium (2023), graphite (2024), and antimony operate through commerce-ministry licensing with no WTO-challengeable mechanism, exactly mirroring US EAR controls but exercised by the dominant processor rather than the dominant buyer. This establishes a two-directional export-control regime that forecloses the GATT architecture from both ends of the supply chain simultaneously.  _(#69, r7)_
- **Indonesia's nickel WTO ruling defiance marks a behavioral norm shift for resource nationalism** — Indonesia maintained its nickel ore export ban after a 2022 WTO panel ruling that it was illegal, subsequently extended analogous bans to bauxite and copper concentrate, and has faced no effective sanction. This is distinct from the WTO Appellate Body crisis (which is about dispute-resolution architecture) — it is evidence that a key mineral-producing state has concluded that WTO-illegality is an acceptable cost of downstream industrial policy, signaling a new revealed preference across the producing-nation peer group.  _(#71, r7)_
- **Post-2022 energy infrastructure build-out creates multi-decade durable bloc-adjacent flows** — European construction of 12+ new LNG terminals (2022–2025), US LNG export-capacity expansion to ~200 mtpa by 2030, and the permanent closure or reversal of Russian pipeline routes represent 20–30-year infrastructure lock-in. Unlike rerouted goods trade (container flows can shift in months), energy infrastructure lock-in produces durable bloc-adjacent trade patterns that persist regardless of political reconciliation. This is categorically distinct from the existing 'rerouting vs. reshoring' debate, which concerns goods supply chains.  _(#74, r7)_
- **EU CBAM and Global South processing mandates create directly contradictory regulatory incentives with no multilateral resolution** — EU CBAM penalizes carbon-intensive imports — Indonesian nickel smelting (coal-powered), DRC cobalt processing (grid-dependent on Inga hydro deficits), Zimbabwean lithium refining. Simultaneously, these countries' export-ban processing mandates require they build that very carbon-intensive processing capacity now. Western clean-energy standards thus act as a non-tariff barrier against the downstream industrialization that producing nations' resource-nationalist policies are trying to create. No existing multilateral forum — WTO, UNFCCC, G20 — has jurisdiction over this intersection, leaving it as an open, structurally unresolvable north-south fracture.  _(#80, r7)_
- **2023 US outbound investment controls weaponize capital outflows — a postwar first** — The August 2023 Executive Order on Outbound Investment (implemented via Treasury NSPM-10 rules, finalised late 2024) prohibits or requires notification of US-person investments in Chinese AI, semiconductor, and quantum computing firms. This is categorically different from tariffs, export controls, or inbound screening: it restricts what US investors may do with their own capital. No GATT/WTO provision governs outbound FDI controls, and no postwar precedent exists for systematic capital-outflow weaponization by the system's anchor economy.  _(#82, r8)_
- **Energy Charter Treaty mass exit is a completed institutional death, not just crisis** — Germany, France, Netherlands, Spain, Poland, Slovenia, Luxembourg, and Denmark formally withdrew from the ECT between 2023 and 2024 (with Italy having exited in 2016), citing ISDS liability for clean-energy regulatory measures. Unlike the WTO Appellate Body, which remains formally alive, the ECT has experienced actual treaty termination by its core membership — providing a concrete template for how postwar investment-protection institutions die in practice, not merely in theory.  _(#83, r8)_
- **US capital-markets restrictions on Chinese firms extend managed-trade logic into portfolio investment** — The Holding Foreign Companies Accountable Act (HFCAA), FIRRMA provisions, and executive orders excluding PLA-affiliated companies from US index inclusion (e.g., the 2021 OFAC MMC list) impose compliance conditions or outright restrictions on Chinese-company access to US equity and debt markets. This extends the managed-trade fragmentation sequence from goods → services → FDI → portfolio capital — a layer with no WTO remedy and with its own distinct financial-fragmentation pathway separate from dollar weaponisation via SWIFT/sanctions.  _(#85, r8)_
- **ISDS asymmetry (Northern MNCs suing Southern governments) makes BIT-regime collapse more politically durable than AB crisis** — The political economy driving BIT withdrawal differs fundamentally from the AB crisis: Global South countries exit BITs because ISDS has been used predominantly by Northern multinational investors to challenge Southern regulatory measures (Philip Morris v. Uruguay; Vattenfall v. Germany; Lone Pine v. Canada). This is a North–South distributional grievance, not a US–China strategic-competition grievance. It means BIT-regime erosion has a broad, durable political coalition spanning the Global South, the European left, and climate-policy advocates — a more heterogeneous and stable constituency for institutional dismantlement than the bilateral US–China conflict driving the AB crisis.  _(#92, r8)_

## Disconfirming evidence (17)

- **Supply chain stickiness and MNC lobbying create strong floor against full decoupling** — Despite 'friend-shoring' rhetoric, MNC sunk costs in China-integrated supply chains run into trillions. Apple's China dependency, Volkswagen's China revenue (30%+), and TSMC's customer concentration all mean corporate lobbying actively resists wholesale decoupling. The empirical record 2018-2025 shows 'China+1' partial diversification, not wholesale exit. Trade volumes in goods (ex-tariff-affected categories) remain near historic highs.  _(#8, r1)_
- **CPTPP, RCEP, and MPIA demonstrate continued appetite for rules-based trade outside US leadership** — After US withdrawal from TPP, the remaining 11 members ratified CPTPP (2018) with WTO-plus commitments. RCEP (2022) covers 30% of world GDP. The Multi-Party Interim Appeal Arbitration Arrangement (MPIA) — now 53 WTO members — creates a functioning AB substitute. These demonstrate that US abdication created a leadership vacuum that others are partially filling, not a collapse of multilateral willingness.  _(#9, r1)_
- **Global South non-alignment is empirical evidence against clean bloc formation** — India, Brazil, Vietnam, Indonesia, Saudi Arabia, and most of Africa are visibly refusing to choose sides—trading actively with both the US and China, joining neither CPTPP nor RCEP exclusively, and blocking G7 attempts to impose China-containment language at multilateral forums. If bloc formation were the trajectory, revealed preference would show hedging decreasing over time; it is increasing.  _(#20, r2)_
- **Services trade continues to liberalise while goods trade fragments** — Cross-border services trade (finance, insurance, professional services, digital platforms, tourism) has grown faster than goods trade since 2010 and faces far lower formal barriers. WTO's GATS framework and bilateral investment treaties continue to deepen. The crisis is concentrated in manufacturing goods with national-security adjacency—treating that as representative of the whole order risks a base-rate error.  _(#21, r2)_
- **Agglomeration economics makes supply-chain concentration self-reinforcing regardless of policy** — Krugman's new economic geography shows that once manufacturing ecosystems (suppliers, skilled labour pools, infrastructure) concentrate in a location, increasing returns create lock-in that tariffs and subsidies alone cannot overcome cost-effectively. This means 'decoupling' is far more expensive than political actors anticipate—a structural floor against full fragmentation that is distinct from MNC lobbying (it operates even without political pressure).  _(#23, r2)_
- **Friend-shoring announcements vastly outrun actual investment repatriation** — Empirical tracking of capex flows (2018–2025) shows that most manufacturing FDI continued to follow cost and agglomeration logic rather than security rhetoric: announced reshoring projects in semiconductors and EVs routinely face multi-year delays, cost overruns, or quiet cancellation. The gap between political signal and economic behaviour is disconfirming evidence that the order is fracturing in rhetoric more than in actual production geography.  _(#26, r3)_
- **Single-undertaking loss undermines the minilateralism-as-equilibrium hypothesis** — The GATT/WTO 'single undertaking' allowed cross-issue package deals: agriculture concessions bought IP protection, services access offset manufacturing exposure. Minilateral club-goods deals among like-minded economies lack this width, making comprehensive liberalisation harder, not easier. Empirically, every major plurilateral since the WTO's founding (e.g. TISA, Environmental Goods Agreement) has stalled precisely because narrower membership reduces the tradeable issue-space. Minilateralism may produce shallower, not deeper, rules.  _(#31, r3)_
- **Fiscal unsustainability is a temporal ceiling on the managed-trade era** — US industrial policy (IRA, CHIPS Act, tariff-induced revenue) presupposes sustained fiscal capacity to subsidise reshoring and absorb tariff-pass-through inflation. With US debt-to-GDP above 120% and structural deficits projected to widen, future Congresses face pressure to roll back subsidy commitments — as already seen in IRA implementation uncertainty. If managed trade requires permanent, large-scale fiscal transfers to be viable, fiscal consolidation becomes a self-limiting mechanism that makes the current regime a transitional episode rather than a durable new order.  _(#32, r3)_
- **WTO Joint Statement Initiatives and the fisheries subsidies agreement show institutional adaptation, not death** — While the Appellate Body crisis is correctly flagged, the WTO has simultaneously advanced: the first new multilateral agreement in 25 years (fisheries subsidies, 2022), and plurilateral Joint Statement Initiatives on e-commerce, investment facilitation, and services domestic regulation with 80–90 member participation. These are adaptive responses within the institution that the 'fragmentation' narrative systematically underweights.  _(#33, r3)_
- **Observed trade-route shift is largely rerouting, not true supply-chain decoupling** — Bilateral US-China goods trade has fallen while US imports from Vietnam, Mexico, and other connectors have surged with suspiciously high Chinese-input content. This implies 'fragmentation' has been a statistical artefact of partner-country relabelling rather than a physical reorganisation of production networks — the opposite of what the managed-trade narrative requires.  _(#35, r4)_
- **Geographic and institutional bottlenecks severely limit connector-economy capacity, capping how much fragmentation they can absorb** — Only a small number of countries — Vietnam, Mexico, Morocco, Poland, India, possibly Bangladesh — combine adequate infrastructure, labour depth, property rights, and geographic proximity to either bloc to absorb meaningful China+1 FDI. The bottleneck is not capital but institutional absorptive capacity. This means connector rents are highly concentrated among a few 'winner' economies and that the rerouting phenomenon cannot diffuse into a genuinely distributed production world, limiting the scale of the connector-arbitrage equilibrium.  _(#41, r4)_
- **Connector-economy gains are distributed narrowly within those countries, undermining their political durability** — Stolper-Samuelson dynamics operate inside connector economies too: export-processing-zone FDI accrues heavily to capital owners and a narrow export-sector workforce while broader domestic labour and agriculture remain disconnected from the rents. Vietnam, Mexico, and Morocco all show rising within-country inequality alongside export booms. This creates domestic political pressure against the neutrality strategy — echoing the original embedded-liberalism collapse in rich economies — and introduces a political-sustainability crux for the connector equilibrium itself.  _(#44, r4)_
- **1930s interwar collapse was deeper than today's, yet produced Bretton Woods** — The pre-GATT fragmentation was categorically more severe — Smoot-Hawley, bilateral clearing agreements, competitive devaluations, imperial preference blocs — than the current partial managed-trade regime. The post-1945 reconsolidation therefore constitutes a existence proof that a more fragmented equilibrium can snap into a durable rules-based order within a decade. The current fragmentation does not foreclose an analogous discontinuity.  _(#45, r5)_
- **US–Japan 1980s managed trade ended in convergence, not permanent fragmentation** — The Voluntary Export Restraints, semiconductor agreements, and Structural Impediments Initiative of 1986–1995 constituted a full managed-trade episode between the world's two largest economies. They resolved into Japan's deeper integration, not a permanent bloc split. This is the most precise precedent for the current US–China dynamic and directly challenges the permanence hypothesis.  _(#47, r5)_
- **IRA/CHIPS subsidies flow disproportionately to capital, not workers — undermining managed trade's political coalition** — Early empirical evidence shows IRA manufacturing credits and CHIPS Act grants accruing primarily to large-cap equity holders and executive compensation rather than broad factory employment: CHIPS fab projects have faced repeated delays while subsidy recipients have simultaneously run stock buyback programs; IRA EV credits have been captured largely by higher-income consumers and OEM margins rather than UAW members. If the distributional outcome of managed trade resembles financialized neoliberalism with higher tariff walls, the working-class coalition sustaining it dissolves before reconsolidation becomes politically available.  _(#57, r6)_
- **US hyperscaler financial dependence on cross-border data flows is a structural lobby against digital fragmentation** — Google, Meta, Apple, Microsoft, and Amazon derive a significant share of revenue and virtually all marginal profit from cross-border data flows — targeted advertising, cloud services, platform fees. Their combined lobbying capacity dwarfs most industrial sectors and is directly aligned against data localization, internet balkanization, and e-commerce moratorium expiration. This is a high-powered, well-organized counterforce to digital fragmentation, analogous to MNC supply-chain lobbying against goods-trade decoupling but potentially more effective because these firms have greater margins, more concentrated political access, and clearer stakes.  _(#64, r6)_
- **ISDS caseload growth despite BIT withdrawal shows procedural use and political legitimacy are diverging** — UNCTAD tracks over 1,300 known investor-state arbitration cases filed cumulatively, with annual filings remaining elevated even as countries formally exit BITs. States are simultaneously withdrawing from the political regime while litigating heavily under legacy treaties with survival clauses (typically 10–20 years). This divergence between normative delegitimization and functional persistence means 'death of ISDS' is likely premature as an institutional indicator — the regime may limp on through legacy treaty exposure for decades, complicating any clean periodization.  _(#86, r8)_

## Cruxes (20)

- **Crux: Is China a system-reformer or system-wrecker?** — If China seeks to reshape (but participate in) a rules-based trade order — accepting WTO discipline, engaging plurilateral deals — the system can evolve into a Sino-US co-governed order. If China is building a genuinely parallel architecture (BRI financial standards, SCO/BRICS settlement systems, RMB alternatives to SWIFT), bifurcation is structural. Current evidence is genuinely ambiguous: China uses WTO dispute settlement selectively while also building alternatives.  _(#10, r1)_
- **Crux: Is the rupture demand-side (publics against trade) or supply-side (institutions can't deliver)?** — If the core problem is that democratic publics in key countries have structurally turned against open trade (demand-side), no institutional reform can restore the order — it requires decades of political realignment. If the problem is institutional failure (WTO dispute settlement paralysis, MFN free-rider problems), targeted reforms (plurilateral critical-mass agreements, AB reform) could restore functionality without mass public conversion. These require entirely different remedies.  _(#11, r1)_
- **Crux: Is the WTO Appellate Body crisis reversible — a leading institutional indicator** — The AB has been non-functional since Dec 2019 due to US blockage of appointments. If the MPIA scales to cover major economies (currently excludes US, China on some cases) and/or AB reform negotiations succeed, it signals institutional resilience. If the MPIA remains a rump arrangement and AB stays paralyzed through successive US administrations regardless of party, it signals the enforcement backbone of the rules-based order is durably broken — even if the WTO's trade-facilitation functions survive.  _(#12, r1)_
- **Crux: Does dollar reserve-currency status survive the weaponisation era?** — The post-1945 trade order's settlement layer rests on dollar dominance—without it, bilateral/barter-like trade expands at the expense of multilateral flows. If the 2022 Russia sanctions triggered durable reserve diversification (tracked by IMF COFER data showing dollar share decline accelerating), the financial plumbing that makes multilateral trade tractable degrades independently of tariff negotiations.  _(#24, r2)_
- **India's alignment choice is the swing variable for whether a coherent Western-led bloc can form** — India has the second-largest population, is the fastest-growing large economy, rejected RCEP, remains ambivalent about CPTPP, maintains high import tariffs, and pursues strategic autonomy explicitly. Unlike smaller Global South economies, India has enough scale to prevent any US-led bloc from achieving critical mass in manufacturing if it declines to join — and enough leverage to negotiate terms that dilute bloc coherence. Whether India tilts toward the US-EU-Japan network or maintains genuine non-alignment is a more pointed swing variable than aggregate 'Global South' behaviour.  _(#30, r3)_
- **Crux: Does managed trade require a coherent state-capacity counterpart that most democracies lack?** — Strategic trade theory legitimises managed trade on welfare grounds, but executing industrial policy well requires fine-grained state capacity to pick winners, avoid capture, and exit losers — capacity that developmental states (Korea, Taiwan, China) demonstrated but that the US, UK, and most EU members have atrophied over 40 years of liberalisation. If the state-capacity gap is binding, 'managed trade' in Western democracies degenerates into rent-distribution to incumbent industries rather than the welfare-improving strategic equilibrium the theory predicts. This would make the managed-trade era self-defeating.  _(#34, r3)_
- **Rules-of-origin enforcement capacity is the decisive swing variable for whether connector arbitrage persists** — The transshipment/connector model survives only if customs authorities in the importing blocs lack the political will or administrative capacity to enforce strict origin rules. US Section 301 anti-circumvention cases (Vietnamese solar, Cambodian textiles) and EU anti-dumping on Chinese EVs via Turkey show enforcement is escalating. Whether enforcement reaches a threshold that closes the arbitrage window is the single institutional variable that determines if fragmentation is cosmetic or structural.  _(#37, r4)_
- **Taiwan/TSMC acute contingency is the one scenario that could produce irreversible, rapid bloc formation** — Approximately 90% of sub-5nm logic chip production is concentrated in a militarily contested island. A kinetic Taiwan scenario would sever leading-edge semiconductor supply on a timescale (days) that no policy substitute — Intel fabs, Samsung, TSMC Arizona — could address for at least 5–10 years. This is qualitatively different from the existing export-controls note: it is a supply shock, not a policy trajectory, and would force hard bloc formation as a survival response rather than a strategic preference.  _(#40, r4)_
- **China's leverage over connector assembly via intermediate-goods supply is an underappreciated chokepoint** — Vietnamese electronics, Mexican auto-parts, and UAE re-exports all depend heavily on Chinese intermediate inputs (components, chemicals, machinery). China could weaponise this dependency — restricting component exports to countries it judges to be facilitating US-bloc decoupling — threatening the connector equilibrium from the upstream side. Whether China exercises this leverage, and when, is a crux that determines whether the connector model is sustainable or a Chinese-tolerated arbitrage that can be closed.  _(#42, r4)_
- **China's export-to-consumption growth transition is the structural crux for reconsolidation** — If China successfully pivots to domestic-demand-led 'dual circulation' growth, the mercantilist export pressure that generates bilateral surpluses and triggers countermeasures diminishes, reducing the Varieties of Capitalism structural incompatibility. The crux is not merely whether Xi is a reformer or wrecker but whether China's political economy can sustain this structural transition against entrenched SOE and provincial interests.  _(#48, r5)_
- **What new domestic compensation mechanism makes reconsolidation politically durable in democracies?** — Embedded liberalism's compensation bargain (trade liberalization + social insurance) failed. A new reconsolidated order requires a different domestic coalition. Candidate mechanisms include: sovereign wealth redistribution of trade rents, labor-mobility agreements within trade blocs, technology-access provisions for developing countries. The specific design determines political durability — reconsolidation without a viable new compensation structure replicates the embedded liberalism failure.  _(#53, r5)_
- **'Peak China' demographic and productivity deceleration moderates power-transition conflict dynamics** — Power transition theory predicts maximum conflict at parity approach. But China faces structural headwinds — demographic decline (shrinking working-age population from ~2014), property sector debt overhang, and productivity convergence — that may slow its trajectory, lengthening or truncating the parity-approach window. A slower-rising or plateauing China faces different incentives: the opportunity cost of protracted conflict rises, making negotiated settlement more attractive to Beijing than in a high-growth trajectory.  _(#54, r5)_
- **Whether CHIPS/IRA-style subsidies create structural domestic oligopoly in protected sectors is the crux for reconsolidation feasibility** — Subsidized semiconductor fabs, battery gigafactories, and shipbuilders are not merely rent-seekers; they become domestic oligopolists whose pricing power and market share are structurally dependent on continued exclusion of lower-cost competitors. Once this industrial structure is locked in (a 5–10 year horizon for fabs), reconsolidation requires dismantling domestic monopoly rents — a political economy problem orders of magnitude harder than reducing tariff rates. This is the firm-level structural analog to the 'fiscal unsustainability' temporal ceiling: it is a political feasibility ceiling, not a fiscal one.  _(#59, r6)_
- **Whether the WTO e-commerce moratorium is permanently extended or expires is the decisive digital-trade order crux** — The moratorium is the last multilateral line holding digital trade to an open-by-default regime. Its expiration would permit WTO members to schedule customs duties on electronic transmissions, enabling developing countries to use digital tariffs as development-finance and infant-industry tools. This would create the first formal multilateral legitimization of digital trade restriction, triggering a cascade of scheduled digital tariffs analogous to the GATT era's tariff bindings — but in the reverse direction. Unlike the Appellate Body crisis (which concerns dispute settlement), moratorium expiration would affirmatively expand permissible digital trade restrictions.  _(#68, r6)_
- **Mining diversification ≠ processing diversification is the decisive crux for critical-mineral friend-shoring** — Western governments are accumulating mining rights (IRA domestic-content rules, US–Australia–Canada Critical Minerals Agreement, EU CRMA), but processing capacity — lithium hydroxide conversion, REE separation, cobalt sulphate refining — requires 10–15-year build timescales, enormous capex, and generates toxic waste streams that face domestic permitting resistance. The US currently has near-zero lithium hydroxide processing capacity. Whether friend-shoring can bridge the mine-to-cell gap, not just the mine-to-ore gap, is the operative crux for energy-transition and defence-industrial resilience.  _(#72, r7)_
- **Green transition demand for critical minerals creates a structural decarbonization/deglobalization tension** — IEA projects 4–6× higher critical mineral demand by 2040 for EV batteries, wind turbines, and grid storage. If friend-shoring concentrates mining and processing in high-cost Western jurisdictions, the marginal cost of decarbonization rises sharply and the implied cross-subsidy from climate policy to industrial policy becomes politically contested. Governments simultaneously pursuing green industrial policy and friend-shoring will face a moment where the two agendas are arithmetically incompatible without accepting either higher energy costs or Chinese supply-chain dependence.  _(#75, r7)_
- **Whether GATT Article XXI 'essential security' exception expands to cover critical minerals becomes a legitimation crux** — Recent WTO panel decisions (Russia–Traffic Transit, 2019; Saudi Arabia–IPR, 2020) have tested but not clearly bounded the Article XXI security exception. If panels accept 'critical mineral security' as a self-judging security interest — as the US argues for semiconductor controls — it creates a broad, expandable carve-out that industrial lobbies can capture for non-security managed trade, analogous to the abuse of agricultural 'food security' exceptions. Whether this exception is judicially contained or expands to legitimize a permanent managed-trade regime in strategic inputs is a decisive institutional crux.  _(#79, r7)_
- **Managed trade and ISDS/BIT investment protection are structurally incompatible — you can't have both** — IRA domestic-content requirements, CHIPS Act conditions, and allied industrial-policy subsidies all create ISDS liability: foreign investors can — and do — sue governments for measures that discriminate against their investments. The political move toward managed trade therefore logically requires unwinding BIT/ISDS regimes, as the EU's ECT exit demonstrates. This means the BIT collapse is not collateral damage to the trade-order crisis but a necessary precondition for the managed-trade model. Whether democracies can execute both transitions simultaneously without destroying the investment certainty friend-shoring depends on is an under-examined crux.  _(#84, r8)_
- **EU–China CAI failure is the decisive negative crux for investment-regime reconsolidation** — The EU–China Comprehensive Agreement on Investment was initialed in December 2020 after 7 years of negotiation, then frozen by the European Parliament in May 2021 following reciprocal sanctions over Xinjiang. It represented the best-case scenario for embedding investment rules at the most critical bilateral nexus — pursued during the most trade-cooperative geopolitical moment available. If this failed, the conditions for a successor framework are substantially harder. The CAI's collapse is a stronger negative indicator for investment reconsolidation than any theoretical argument.  _(#88, r8)_
- **Friend-shoring cannot be operationalized without investment-protection frameworks that are being simultaneously dismantled** — Friend-shoring requires private capital to relocate supply chains to ally countries — but that capital needs legal protection (BIT, FTA investment chapters, ISDS) to accept the risk of investing in emerging-market ally jurisdictions. The BIT network is eroding at exactly the moment the friend-shoring strategy demands it most. The US has not ratified a new BIT since the 1990s. Unless governments substitute public-finance mechanisms (DFC, PGII guarantees) for private investment protection, friend-shoring's capital mobilization logic has a structural gap that existing notes on friend-shoring (rerouting vs. reshoring) do not capture.  _(#91, r8)_

## Historical analogies (60)

- **First globalization collapse → interwar fragmentation, 1870s–1939** — The first era of globalization — driven by the gold standard, falling transport costs, and British hegemony — produced trade/GDP ratios not matched again until the 1990s. After WWI the system frayed; the 1929 crash triggered Smoot-Hawley (1930) and retaliatory tariffs worldwide, collapsing trade volumes ~65%. The 1930s saw competitive devaluations, bilateral clearing agreements, and formal blocs (Sterling Area, Reichsmark Bilateral Clearing Zone, Yen Bloc). The system did not self-repair; it took WWII and deliberate US-led reconstruction (Bretton Woods, GATT 1947) to build a successor order.
  - _Similar:_ A hegemonic anchor (UK then, US now) in relative decline and losing willingness to absorb systemic costs; A major rising challenger pursuing mercantilist industrial policy (Germany/US then, China now); A sequence of beggar-thy-neighbour moves that each seem rational to the individual actor; Rules-based institutions (gold standard rules, League of Nations) failing to bind defectors; Strategic sectors (steel, chemicals, agriculture) carved out of liberal rules first, then the carve-outs expand
  - _Different:_ Today's supply chains are far more intra-firm and cross-border than 1930s trade, raising the exit cost from blocs enormously; Nuclear deterrence makes a 'hot war' reset much less likely — the interwar analog ended in WWII, which is what cleared the way for a new order; The WTO's dispute-settlement architecture and trade-remedy law give grievances a legalistic outlet the 1930s lacked; No gold-standard constraint forces monetary austerity today, so the deflationary spiral that radicalized electorates in the 1930s is less automatic; China is already deeply embedded in the global system (WTO member, reserve currency aspirations) unlike 1930s Germany/Japan which were partially outside
  - _Implies:_ The 1930s analog is the strongest bear case: cascading retaliation can exceed the rational interest of any individual actor, blocs form not by design but by exclusion, and once trade collapses it takes a generation and a geopolitical reset to rebuild. However, the much higher structural cost of unwinding integrated supply chains provides a meaningful circuit-breaker that did not exist in the 1930s — making a full fragmentation slower and less complete than the interwar case, though not impossible.  _(#A1, r1)_
- **Bretton Woods monetary order collapse, 1971–1976** — The post-1945 fixed-exchange-rate system (gold at $35/oz, other currencies pegged to the dollar) was formally ended by Nixon's August 1971 'closing of the gold window.' The IMF's Articles of Agreement — the rulebook for the monetary order — became legally inconsistent with reality. Yet the dollar remained dominant, exchange rates were managed through G7 co-ordination (Plaza Accord, Louvre Accord), and the IMF was repurposed from rulebook-enforcer to crisis-lender. A new informal managed-float non-system replaced the rules-based system within 5 years and has operated ever since.
  - _Similar:_ A rules-based international economic order that the hegemon (US) itself chose to exit when the rules became too costly; Institutional framework (IMF then, WTO now) that survived the collapse of the underlying rules it was built to enforce; Repeated attempts at rule-based re-stabilisation (Smithsonian Agreement 1971, G7 co-ordination) that kept failing and were replaced by ad-hoc management; Dollar hegemony persisting even after the rule it depended on was abandoned — analogous to US alliance networks persisting even as WTO rules erode
  - _Different:_ Monetary order collapse affected exchange rates, not the physical flow of goods — trade volumes kept rising after 1971; a trade-rule collapse is structurally more disruptive; The managed-float system that replaced Bretton Woods was arguably as good or better for growth; there is no obvious 'managed trade' substitute that preserves aggregate welfare; The monetary system had only ~15 significant players; the WTO system has 165 members with far more diverse interests, making re-coordination harder; The US retained overwhelming financial dominance post-1971; in trade, China is near-parity with the US in goods, so no single power can anchor a successor order alone
  - _Implies:_ The Bretton Woods analog is the main bull case for resilience: rules-based orders can formally break while informal co-ordination and institutional shells persist and eventually stabilise into a new managed non-system. The WTO may continue to exist as a forum and crisis-manager even after its Appellate Body and MFN disciplines lose binding force — not death, but transformation into a G20-style talking shop with selective enforcement.  _(#A2, r1)_
- **Pax Britannica free-trade order / Corn Laws repeal, 1846–1914** — Britain unilaterally repealed the Corn Laws in 1846 and moved to near-zero tariffs, betting that free trade served British industrial interests regardless of reciprocity. Other powers (France via Cobden-Chevalier Treaty 1860, then spreading via MFN clauses) partially followed. The resulting liberal order lasted until WWI but was always asymmetric: the US and Germany maintained high tariffs throughout. When British manufacturing competitiveness eroded (~1890s on), Britain could no longer absorb systemic costs and the institutional glue dissolved. The liberal order was hegemonic, not rule-based.
  - _Similar:_ The post-1945 order was similarly anchored by one power (US) willing to run trade deficits and absorb others' export surpluses to buy geopolitical loyalty; The anchor power's domestic political economy eventually turned against the bargain when the costs became visible (UK Joseph Chamberlain tariff-reform movement ~1903; US Rust Belt politics ~2016); The liberal order coexisted with major mercantilist outliers (US/Germany then, China now) throughout its existence — it never required full conversion; Challengers weaponised their industrial policy precisely because they correctly read that free trade served the hegemon's structural interests, not theirs (Friedrich List's critique)
  - _Different:_ Britain's hegemony was financial and naval; US hegemony is also nuclear/security, meaning partners face a much steeper security cost from defection — trade and security are more bundled; The Cobden-Chevalier network operated through bilateral MFN treaties, not a multilateral institution; the WTO's single-undertaking architecture is stickier and harder to exit cleanly; Britain never seriously threatened or used trade as a coercive tool; the US under Trump/Biden has explicitly weaponised market access, tariffs, and export controls — changing the nature of the anchor; The 19th-century order had no mechanism for developing-country catch-up within the rules; the WTO's special & differential treatment provisions at least acknowledge this tension
  - _Implies:_ The Pax Britannica analog suggests the post-1945 order was always more fragile than it appeared — sustained by US willingness to bear asymmetric costs, not by genuine multilateral commitment. Once that willingness erodes (as it has), the institutional shell (WTO) can persist while the underlying bargain dissolves, just as MFN treaties persisted into the 1930s long after the liberal ethos had died. The implication is that the order is not 'broken' by an external shock but hollowed out by the hegemon's own exit.  _(#A3, r1)_
- **US–Japan Semiconductor Trade War and Voluntary Export Restraints, 1986–1991** — After Japan's MITI-directed DRAM industry captured >80% of global market share by the mid-1980s, the US government negotiated the 1986 Semiconductor Trade Arrangement — forcing Japan to maintain floor prices on chips exported to the US (a cartel enforced by the alleged 'dumper'), guarantee US firms 20% share of Japan's domestic market, and submit to export monitoring. GATT rules were openly subordinated to bilateral managed trade. The arrangement was renewed in 1991. Japan's DRAM industry subsequently declined (partly due to the arrangement itself, partly due to Korean competition). The US unilaterally used Section 301 threats to compel compliance.
  - _Similar:_ Strategic technology sector explicitly carved out of free-trade rules on national-security grounds — exactly the logic of today's CHIPS Act, export controls on advanced semiconductors, and Entity List; The dominant power (US) using market-access leverage to force structural changes in a rival's industrial policy rather than relying on WTO adjudication; Government-sponsored collusion (floor prices) that would be illegal under domestic antitrust law accepted as the price of avoiding trade war; The framing of 'predatory state-backed competition' as justifying departure from MFN rules — identical to current US framing of Chinese overcapacity
  - _Different:_ Japan was a US security ally with no nuclear deterrent and no alternative security patron — its compliance was partly coerced; China faces no equivalent structural constraint; The 1986 arrangement covered one product category; current US-China tech decoupling spans semiconductors, AI, biotech, quantum, space — scope is orders of magnitude larger; Japan's semiconductor industry was privately owned; China's strategic industries are state-directed, making 'market share guarantees' as a concession structurally harder to extract; The WTO's DSU (Dispute Settlement Understanding) now exists; Japan had only GATT's weaker consultation mechanism, so legal challenge was less available to Japan — China can and does litigate
  - _Implies:_ The 1986 STA is the clearest historical proof-of-concept that managed trade in strategic sectors can be imposed on a trading partner outside multilateral rules, can persist for years, and can achieve its defensive aim (preserving a US industry) at the cost of global efficiency. It normalises exactly the toolkit being deployed now. The key difference is that Japan accepted; China will not. This suggests the current managed-trade push will produce not a bilateral arrangement but a technological bloc split — two parallel semiconductor ecosystems — which is structurally more disruptive than the 1986 outcome.  _(#A4, r1)_
- **Interwar trading and currency blocs — Sterling Area, Reichsmark bilateral clearing, Yen bloc, 1931–1945** — After the gold standard collapsed (1931 UK exit, 1933 US exit), the world organised into discrete trade and currency blocs. The Sterling Area linked ~50 countries to the pound with capital controls. Germany built a Reichsmark clearing zone across Central/Eastern Europe using bilateral barter-style agreements that exploited German monopsony. Japan carved out the Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere. Intra-bloc trade grew; inter-bloc trade shrank and became strategically hostile. The blocs reflected geopolitical spheres of influence as much as economic complementarity. Post-WWII, the US dissolved these blocs (Article VIII IMF obligations, Marshall Plan conditionality) and rebuilt on MFN foundations.
  - _Similar:_ Bloc formation driven by a major power using market-access leverage over smaller economies that depend on it — exactly the dynamic of US-allied tech supply chains vs. China-dependent commodity/manufacturing chains; Bilateral clearing agreements as a substitute for multilateral settlement — analogous to proposals for yuan-settled trade among BRI countries, BRICS payment systems, and sanctions-driven de-dollarisation; Smaller economies pressured to 'choose a side' and face preferential/discriminatory pricing from each bloc — the current complaint of ASEAN, India, Gulf states; The blocs reinforced each other's exclusivity: once inside, exit costs rose, creating path dependency even when the economics were unfavourable
  - _Different:_ The 1930s blocs formed rapidly because the gold standard's sudden collapse created a monetary vacuum; today's bloc formation is much slower because the dollar system is intact and WTO membership is legally sticky; The 1930s major powers had relatively autarkic industrial structures; today's US, EU, and China all have deep mutual supply-chain dependencies that raise the cost of hard bloc separation; Digital goods, services, and financial flows cannot be easily 'bloc-ified' by tariffs — the fracture lines are in physical goods, semiconductors, and data/platform access, not aggregate trade; Post-WWII US had overwhelming power to dissolve the blocs; no equivalent power exists today to enforce a re-unification if blocs form
  - _Implies:_ The interwar blocs show that once exclusionary logic takes hold and network effects bind smaller economies to a major power's orbit, reconvergence requires external shock (WWII) or overwhelming coercive power. If the current US-China bifurcation in technology and finance crosses a threshold of mutual dependency, a soft partition may become self-reinforcing rather than self-correcting. The critical variable is whether the middle economies (India, ASEAN, Gulf, Brazil) are forced to choose or remain swing states — in the 1930s most were forced to choose; today they have more agency, which is the main reason a hard bloc split may be avoided.  _(#A5, r1)_
- **US protectionist industrialisation under Hamilton–Clay–Lincoln tariff system, 1790–1934** — The United States was the world's most protectionist large economy for most of its industrial rise. Hamilton's 1791 Report on Manufactures explicitly argued infant-industry protection against British competition; Clay's 'American System' built it into policy; the Republican tariff regime from Lincoln through Hoover averaged 40–50% on manufactures. The US did not adopt a liberal trade posture until the Reciprocal Trade Agreements Act (1934) and then the GATT (1947) — after its manufacturing supremacy was unassailable. Friedrich List codified the theory: free trade is the doctrine of the already-powerful.
  - _Similar:_ China's current industrial policy (Made in China 2025, subsidised overcapacity, forced technology transfer) is structurally identical to what the US did — state-directed catch-up protectionism against the incumbent hegemon; The US complaint that China 'cheats' by using industrial policy mirrors Britain's 19th-century complaint that the US 'cheated' by ignoring British patent law and maintaining high tariffs; Both cases involve the challenger eventually becoming so large that the incumbent cannot simply absorb the disruption — Britain never successfully forced US trade liberalisation; the US will likely fail to force China's; The political economy logic is symmetric: domestic industries created under protection build lobbying coalitions that perpetuate protection long after the infant-industry justification expires
  - _Different:_ The US industrialised inside a liberal international order it did not yet anchor; China is industrialising inside a liberal order the US built and still leads — the challenge is more direct and destabilising; 19th-century US protectionism primarily damaged British exporters of manufactures; Chinese overcapacity in solar, EVs, steel, and chips damages producers in every major economy simultaneously, generating broader coalition opposition; The US did not use state-owned enterprises or directed lending at the scale China does — the distortion to global price signals is quantitatively larger with Chinese model; The US–UK contest played out in a world without nuclear weapons, WTO membership, or global supply chains — the escalation ladder and exit options were both different
  - _Implies:_ The Hamilton–List analog reframes the focal question: the post-1945 free-trade order was always a contingent bargain by the already-industrialised, not a universal principle. China is doing what successful industrialisers have always done; the tension is not a deviation from historical norms but the norm reasserting itself. This suggests the order will not 'break' catastrophically but will be renegotiated to accommodate a second industrial giant — just as British free trade eventually accommodated American industrialism, with decades of friction and a world war in between.  _(#A6, r1)_
- **COMECON vs. GATT — two parallel trade orders, 1949–1991** — The Soviet-led Council for Mutual Economic Assistance (COMECON) operated as a fully managed, state-directed trade bloc — administered prices, bilateral clearing, technology denial — running alongside GATT for ~40 years. - **League of Nations Article 16 collective sanctions and the Abyssinia crisis, 1935–1936** — Italy invaded Abyssinia in October 1935. For the first — and last — time, the League Council invoked Article 16 and imposed economic sanctions. But oil (Italy's critical import) was excluded after Anglo-French pressure; the Hoare-Laval Pact was secretly negotiated to partition Abyssinia in Italy's favour; enforcement was patchwork and self-selecting. Sanctions were lifted in July 1936 after Italy completed its conquest. The binding enforcement mechanism was demonstrated to be hollow when a major power was the target and other major powers had countervailing strategic interests. The League never again invoked Article 16. Germany and Japan drew the lesson that Article 16 was a paper tiger. The institution formally dissolved in 1946.
  - _Similar:_ A nominally automatic and binding multilateral enforcement mechanism was effectively nullified in the highest-profile test case, despite formal compliance with procedures; Major powers (UK, France) chose strategic bilateral interests over institutional credibility — analogous to WTO members acquiescing to AB paralysis rather than retaliating against the US; Selective hollowing out of one high-profile case destroyed institutional credibility for all future cases non-linearly — the League's authority collapsed faster than the catalogue of cases would predict; The institution survived in name for years while being functionally over — 'zombie institution' dynamics directly relevant to the AB question
  - _Different:_ Article 16 was about military aggression and security enforcement, not trade adjudication — political economy of compliance was categorically different and more existential; The League's hollowing out occurred through selective non-enforcement (sanctions imposed but deliberately incomplete), whereas the WTO AB paralysis is a structural administrative blockage rather than selective case-by-case non-enforcement; No coalition workaround (no 'MPIA equivalent') emerged — the League simply failed and collapsed; the WTO has so far generated MPIA, suggesting more institutional resilience; The interwar period lacked durable alternative institutions; today's multilateral system has more redundancy (regional blocs, bilateral FTAs, MPIA)
  - _Implies:_ The Abyssinia precedent is the cautionary archetype: once a binding enforcement mechanism is visibly shown to be hollow against a major power, the rational response of other actors is to discount the institution's deterrent value entirely. The WTO AB's incapacitation carries the same risk — even if MPIA functions as a procedural workaround, the perception that the US can violate WTO rules without binding AB discipline may unravel dispute settlement credibility among all members, not just MPIA non-participants.  _(#A59, r10)_
- **NAFTA Chapter 11 ISDS dismantlement in USMCA renegotiation, 2018–2020** — The US had been the original architect and champion of investor-state dispute settlement in NAFTA's Chapter 11, viewing it as a template for binding commercial adjudication globally. By the Obama years, the US had become one of the most-sued respondents under Chapter 11. During the 2018–2020 NAFTA renegotiation, the US (Trump administration) effectively eliminated ISDS between the US and Canada entirely, and severely restricted it for US-Mexico (limited to specific sectors; domestic remedies must be exhausted first; three-member rather than five-member panels). The rationale was framed as sovereignty protection. Canada and Mexico acquiesced, having their own concerns. The outcome was a negotiated dismantlement of a binding adjudication mechanism the hegemon itself had designed.
  - _Similar:_ The hegemon that originally designed and championed a binding adjudication mechanism reversed position when it became a net loser under that same mechanism; Dismantlement was rationalized through 'sovereignty' and 'fairness' arguments that reframed the binding mechanism as a structural imposition rather than a rules-based benefit; The replacement mechanism is explicitly weaker and less automatic — state-to-state consultation rather than binding investor arbitration; The change was accomplished without formally abandoning the multilateral principle — USMCA retains dispute resolution, just in weakened form — analogous to how a reformed WTO AB might preserve the form while gutting the substance
  - _Different:_ USMCA was a formal trilateral renegotiation with the consent of all parties — a legitimate treaty process; the WTO AB blockage is unilateral and coercive, with no consent from other WTO members; Chapter 11 ISDS governs investor-state not state-to-state disputes, making the political economy different (corporate lobbies vs. government-to-government interests); Canada and Mexico had independent reasons to accept ISDS reform; WTO members broadly oppose the AB paralysis, meaning a negotiated 'USMCA-style' reform would face far more resistance in the multilateral context; The USMCA model established a precedent for weakening binding adjudication through renegotiation that could be copied in future WTO reform proposals
  - _Implies:_ USMCA provides the most likely template for how WTO dispute settlement 'reform' might actually happen: a formal renegotiation in which the US secures carve-outs, weakened automaticity, and national-security exceptions in exchange for nominally restoring the appellate function. The historical pattern suggests the resulting mechanism will be structurally weaker and more vulnerable to subsequent erosion, with major power interests baked into the design from the outset.  _(#A60, r10)_
