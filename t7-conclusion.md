# Conclusion — Regulatory, standards, and data fragmentation as AI market protectionism — including the EU's fate, training-data legality, and protocol/interface capture

## Thesis

The AI-protectionism contest is real, well-funded, and mostly aimed at the wrong layer. Every named model-layer lever — training-data copyright, GPAI FLOP thresholds, EU high-compliance rules, interface/protocol enclosure — targets precisely the layers deflating fastest, while durable rent concentrates at the compute/infrastructure chokepoint that is jurisdiction-agnostic and needs no enforcement agency (C4 holds, high confidence). Model-layer moats erode (C1: no), training data stays broadly obtainable via fair use / licensing / substitution with a durable first-mover cost edge (C2: yes), the EU regime survives as text but is under-enforced and actively self-deregulating so its 'global forcing function' is broken (C3: no), and interface standards resist proprietary enclosure because a JSON schema is cloned overnight (C5: no) — yet AI remains a high-rent prize because the rent migrated one layer down to CUDA/GPU/cloud (C6: yes). Net: most regulatory/data/standards levers redistribute value at the margin and AMONG incumbents rather than capturing or breaking the decisive moat; catastrophe more likely deepens fragmentation than forces convergence; and the binding EU constraint is talent/capital economics, not compliance drag. The protectionism most consequential to market structure — export controls and compute — is the piece least discussed in copyright/standards debates.

## Forecasts

- **P = 0.60** — [#4] Copyright enforcement is structurally backward-looking, creating a temporal ratchet protecting already-trained incumbents.
  - _why:_ The backward-looking core is near-definitional and written into law (#43 Art.111 grandfather; A2 Hatch-Waxman, A3 Betamax show first-movers get permanently lower-cost lineages). But C2=yes (data licensable/substitutable) and C1=no cap the magnitude: fair use makes future training legal without a clearinghouse, and synthetic/open-weight substitution (#13,#6,#119) compresses fast-follow, so the 'ratchet grows with delay' framing overstates a real-but-modest edge.
- **P = 0.44** — [#11] MCP-style open protocols are a Trojan horse — open standard, proprietary value capture via compliance certification.
  - _why:_ C5=no says the protocol itself won't be enclosed (MCP shipped open, cross-vendor adopted). But the verdict explicitly preserved the #11 Android-path as a real partial-capture route, and compliance-as-product (#59,#61,#107) plus the A19-A24 'value re-accretes one layer up' pattern support value migrating to a proprietary compliance/certification layer. The specific 'only Anthropic-certified providers clear compliance' framing is speculative, holding this below even odds.
- **P = 0.38** — [#17] Regulatory havens (UAE, Singapore, UK) are actively recruiting labs priced out of EU compliance.
  - _why:_ Active recruitment is real, but the causal driver the hypothesis names is weak: C3=no means EU compliance drag is softer than assumed and self-deregulating (#95), while #15/#27 show geo-fencing converts EU market-exit into a config flag — so labs need not relocate to escape EU costs. Haven pull is driven more by compute/energy/tax/SWF (#34,#110) than by EU-compliance arbitrage.
- **P = 0.40** — [#37] US deregulatory EOs constitute an affirmative anti-regulation export — a deregulatory Brussels Effect.
  - _why:_ EO 14179 and multilateral posture are real, but the classic Brussels Effect required market-access forcing that non-compliance forfeits; a deregulatory 'do less' posture lacks an equivalent hard forcing mechanism, so soft alignment pressure is plausible but far weaker than the inverted-Brussels label implies. Consistent with C3's broken-forcing-function logic cutting both ways.
- **P = 0.20** — [#38] A catastrophic AI harm triggers emergency regulatory convergence that destroys compliance-expertise moats.
  - _why:_ Directly disfavored by #46: unlike nuclear/aviation/chemical there is NO pre-existing AI institution with both technical competence and enforcement authority, so post-catastrophe coordination itself becomes a fragmentation contest. Even nuclear convergence took decades. The Cambridge Analytica precedent (#39) is the stronger base rate.
- **P = 0.63** — [#39] Jurisdictions respond divergently to a catastrophic AI incident, deepening fragmentation.
  - _why:_ The historically grounded scenario: #46 institutional vacuum plus the Cambridge Analytica precedent (EU tightening, US 'maintain leadership', China control expansion) — regulators weaponize the same event toward pre-existing agendas. Reinforced by the three/four-pole structure (#16,#31,#108) and C3's finding that EU/US already diverge structurally.
- **P = 0.50** — [#51] EU competitiveness is bound primarily by talent flows, not compliance drag; visa instruments have structurally failed.
  - _why:_ C3=no supports the claim that compliance drag is real but not the binding constraint, and the equity/tax/language evidence (#58,#5) plus visa-instrument underperformance are well-grounded. But crux #56 (talent vs capital) is unresolved and A25 Airbus shows capital/champion consolidation also matters, so the strong single-factor 'primarily talent, not compliance' claim is only coin-flip defensible.
- **P = 0.44** — [#74] Aggressive merger scrutiny perversely concentrates AI by destroying the acqui-hire exit.
  - _why:_ The perverse-feedback mechanism is sound and echoes pharma/semiconductor, supported by #69/#75 (acqui-hire arbitrage) and asymmetric error costs (#78). But the antecedent is weak: #79 shows antitrust timelines (3-7yr) are structurally too slow relative to 12-18mo model cycles, so scrutiny rarely becomes binding enough to actually foreclose the exit.
- **P = 0.60** — [#80] Multi-jurisdictional antitrust divergence lets incumbents play regulators against each other.
  - _why:_ Robustly supported: CMA/FTC/DG Comp already reach different conclusions on the same deals, #76 documents structural-vs-behavioral divergence driving forum choice, and the litigate-all-three-simultaneously asymmetry favors well-resourced incumbents. The absence of coordinated international merger review is a documented incumbency advantage.
- **P = 0.64** — [#86] Aviation certification (DO-178C/ARP4754A) functionally excludes modern ML, protecting avionics incumbents.
  - _why:_ Well-grounded and narrow: deterministic/explainable certification requirements genuinely make non-deterministic ML inadmissible for flight-critical functions today, and #85 shows these regimes predate and are untouched by the AI Act. Discounted modestly because EASA/FAA AI-assurance roadmaps are developing clearance pathways, eroding durability over a longer horizon.
- **P = 0.40** — [#98] Simultaneous US-EU deregulation collapses regulatory-haven arbitrage entirely.
  - _why:_ The antecedent is partly live (C3=no EU rollback #95; #37 US deregulation), and the logic that haven value requires a high-compliance counterparty is sound. But 'entirely' overstates: havens retain non-regulatory pull (compute, energy #34, tax, SWF capital #110), so the differential narrows rather than collapses.
- **P = 0.26** — [#102] European-values AI as a premium export to the Global South is an affirmative EU value-capture thesis.
  - _why:_ Weakly evidenced. A15 (cultural exception) shows values-framing can survive trade pressure but explicitly did NOT close the capability gap. C3=no undercuts the premise — if the EU doesn't maintain enforced high standards, the premium attributes aren't credibly funded — and there is little evidence of actual willingness-to-pay for the attribute over capability.
- **P = 0.50** — [#108] Japan's non-binding, innovation-first regime positions it as a G7-credible counterweight / fifth pole.
  - _why:_ The descriptive claim is fairly true: Japan's 2024 voluntary guidelines, G7 membership, Hiroshima Process, and semiconductor/robotics leverage make it a distinct innovation-first pole with legitimacy Gulf/Singapore havens lack. The causal claim that it 'pulls multilateral norms toward voluntary' is weaker — Japan's actual norm-setting influence is modest against EU/US gravity.
- **P = 0.61** — [#109] The EU open-source GPAI exemption creates a regulatory-arbitrage incentive to open-release weights.
  - _why:_ Concrete and grounded: the Act does exempt open-weight FOSS GPAI below the systemic-risk threshold, creating a genuine structured incentive for Meta/Mistral, reinforced by C1=no (open weights commoditize the model layer) and #9 (efficiency keeps challengers sub-threshold). Discounted because open-release decisions are driven by broader strategy (A19-A24) than EU-internal arbitrage alone.
- **P = 0.30** — [#118] Formal AI interoperability standards could attract SEP/FRAND patent-tax dynamics.
  - _why:_ C5=no but the verdict explicitly preserved this as a non-negligible 5-10yr tail via the CEN-CENELEC harmonized-standards path (A36 Qualcomm, A16 Rambus show the mechanism is durable once IP is essential). Held low because it hasn't fired, post-Alice weakens protocol patents, and there is no withholding mechanism — you cannot refuse to ship a JSON schema competitors clone overnight.
- **P = 0.27** — [#122] Essential-facilities doctrine could mandate access to non-replicable training datasets.
  - _why:_ C2=yes shows most data is broadly obtainable, undercutting 'non-replicable' for the general case. The theory bites only in regulated verticals (HIPAA/clinical #87) that are genuinely non-substitutable — but US essential-facilities doctrine is narrow post-Trinko, and even the EU Magill/IMS line is applied sparingly. A live but low-probability legal theory.
- **P = 0.54** — [#133] GDPR 'legitimate interest' guidance is the key training-data barrier-softening vector.
  - _why:_ A real, active vector — the EDPB has already issued an opinion on Art.6(1)(f) legitimate interest for AI training, and C3=no plus C2=yes both point toward the barrier softening. 'Key' is strong (Art.4 TDM opt-out #113 and non-copyright layers CFAA/§1201 #106/#120 survive any GDPR softening), but directionally the guidance trend genuinely erodes GDPR-as-moat.

## What drives it

**Settled (conviction):**
- C4 (high confidence): the compute/infrastructure chokepoint holds — no near-horizon discontinuity reroutes the stack. Even the plausible 'non-CUDA silicon' disruption (TPU/Trainium/Maia) reroutes value AMONG hyperscaler-owned infra incumbents, not away from the layer (Wintel A31, ASML A32, Standard Oil A35). This is the one firm anchor of the whole framework.
- Directionally settled: value migration is downward/away from the contested layers. C1 (model-layer moats erode), C5 (interface resists enclosure), and C6 (rent survives at compute) jointly converge on the same structural conclusion — the levers this topic catalogs target deflating layers while durable rent accretes to compute, which is immune to the regulatory/adequacy contests foregrounded here.

**Open (bounds confidence — the debate battleground):**
- C2 (medium): whether US fair use survives on appeal — a loss would flip specific segments toward the absolute-moat pole and sharpen #4's ratchet, though substitution still caps the clearing price.
- C3 (medium): the EU splits into text-durability (strong) vs enforced-high-compliance (weak/eroding). Under-enforcement STRENGTHENS the reverse-Brussels/drag theses while dissolving the compliance-moat/export theses — so 'the EU premise' is not one variable but four (rule text, soft-law CoP, enforcement capacity, member-state political economy, #99).
- #56 (medium leverage, unresolved): talent-mobility vs capital-mobility as the binding EU constraint — governs whether #51 is right that immigration/labor reform, not compute or AI Act tweaks, is the primary lever.
- Catastrophe-response direction (#38 vs #39): whether a major harm converges or fragments rules — bounded by the #46 institutional-vacuum finding but genuinely contingent on which incident and which institutions mobilize.
- C1 (medium): whether non-synthesizable continuous/regulated data (RLHF #26, HIPAA #87, national-language corpora #101) resists commoditization enough to preserve segment-specific model-layer moats against the near-unbroken opened-layer base rate.

## Decisive cruxes & verdicts

- **[C1, leverage high]** Does frontier capability remain a function of scale in proprietary data and compute — so that model-layer moats (training-data legality, GPAI FLOP thresholds, amortized compliance cost) actually bite — rather than being commoditized by efficiency gains, synthetic data, and open weights?
  - verdict: **no** (confidence medium) — The crux bundles two inputs (proprietary-data scale AND compute scale) whose fates diverge, and the specific moats it names — training-data legality, GPAI FLOP thresholds, amortized compliance — sit on the eroding data/regulatory side. The outside-view base rate (opened-layer beats proprietary moat: A19–A24) is nearly unbroken, and the only place it was said to break — model collapse (#40) as a hard fidelity ceiling — is a testable hypothesis that has NOT manifested: DeepSeek R1 and the distillation+RL wave (#6, #13) are post-Shumailov existence proofs of capability still rising, not plateauing. That untriggered escape hatch fails to rescue the prior. The FLOP-threshold moat is self-defeating (efficiency lets challengers stay sub-threshold, #9); training-data legality protects already-trained weights whose scarcity value fast-follow keeps compressing (#4, #43); compliance cost is a real fixed-cost moat but largely orthogonal to the capability question and itself eroding (#93/#95, #64). The durable YES asset — compute/export/CUDA chokepoints (#25, #126, #A31, #A32) — is genuinely strong but, per the analogies' own logic, accretes value ONE LAYER UP, away from the model-layer proprietary-data scale the crux specifies; that is a defeat of the crux as stated. Held to medium (not high) because distillation is downstream of mega-scale frontier teachers (being AT the true frontier still costs billions), and non-synthesizable continuous/regulated data — RLHF/usage (#26), HIPAA clinical and national-language corpora (#87, #101) — resist commoditization in ways the base rate doesn't touch.
  - would flip if: Three things would move this to YES. (1) Model collapse manifesting empirically as an actual capability plateau — distilled/synthetic pipelines demonstrably capping well below the leading edge while the incumbent-vs-challenger gap widens rather than compresses, showing fresh proprietary human corpus is the binding constraint. (2) Evidence that fast-follow structurally cannot reach the frontier (DeepSeek-class replication topping out a generation behind and falling further back), proving the frontier itself, not just a capability point, requires irreproducible data+compute scale. (3) Courts/regulators making training-data licensing de facto unavailable (injunctions against training, holdout, collective-licensing failure per #8), which would make the data-legality moat absolute regardless of efficiency gains.
- **[C2, leverage high]** Will training data be licensable at market rates to all comers (competitive market), or is it de facto unavailable via rights-holder holdout, collective-licensing failure, or injunctions against training itself (absolute moat protecting only pre-trained weights)?
  - verdict: **yes** (confidence medium) — The crux's "no" pole is a strong claim: training data becomes "de facto unavailable" leaving "only pre-trained weights" legal. That requires a conjunction of failures — fair use loses, licensing markets fail to clear, non-copyright access layers foreclose everything, AND no viable substitute exists. The evidence knocks out the load-bearing conjuncts.

Outside view: the analogy lineage (Betamax A3, Google Books A13, Oracle v. Google A24, news codes A12, ASCAP A33, SoundExchange A34) is one-directional — mass ingestion of copyrighted works always resolved into a market or a fair-use finding, never a prohibition or injunction against the technology. An injunction against training itself, the purest form of the absolute-moat pole, has essentially zero precedent and the recent US trend (2024–2025 transformative-use rulings) cuts hard against it.

Inside-view updates that move me off the "moat but not absolute" base rate toward the licensable/usable pole: (1) In the dominant frontier jurisdiction (US), fair use increasingly makes training legal WITHOUT any clearinghouse — which defeats the NO case's strongest structural point (ASCAP machinery can't scale) because no clearinghouse is needed. (2) Credible substitutes — synthetic pipelines, open weights (Llama/Mistral/DeepSeek), and copyright-free AI-generated corpora — structurally cap the clearing price and prevent genuine "unavailability"; a holdout is disciplined by outside options. (3) Licensing deals are already transacting at scale (News Corp, Reddit, AP, Shutterstock), empirically demonstrating a functioning market rather than lockout.

What holds confidence at medium, not high: the NO case wins on specific segments. The EU is a genuine contested case — Art. 4 TDM opt-out (#113) is an ex ante property ratchet publishers already exercise, and Google Books (A13) is live proof EU fragmentation can starve a corpus rather than birth a market. Non-copyright layers (CFAA #106, §1201 #120, HIPAA #87) survive any fair-use win and produce near-absolute moats in verticals like clinical data. And even the favorable cases leave early movers permanently lower-cost — so "market rates to all comers" is not a clean description; it's "broadly available with a durable first-mover cost edge." But that is the not-absolute pole, which maps to YES on this binary.

Net: evidence favors the competitive/licensable pole (training data will be broadly obtainable via fair use, licensing, or substitution) over the absolute-moat pole, with the honest exception that the EU and specific regulated-data verticals tilt the other way.
  - would flip if: A court issuing an actual injunction against training (not damages) that survives appeal; or a decisive US fair-use LOSS at the circuit/Supreme Court level combined with documented collective-licensing failure; or hard empirical proof that model collapse (#40) is a fundamental ceiling making pre-2024 human corpora irreplaceable (killing the synthetic-substitute discipline); or broad CFAA/§1201 enforcement foreclosing open-web scraping regardless of copyright. Also flips if the question is scoped to the EU as the decisive market rather than the US-dominated frontier — there the TDM opt-out plus absent registry genuinely tips toward corpus starvation.
- **[C3, leverage high]** Will the EU actually maintain a durable, enforced high-compliance AI regime — or does self-deregulation (Draghi/digital-omnibus rollback) plus chronic under-enforcement dissolve the shared premise that all the EU-facing theses depend on?
  - verdict: **no** (confidence medium) — Start from the outside view: EU rule-persistence base-rate leans durable-as-text (REACH #A27, GDPR, cultural exception #A15, and even GMO #A28 all survived as rules for decades, some despite adverse WTO rulings). So the DURABILITY conjunct of the crux largely holds — co-decided law is hard to repeal, Article 111's grandfather (#43) is written in, and the digital-omnibus/Draghi vector is Commission-led trimming at deadlines/burden-allocation, not deletion of prohibited-use or liability cores. If the crux asked only "will the text survive," I'd lean YES.

But the crux is a conjunction — durable AND ENFORCED AND HIGH-COMPLIANCE — and it fails on its weakest, most AI-specific links. Three independent inside-view updates push below the base rate: (1) The forcing function is broken. REACH/GDPR exported globally ONLY because non-compliance meant losing the EU market entirely and EU consumption share crossed ~25%. Geo-fenced selective compliance (#27, Meta withholding multimodal AI) converts market-exit into a config flag, and EU AI-consumption share plausibly sits below threshold — so the exact condition under which the base rate transfers is absent. This is the decisive break. (2) Enforcement is the historically weak conjunct and the DIRECT institutional predecessor (GDPR: 4–7yr timelines, sub-0.1%-revenue fines, chronically under-resourced DPAs — #96/#28) predicts NMSAs, facing a harder task with less capacity, will produce a high-cost/low-enforcement regime — a tax on good-faith domestic actors, not a binding constraint on foreign incumbents served from Singapore/UAE (#15). (3) The rollback is documented and active as of mid-2026, not speculative (#95), trimming exactly the "high-compliance" the theses need. The purest precedent — the Database Directive (#A8), the EU's one prior attempt to manufacture a data-IP industrial policy — produced no champions, incumbent rents, ECJ narrowing, and zero stimulative effect.

Two hedges keep this from high confidence: text durability is genuinely strong, and "enforced" is slow-but-real (Schrems, the €1.2bn Meta fine) rather than absent. Also the "shared premise" is not monolithic (#99/#100): under-enforcement actually STRENGTHENS the Reverse-Brussels/regulatory-drag theses (#2, #5) while dissolving the compliance-moat and Brussels-export theses. So the STRONG shared premise (enforced high-compliance with a global forcing function) dissolves — favoring NO — while a weaker durable-but-leaky version persists. Net: below the base rate, leaning NO with medium confidence.
  - would flip if: Toward YES: (a) the digital-omnibus stalls and core high-risk/GPAI obligations draw material fines against foreign incumbents within ~2 years; (b) NMSAs get resourced and act materially faster than the Irish DPC did; (c) geo-fencing proves commercially untenable so providers comply globally (Brussels Effect fires); (d) hard data showing EU AI-consumption share clearly above the ~25% Brussels threshold. Toward stronger NO: formal Parliament+Council rollback of prohibited-use/GPAI cores, or CoP soft-law fully hollowing obligations while enforcement actions stay near-zero through 2027.
- **[C4, leverage high]** Is the compute/infrastructure chokepoint (NVIDIA + CUDA middleware + hyperscaler cloud) durable enough to capture AI value regardless of the data/standards/regulatory contest — or does a technological discontinuity (post-transformer, non-CUDA silicon, edge/on-device) reroute the stack?
  - verdict: **no** (confidence high) — The crux asks whether the compute/infrastructure chokepoint is durable enough to capture value regardless of the data/standards/regulatory contest, or whether a discontinuity reroutes the stack. I rule that no discontinuity reroutes the stack on the horizon relevant to current market structure — i.e., the chokepoint holds.

Outside view: the analogy base is unusually one-directional. Wintel (#A31) is the near-exact template — an OPEN architecture hyper-concentrated rent at the two irreplaceable interface layers (ISA + OS ABI), the precise analog of H100 microarchitecture + CUDA. Linux→hyperscalers (#A19), Kubernetes→GKE/EKS/AKS (#A21), Firefox→Chrome (#A23), and RISC-V→fabs (#A22) all show the same regularity: every layer commoditized by openness or attacked by regulators saw rent re-accrete to the infrastructure chokepoint below it. ASML (#A32) and Standard Oil (#A35) confirm that manufacturing/infrastructure chokepoints are the most durable rent and survive even court-ordered breakup. This is not a coincidental cluster; it is a structural pattern with no strong counter-analogy in the graph.

Inside view updates in the SAME direction rather than against it. The decisive move is the YES case's layer-vs-vendor distinction, which the NO case implicitly concedes: the CUDA substitutes that could displace NVIDIA — TPU, Trainium, Maia — are themselves hyperscaler-owned. So the most plausible near-term "discontinuity" (non-CUDA silicon) reroutes value AMONG infrastructure incumbents, not away from the layer. Meanwhile the contest layers actively feed the chokepoint: sovereign-AI narrative → NVIDIA hardware (#135), cloud-exclusivity clauses (#71), FedRAMP duopoly (#61), and antitrust timelines (3–7yr) structurally too slow for a 12–18mo consolidation cycle (#79). CUDA is jurisdiction-agnostic and needs no enforcement agency (#126), so it is immune to the very regulatory/adequacy contests the topic foregrounds.

The break factors are real but bounded. NVIDIA's SPECIFIC vendor monopoly is genuinely more contestable than ASML's — Ascend/TPU/Trainium have real traction, compute turns over on 12–18mo cycles, and CUDA is technically (if not practically) portable. And Wintel WAS eventually rerouted by smartphones/ARM. But that reroute took 20+ years, and vendor contestability ≠ layer reroute. On the horizon that governs who captures value this market cycle, durability dominates. I discount from "very high" to "high" only for the tail risk that on-device/edge inference or a compute-demand collapse dissolves the layer rather than reshuffling its occupants.
  - would flip if: Evidence that a discontinuity moves value OUT of the infrastructure layer entirely, not merely among its incumbents: (1) edge/on-device inference reaching frontier capability at scale, eliminating the cloud-rent toll gate for the bulk of workloads; (2) a non-CUDA, non-hyperscaler-owned silicon path (open hardware on commodity/multi-source fabs, à la a hardware RISC-V that DOES escape the fab chokepoint) achieving frontier training economically; (3) algorithmic/efficiency gains (extreme distillation, post-transformer architectures) collapsing compute demand enough that compute ceases to be scarce and stops commanding rent; or (4) durable frontier training on non-NVIDIA silicon owned by actors OUTSIDE the current cloud oligopoly (e.g., a sovereign or independent-fab champion capturing share NVIDIA+hyperscalers cannot). Also flipping: concrete evidence the 12–18mo turnover lets Ascend/domestic stacks reach parity fast enough that no single infra layer captures supernormal rent within this market cycle.
- **[C5, leverage medium]** Will the dominant AI interface standard (OpenAI API shape, MCP, function-calling/agent schemas) become a durable proprietary tollbooth — potentially with SEP/FRAND patent-tax dynamics — or does organic, developer-adopted openness resist capture the way TCP/IP did?
  - verdict: **no** (confidence medium) — The crux, read literally, asks about the INTERFACE STANDARD layer (OpenAI API shape, MCP, function-calling/agent schemas) — not compute. This distinction is decisive and the YES case quietly relocates its strongest evidence away from it.

Outside view: the analogy base is genuinely mixed. Qualcomm (#A36) and Rambus (#A16) are real proofs that standards-embedded IP produces durable, enforcement-proof rents; SWIFT (#A11) shows neutral protocols becoming chokepoints. But the base's own stated break condition — near-zero forking cost — is exactly the salient feature of AI interface schemas, and the closest same-domain precedents (TCP/IP over OSI #A5, #14, Oracle v. Google #A24, IBM PCM #A9, Carterfone #A17) all resolved toward interoperability winning over interface ownership.

Inside view, and this is what moves me off the base rate: enclosure at the interface layer has already been attempted and is failing in real time. The OpenAI API shape — the single most plausible enclosure candidate — is universally re-emitted as "OpenAI-compatible" endpoints at near-zero marginal cost, and MCP shipped open and was adopted cross-vendor rather than gated. Oracle v. Google removed the legal basis for owning a declaring interface; post-Alice weakens protocol patents. Qualcomm's leverage was "no licence, no chips" — a physical good it could withhold. No withholding mechanism exists when the "chip" is a JSON schema competitors clone overnight. That structural disanalogy guts the SEP transplant.

The YES case's genuinely strong points — CUDA (#126), Wintel (#A31), ASML (#A32) — are about the compute/middleware layer, where forking cost IS high and rent reliably re-accretes. I largely accept those. But conceding them sharpens rather than supports the crux: value migrating to compute is evidence that the interface/protocol layer specifically resists enclosure. The YES side is answering "does AI have a durable chokepoint?" (yes, at compute) while the crux asks "is that chokepoint the interface standard?" (currently no).

Why only medium, not high: the SEP/FRAND-via-CEN-CENELEC path (#118) is a live, non-trivial tail. If the AI Act's harmonized-standards mechanism codifies a mandatory interoperability standard and an incumbent holds genuinely essential IP, the Qualcomm playbook becomes available and courts have shown they won't reliably kill it. That chain hasn't fired and faces the withholding-mechanism problem, but over a 5–10 year horizon it is not negligible. The #11 Android-path (open protocol, proprietary compliance-certified integrations) is also a real partial-capture route that wouldn't show up as "protocol enclosure" yet still tolls enterprises.
  - would flip if: Concrete evidence that the codification trigger has fired: the EU AI Act/DMA mandating a specific interoperability standard through CEN-CENELEC in which an incumbent holds genuinely technically-essential (not interchangeable-convention) IP, combined with a regulator or court upholding FRAND royalties assessed on inference volume. Alternatively, the #11 path materializing — MCP or its successor remaining nominally open while high-value tool/integration access is gated behind proprietary safety/compliance certification only incumbents clear, such that enterprises pay a durable toll despite the "open" spec. Either would show the withholding mechanism reconstituting at a layer software forking can't route around.
- **[C6, leverage high]** Is AI a durable high-rent market worth capturing at all — or does inference commoditize toward marginal cost and enterprise ROI disappoint, so the entire protectionism contest is a fight over a shrinking prize?
  - verdict: **yes** (confidence medium) — The crux as literally posed — "is AI a durable high-rent market worth capturing AT ALL?" — is cleared by the one point both advocates and the framing itself concede: the compute chokepoint (C4) concentrates durable rent even if models deflate. That is decisive, because "worth capturing at all" is a disjunctive bar: rent need only survive durably at SOME capturable layer. The outside-view base rate for GPTs is near-unanimous that it does. Wintel (#A31), Standard Oil (#A35), and ASML (#A32) all show commoditizing one layer does not destroy the prize — it relocates it to the irreplaceable interface (CUDA/GPU/cloud, #126/#35), which if anything is a MORE durable and more monopolized position (no AMD-equivalent cross-license in sight). Export controls (#25), energy/data-center subsidy races (#34), and cloud-exclusivity foreclosure clauses (#71) are states and incumbents spending real capital there — revealed preference that the rent is real. Layered on top, compliance-as-product (#59/#61) and vertical regulated-data capture (#81/#87/#A36) manufacture premium, defensible demand in finance/health/government that commodity model pricing cannot erode.

The NO case is genuinely strong but proves a narrower proposition than the crux states. It correctly shows that the SPECIFIC levers this topic catalogs — regulatory compliance, training-data legality, protocol/standards capture — target precisely the layers deflating fastest (models #6/#13, API-delivered capability #33, open protocols #14), and that history (#A19/#A20/#A21/#A23, and above all the Database Directive #A8 and Minitel #A14) shows the fought-over layer collapses to marginal cost. That is a sharp indictment of the protectionism contest's target selection — the rent migrated one layer down, out of reach of most levers discussed. But "the regulatory/data/standards prize is shrinking" is not the same claim as "AI is a shrinking prize." On the literal crux, the rent's migration to compute is the answer, not a refutation.

I dock confidence to medium, not high, for two reasons the NO case surfaces honestly. First, the "enterprise ROI disappoints" leg is under-evidenced on both sides — if aggregate deployment returns disappoint, demand contracts and even compute rent thins; this is a live empirical unknown. Second, no prior GPT combined near-zero replication cost with open-weight commoditization at this speed, so the deflation could propagate further and faster than any analog, and the compute monopoly is younger and more contested (Huawei Ascend, TPU/Trainium/Maia, DOJ scrutiny) than ASML's.
  - would flip if: Concrete evidence that rent does NOT durably survive anywhere capturable: (1) a credible near-parity NVIDIA/CUDA challenger or forced cross-license plus hyperscaler custom silicon reaching production parity — compressing the compute chokepoint that currently carries the YES case; combined with (2) direct data showing broad enterprise AI ROI disappointing (deployment stalls, churn, spend plateau), signaling demand contraction rather than premium capture in regulated verticals.
